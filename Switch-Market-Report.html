Gas NBP — pence per therm

UK NBP gas has eased over the past week as geopolitical risk premium partially unwound following reduced Middle East tensions. The front-month Jun-26 contract settled at 113.94p/therm on 29 May, down 5.7% week-on-week but still up 6.1% over 30 days. The summer strip (Jul–Aug) trades in a tight 113–114p range, while Winter 2026 holds a modest premium at 116.53p, reflecting storage refill risk and winter demand uncertainty.

TTF influence remains strong, with Dutch TTF front-month trading around €45.94/MWh (equivalent to ~£39/MWh or ~133p/therm). The NBP–TTF spread has narrowed from earlier extremes but still favours NBP. Norwegian flows have recovered from the Troll field maintenance that extended into early May, restoring roughly 15% of UK import capacity. UKCS production continues its long-term decline, forecast at 13.1 bcm for summer 2026 (down 6% year-on-year). LNG send-out to UK terminals has picked up, with arrivals expected at Milford Haven, Isle of Grain and South Hook over the coming days. Four LNG cargoes are due within the week, providing prompt supply relief. Weather forecasts show mild temperatures, suppressing NDM demand, though intra-day volatility in gas-for-power persists as wind output fluctuates.

Power UK Baseload — £/MWh

UK power has decoupled from gas direction over the past week, holding firmer as carbon allowances strengthened independently. The front-month Jun-26 baseload contract settled at £101.04/MWh on 29 May, up 2.2% on the day and 10.8% over 30 days. The winter strip (Q4-26 / Q1-27) trades around £102/MWh, with the summer 2027 contract at £77.76/MWh — a clear backwardation that reflects near-term tightness versus longer-term expectations of supply relief.

Gas-fired generation (CCGT) continues to set the marginal price for significant periods, contributing ~45% of UK generation as of early June. Wind output has been below seasonal norms at ~18% of the mix, down from a 27% seasonal average, forcing greater reliance on thermal plant. Nuclear availability remains constrained at ~9% with ongoing outages. Interconnector imports are providing critical margin, with net imports of +7.44 GW across IFA, IFA2, ElecLink, BritNed, Nemo, North Sea Link and Viking — utilising 88% of total interconnector capacity. Carbon prices have firmed, with EUA Dec-26 around €80/t, adding to the cost of CCGT generation and supporting baseload levels even as gas softened.

Gas system demand is correspondingly lower, though gas-for-power remains volatile as CCGT plants respond to renewable intermittency. National Gas forecasts total summer 2026 demand at 29.8 bcm, essentially flat versus 2025. The key swing factor is gas-for-power, which depends on wind and solar output. The UK generation mix illustrates why CCGT remains the price-maker even in a decarbonising system. At ~45% of output, gas-fired plant is the largest single source and the marginal technology during most pricing periods. When wind drops to 18% (below the 27% seasonal norm), CCGT fill the gap — directly lifting wholesale power prices. Interconnector imports at 88% capacity utilisation are providing critical balancing, but this volume is price-sensitive: if Continental European prices rise, import economics worsen and GB must rely more heavily on domestic CCGT.

For I&C customers, this means power prices still track gas during tight periods, regardless of renewable build-out. The correlation between NBP and UK baseload remains strong, particularly for Winter-26 and near-curve contracts. Customers on flexible or pass-through arrangements should monitor CCGT margins and carbon prices alongside gas. The growth of solar (5–15% daytime in summer) and continued wind additions will gradually erode gas's share, but the marginal unit — and therefore the price-setter — remains gas for the foreseeable future.

European gas storage is the critical variable for winter 2026/27 pricing. At ~38% full in early June, the EU is running roughly 17 percentage points behind the 5-year average. The European Commission has relaxed the mandatory fill target to 80% by 1 November 2026 (from 90%), acknowledging the difficulty of refill after a cold winter and disrupted LNG supply. Even this lower target requires sustained injections of ~2.1 TWh/day through summer — a pace that depends on continued LNG availability and Norwegian flow stability.

For UK I&C customers, EU storage matters because it drives TTF, and TTF drives NBP. If EU storage falls short of 80%, winter risk premium will embed in Q4-26 and Q1-27 contracts. Germany at ~35% and France at ~34% are particularly exposed. Italy at ~59% is better positioned. GB storage is smaller in absolute terms but operationally flexible. The bottom line: strong storage can dampen winter risk; weak storage or slow refill can support forward prices through summer.

LNG is the marginal supply source for GB and Northwest Europe, and therefore the price-setter during tight periods. GB summer LNG imports are forecast at 2.7 bcm, up 65% from 2025, reflecting higher demand and the need to refill storage. The US remains the dominant source, supplying over half of GB LNG, with liquefaction capacity expected to increase as Corpus Christi Stage 3 and Golden Pass Train 1 ramp up.

The global picture remains tight. Qatar's Ras Laffan complex, damaged in earlier conflict, is under force majeure with recovery timelines stretching into 2027. This removes ~20% of global LNG export capacity from the market. Asian competition for spot cargoes is intense — JKM at ~$17/MMBtu — pulling cargoes away from Europe. For UK I&C customers, this means LNG availability directly affects NBP and therefore business gas prices. Any disruption to US export capacity, shipping routes, or Asian demand shifts will feed into UK delivered gas costs.

Forward Curve & Contracting Window

The forward curve for both gas and power shows a backwardated structure from Winter 2026 through Summer 2027, with near-term contracts trading at a premium to further-dated delivery. This is typical of a market pricing in winter risk premium and expecting supply conditions to ease beyond the immediate heating season. However, the shape is relatively flat beyond Cal-27, suggesting limited directional conviction for 2028–2029.

For I&C and Mid Market customers, the forward curve is what matters for contracts with future start dates. A customer signing a 12-month fix today for October 2026 start is buying Winter-26 and Summer-27 — not today's spot. The curve indicates that longer-dated starts (2027, 2028) are priced lower than near-term starts, but this does not automatically mean they will be cheaper when those periods arrive. As time passes, forward delivery becomes near-term delivery, and the price converges to whatever the spot market is at that time.

I&C and Mid Market Procurement Impact

Fixed-Contract Customers

Customers inside 6 to 18 months of contract end should be testing live supplier pricing now. The near-term curve has softened week-on-week, but the 30-day trend is still elevated. Winter 2026 pricing carries a clear risk premium.

Do not wait for the "bottom" — supplier price books can reprice faster than wholesale moves, and quote validity periods may shorten during volatile periods. Compare 12, 24, 36 and 48-month structures where supplier appetite allows.

Site-level recommendation requires live supplier pricing, consumption profile, contract end date and risk position.

Flexible Procurement Customers

Review your hedge position, trigger levels, tolerance bands and volume exposure. The current backwardation (near-term elevated, further-out lower) creates both opportunity and risk for layered hedging.

Customers with open Q4-26 / Q1-27 exposure should assess whether current winter pricing meets budget parameters. Those with summer 2027 start dates may see value in the lower far-curve, but should not over-commit before volume certainty is clear.

Governance and pre-agreed triggers matter more than timing the market. Ensure your flex policy specifies who can hedge, at what volumes, and within what price bands.

Half-Hourly Electricity Users

Shape risk, peak exposure and non-commodity pass-through are your primary concerns. With CCGT at ~45% of the mix and interconnector imports at 88% capacity, system margins are tight during peak periods.

Peak power prices can spike to £150–200+/MWh during tight evenings. Review your DUoS charging band and whether load-shifting or battery storage could reduce exposure. Non-commodity charges (TNUoS, DUoS, BSUoS, RO/CFD) now represent 40–50% of many HH bills — these are largely outside wholesale market control.

A fixed power contract for HH customers is never truly "fixed" unless non-commodity is also locked. Check your pass-through terms carefully.

Gas-Intensive Users

Your exposure runs through NBP spot, seasonal shape and LNG availability. The current 113–117p range for near-term gas is well above historical averages (~75p for Cal-27 one year ago). If your process requires consistent gas supply, consider whether a structured fixed or flexible approach provides better budget certainty than full pass-through.

Winter 2026 at 116.53p suggests the market is pricing in storage refill risk and possible cold weather. Gas-intensive I&C customers should model scenarios at 130p, 150p and 170p to understand stress-test exposure.

Volume tolerance clauses matter: exceeding agreed volume bands during cold snaps can trigger penalty rates significantly above contract prices.

Supplier Appetite & Price-Book Watch

Supplier pricing behaviour has been mixed through May. During the peak volatility in early May (NBP front-month above 130p, Brent above $110), several I&C suppliers shortened quote validity from 5–7 days to 2–3 days, and some withdrew from the spot market for 24–48 hours. As prices have settled, quote validity has largely returned to normal, though credit check requirements have tightened for new customers and those with adverse payment history.

For I&C and Mid Market, supplier appetite differs materially from SME. Larger volume customers (>£100k annual spend) generally find competitive pricing across 4–6 suppliers. Mid Market customers (£20k–£100k) may find fewer quotes, with some suppliers focusing on larger I&C accounts. Credit position, volume tolerance and shape premiums are increasingly important — suppliers are less willing to absorb volume risk or unusual load profiles without price adjustment.

Pass-through terms vary: some suppliers are offering fully delivered (commodity + non-commodity bundled) fixed prices, while others are quoting commodity-only with pass-through for TNUoS, DUoS and policy costs. The trend is toward greater transparency on pass-through, driven by customer demand and regulatory pressure. For flexible customers, tolerance band widths have narrowed slightly — some suppliers now requiring ±10% rather than ±15% annual volume tolerance.

Supplier book movement is not fully verified from public sources. Treat this as a commercial watch point and confirm against live supplier pricing.

Commercial Takeaway

Current Market Assessment

Gas has softened week-on-week but the 30-day trend remains elevated. Power has decoupled from gas direction, holding firmer on carbon strength. The near-term market (Summer–Winter 2026) is pricing in a combination of geopolitical risk, storage refill uncertainty and LNG tightness. Further out (2027–2029), the curve is lower, reflecting expectations of supply relief from new LNG capacity and demand moderation.

Risk is mixed but tilted to the upside for winter 2026/27. A cold winter, delayed LNG recovery, or renewed geopolitical disruption could push NBP above 150p and baseload above £120/MWh. Conversely, a mild winter, rapid storage refill, and successful Qatar recovery could see prices retreat toward 90–100p for Cal-27.

Waiting is not neutral. It is a decision to stay exposed to wholesale movement, supplier repricing and contract availability.

For customers inside their renewal window, the practical step is to review live pricing now, compare contract lengths, check supplier appetite and decide against budget certainty, risk tolerance and operational need. This is not automatic advice to fix. It is a reason to review live pricing against the customer's contract date, volume, risk profile and budget position.

Cheaper further out does not mean cheaper later. It means there may be an opportunity while that delivery period is still priced on the forward curve.

Flexible customers: Review hedge ratios and trigger levels. The backwardated curve creates opportunity for layered hedging into 2027, but do not over-commit before volume certainty is clear.

Fixed customers: Test 12, 24, 36 and 48-month pricing. Compare fully delivered vs commodity-only quotes. Check pass-through terms for non-commodity charges.

Gas-intensive users: Model stress scenarios at 130p, 150p and 170p. Review volume tolerance clauses. Consider whether fixed or flexible structures provide better budget protection.

Half-hourly electricity users: Review shape risk and peak exposure. Check DUoS banding. Assess whether load-shifting or on-site generation can reduce peak-period costs.

Terms of Reference

NBP
National Balancing Point — the UK virtual trading hub for natural gas. Prices quoted in pence per therm.
TTF
Title Transfer Facility — the Dutch virtual gas trading hub and European benchmark. Prices quoted in €/MWh.
Day-ahead
The price for delivery on the next trading day. Most liquid prompt contract, reflecting immediate supply/demand balance.
Month-ahead / Front Month
The nearest monthly futures contract. Most actively traded beyond the prompt. Sets the tone for near-curve pricing.
Front Season
The nearest seasonal contract (Summer or Winter). Used for medium-term hedging and budget planning.
Front Year
The nearest calendar year contract (e.g., Cal-27). Provides annual average price for longer-term procurement decisions.
Prompt Market
The market for immediate or near-immediate delivery — typically day-ahead and within-day contracts.
Near Curve
Contracts for delivery within the next 6–12 months. Most sensitive to weather, storage and prompt conditions.
Far Curve
Contracts for delivery beyond 12 months. Driven by structural supply/demand, infrastructure and policy expectations.
Forward Curve
The full set of future prices across all delivery periods. Shows market expectations and seasonal patterns.
Baseload Power
Electricity delivered continuously (24/7) across all half-hourly periods. The standard wholesale power contract.
Peak Power
Electricity delivered during high-demand weekday periods (typically 07:00–19:00). Priced at a premium to baseload.
CCGT
Combined Cycle Gas Turbine — the dominant gas-fired power plant technology in the UK. Efficiency ~50–55%.
EUA Carbon
EU Allowance — the carbon permit traded under the EU Emissions Trading Scheme. One allowance = one tonne of CO₂.
LNG
Liquefied Natural Gas — natural gas cooled to liquid form for transport by ship. The marginal supply source for UK/EU.
UKCS / NCS
UK Continental Shelf / Norwegian Continental Shelf — offshore gas production areas supplying GB via pipeline.
Interconnector
High-voltage undersea cable linking GB electricity grid to Continental Europe (e.g., IFA, Nemo, BritNed).
System Demand
Total electricity or gas required by the GB system at a given time. Measured in GW (electricity) or mcm/day (gas).
Seasonal Normal
The 5- or 10-year average demand level for a given date, adjusted for temperature and economic conditions.
Flexible Procurement
A purchasing strategy where the buyer hedges portions of volume over time, leaving some exposure to market movements.
Fixed Procurement
A purchasing strategy where the full volume is locked at a single price for the contract duration. Provides budget certainty.
Shape Risk
The risk that a customer's consumption profile (when they use energy) differs from the baseload shape they are priced on.
Volume Tolerance
The allowed deviation from contracted annual volume (typically ±10–15%). Exceeding bands can trigger penalty rates.
Pass-through Charges
Non-commodity costs (TNUoS, DUoS, BSUoS, RO, CFD) that may be passed through to the customer at actual cost.
Supplier Price Book
The internal pricing matrix suppliers use to quote customers, incorporating wholesale costs, risk margin and overheads.
