Futures Market Explanation

Why the Futures Market Matters

What the Futures Market Shows

The futures market shows the price for energy delivered in future periods. A customer renewing in September 2027 is not only interested in today's price. They are interested in what the market is currently offering for September 2027 onwards.

If 2027, 2028 or 2029 delivery periods are cheaper than the current market, the customer may be able to use a longer contract to access those cheaper future periods now, depending on supplier availability and contract rules.

The risk with waiting is that those future periods move closer every day. When they become the near-term market, they can pick up current risk, supplier caution, non-commodity changes, weather pressure or geopolitical premium.

The Risk with Waiting

  • ✕ Cheaper further out does not mean cheaper later. Today's cheaper future price is only available while that period is still future delivery.
  • ✕ The future market is only useful while the customer can still buy that future delivery period.
  • ✕ Waiting lets the market make the decision for the customer.
  • ✕ This is not automatic advice to fix. It is a reason to review live prices now.

In plain English: If the customer can currently contract into a cheaper future market, waiting does not protect that price. Waiting simply moves the customer closer to delivery, where today's risk premium can become tomorrow's contract price.

Ceasefire Reality Check

Is the Ceasefire Holding?

What the Ceasefire Was Meant to Cover

The April 8, 2026 ceasefire was understood to halt direct US-Iran and Iran-Israel military hostilities. It followed the February-March 2026 war that began with US-Israeli strikes on Iran and included Iranian retaliation against Gulf states and Israel.

What Actually Happened

  • US and Iran exchanged direct fire on 5-6 June (drones, missiles, airstrikes)
  • Iran fired ~30 missiles at Israel on 7 June
  • Israel struck Iran directly, including Mahshahr petrochemical complex, on 8 June
  • Israel struck Beirut/Hezbollah on 6-7 June
  • Houthis resumed missile attacks and declared Red Sea blockade on 8 June

Commercial Assessment

No major escalation is not the same as real de-escalation.

Limited escalation is still escalation when it involves direct US-Iran action, direct Iran-Israel fire, Gulf military targets, shipping routes or energy infrastructure.

The fact that both sides "halted" after 8 June does not erase the precedent that direct military exchange has resumed. The threshold for future escalation is now lower.

Headline relief is not the same as physical supply improvement. Diplomatic language may soften markets temporarily, but it does not reduce customer risk unless physical flows, shipping access, insurance premiums, LNG availability, NBP pricing and supplier price books also improve.

Customer Action

What UK Businesses Should Do Now

For Customers Within 12 Months of Contract End Date

1. Review immediately. Do not wait for clarity. The market can move before you get certainty.

2. Test live supplier pricing. Get actual quotes for your contract end date, not estimates.

3. Compare short and longer-term options. Look at 12, 24 and 36-month fixed contracts.

4. Consider fixing longer where pricing supports it. If forward markets are cheaper, a 36-month fix may access better value.

Waiting is not neutral. It is a bet that the World Bank baseline, current NBP pricing, current UK power pricing, unresolved military escalation, shipping risk, supplier price-book lag and the unsettled diplomatic position are all overstating the risk.

  • Qatari LNG exports normalising with verified production restart

Evidence That Would Increase Risk

  • Further direct strikes on energy infrastructure (nuclear, petrochemical, oil/gas fields)
