NESO Electricity Margin Notice

NESO (National Energy System Operator) issued an Electricity Margin Notice on 9 July 2026 for the evening peak period (18:30-22:30), with a projected system shortfall of 1,000 MW. This was the result of extreme temperatures across Europe reducing the availability of some generation. An EMN is not a blackout warning — it is a routine operational tool NESO uses to balance the electricity system. However, it does mean the system is tighter and NESO wants more available power.

Marginal Pricing Explained

Under marginal pricing, the market price can be influenced by the last and often more expensive unit of generation needed to meet demand. During heatwaves, cooling demand can rise while evening solar output falls, so the system may need more flexible generation. That can support higher UK power prices and balancing costs. The power market remains structurally linked to gas prices, so elevated NBP feeds through to higher power pricing. Power W-26 is up 39.4% year-on-year.

● Forward Market and Backwardation Explained

Understanding the curve structure

The forward market shows what gas or power is currently priced at for future delivery periods. That means a customer starting a new contract in 2028 may not be buying the same market as a customer renewing in 2026.

Backwardation means the near-term market is more expensive than the future market. This is the current structure: W-26 gas at 135.20p is materially more expensive than S-28 at 66.75p, W-28 at 74.00p, S-29 at 59.00p, W-29 at 69.02p, and S-30 at 59.28p. The difference between W-26 and S-30 is 75.92 p/therm.

"Backwardation is not a reason to wait. It is a reason to check whether the customer can act."

The chart in the next section shows the visual representation of this backwardation. The key commercial insight: a customer renewing now may be exposed to the expensive front of the curve (135.20p), while a customer with a later CED may be able to access a cheaper future delivery period now (potentially as low as 59.28p for S-30).

● Futures Market Explanation

Why cheaper further out does not mean cheaper later

"Cheaper further out does not mean cheaper later."

The future market is only useful while the customer can still buy that future delivery period. Once the delivery window approaches, the customer will be buying whatever the market is at that time — not the cheaper price that was available months earlier.

Consider the numbers: S-30 gas at 59.28p is available now as a forward price for delivery in Summer 2030. If the customer waits until 2030 to buy, they may face a completely different market. The 59.28p price is not a prediction of 2030 prices. It is a price that can be locked in today.

Waiting lets the market make the decision for the customer. If the backwardation persists, the customer may still get a reasonable price. If the curve flattens or inverts (contango), the customer loses the opportunity.

"The mistake customers make is thinking cheaper 2028 / 2029 pricing means they can wait. It does not. It means the cheaper part of the curve may be available now, and waiting gives the market more time to take it away."

● Hormuz Controlled Corridor Check

Open is not the same as normal

⚓ Hormuz: Managed Security Corridor

Trump's position appears to be that Iranian-linked shipping will be blocked or restricted, while non-Iranian Gulf energy flows are allowed to continue through controlled Hormuz lanes. The US blockade applies only to vessels transiting to or from Iranian ports. The southern route in Omani waters remains open for neutral vessels with US/Omani coordination.

CENTCOM stated: "Transit passage through the Strait of Hormuz to or from non-Iranian destinations will not be impeded." However, JMIC has raised the threat level to SEVERE, and UKMTO warns that deliberate hostile acts remain highly probable.

What "Managed Security Corridor" Means

  • Iranian-linked vessels are restricted or blocked by US naval blockade
  • Non-Iranian Gulf energy cargoes move through US/Omani-coordinated southern route
  • Vessels face VHF hailing, naval presence, and mine danger areas
  • Some ships transit with AIS transponders off to avoid detection
  • Insurance and war-risk premiums remain elevated
  • Traffic volumes well below pre-war levels (~19 US-assisted transits in 48 hours vs ~140/day pre-war)

"Hormuz may be open, but open is not the same as normal. If non-Iranian vessels need protected lanes while Iranian-linked vessels are blocked or restricted, the Strait is being managed as a security corridor."

The commercial issue is not only whether ships can pass. It is whether shipowners, insurers, LNG operators and suppliers trust the route enough to price it normally. A protected route still carries risk premium. The market does not need full closure for prices to move.

● TTF / NBP Explained

The two benchmarks that matter for UK business energy

TTF — Title Transfer Facility

The European gas benchmark, traded in the Netherlands. TTF serves as the reference price for continental European gas and indirectly influences global LNG pricing. When TTF rises, it signals European gas scarcity or risk premium. Current level: ~51-53 EUR/MWh.

NBP — National Balancing Point

The UK gas benchmark. NBP is the price reference for UK wholesale gas and directly feeds into UK business gas contracts. NBP responds to TTF, LNG arrivals, Norwegian flows, and UK-specific supply/demand factors. Current W-26 level: 135.20 p/therm (internal evidence).

How They Connect

LNG moves TTF, TTF can influence NBP, and NBP can move UK business gas prices. The interconnectors between the UK and continental Europe allow gas to flow in both directions, linking the two benchmarks. When TTF is elevated due to Asian LNG competition or European storage deficits, NBP typically follows.

Current levels: TTF ~51-53 EUR/MWh and NBP W-26 ~135.20 p/therm represent elevated pricing. NBP is well above the £1 threshold and has now breached 130p. Both are well below 2022 peaks but structurally above pre-2021 averages.

● Supplier Price-Book Lag

The opportunity and the danger

"Supplier price-book lag is the opportunity and the danger."

Wholesale markets move first. Supplier books often follow. When NBP moves 4.70p in a day (as it did overnight 14-15 July), supplier pricing systems may not reflect that change immediately. Some suppliers update daily, others weekly, and some only when triggered by volume thresholds.

This creates a window where wholesale prices have moved (W-26 now at 135.20p) but supplier books have not yet caught up. For customers, this is both an opportunity (rates may still be available at pre-move levels) and a danger (once books update, the old rates disappear).

What This Means for Different Customer Categories

Inside 90 Days

Act urgently. Your renewal is approaching fast and supplier books can move at any time. W-26 at 135.20p is the new benchmark. Waiting risks buying at even higher post-escalation rates.

Inside 12 Months

Review live options now. You have time to compare, but the window for pre-escalation pricing may already have closed. Front-end prices have moved sharply.

Outside 12 Months, High Usage

Check the forward curve. The 75.92p spread between W-26 (135.20p) and S-30 (59.28p) creates a potential future-start opportunity. An LOA lets you confirm without commitment.

"Once a supplier withdraws a rate, the customer cannot go back and buy yesterday's price."

Waiting for certainty can mean missing the price movement. In volatile markets, the customers who act when the window is open — rather than waiting for confirmation that the market has moved — typically achieve better outcomes.

● Commercial Takeaway

The bottom line for UK business energy buyers

"The market has not normalised."

Hormuz may be open, but it is not normal. If Iranian-linked vessels are restricted while non-Iranian Gulf energy cargoes move through controlled lanes, the Strait has become a managed security corridor. That carries risk premium.

Oil moving is the headline. LNG, gas and power are the customer risk. Brent at ~$86 captures attention, but UK business energy prices are driven by NBP (now at 135.20p), TTF, LNG supply, and supplier pricing.

Above £1 per therm is not safe. At 135.20p, UK gas is not only above £1 — it is above 130p. The market has moved again, and sharply. Year-on-year, W-26 is up 48.2%.

Supplier price-book lag is the opportunity and the danger. Wholesale markets move first. W-26 gained 4.70p overnight. Once a supplier withdraws a rate, the customer cannot go back and buy yesterday's price.

The buying window is still open, but it is more fragile than yesterday.

Customers renewing now may be buying the expensive front of the market (135.20p). Customers with later CEDs may be able to access a cheaper future market now (as low as 59.28p for S-30).

Cheaper further out does not mean cheaper later. Backwardation does not mean wait. It means check whether the cheaper future period can be secured now. The 75.92p spread between W-26 and S-30 is exceptional — but it can close.

This is a review-now window, not a wait-and-see market.
