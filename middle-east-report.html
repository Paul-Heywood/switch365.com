● Why the Futures Market Matters

The futures market shows the price for energy delivered in future periods. A customer renewing from September 2027 is not only interested in today's price -- they should look at what the market is currently offering for September 2027 onwards.

If 2027, 2028 or 2029 delivery periods are cheaper than the current market, the customer may be able to use that future pricing now, depending on supplier availability and contract rules.

Cheaper further out does not mean cheaper later. The future market is only useful while the customer can still buy that future delivery period. Waiting lets the market make the decision for the customer.

NBP Forward Curve (approximate midpoints) · Source: ICE, Argus

This is not automatic advice to fix. It is a reason to review live prices now. If your contract end date sits in a future period that is currently cheaper than the near-term market, there may be a commercial case for a longer-term fix. But that case depends on live supplier pricing, your risk profile, and your budget requirements. Test the market, compare options, and decide -- do not wait for certainty that may never arrive.

What Full Normalisation Requires

Full normalisation requires more than early vessel movement:

  • • Sustained commercial vessel traffic (not just selective LNG)
  • • War-risk insurance premiums falling materially
  • • Shipowner confidence returning to normal routes
  • • No further military incidents near the strait
  • • Full LNG and oil flow restoration to pre-war levels
  • • Iranian management protocols transparent and predictable

Impact on UK Energy

UK imports LNG via South Hook, Dragon, and Isle of Grain terminals. UK domestic North Sea production covers ~40% of gas demand; remainder imported via pipeline (Norway, continental Europe) and LNG. NBP is the UK wholesale benchmark and directly influences supplier pricing. Any disruption to LNG supply routes or European gas storage refill raises NBP and therefore UK power prices. Even after Hormuz reopens, knock-on effects on storage, insurance, and shipping schedules will persist for months.

Key commercial point: Early vessel movement is a positive signal, but the market should not treat it as proof of full normalisation. The gap between "first tankers moving" and "commercial shipping fully resumed" can be weeks or months. UK energy buyers should factor this lag into procurement decisions.

Supplier Price-Book Lag and Procurement Window

Has NBP moved before supplier books? NBP day-ahead fell from ~120 p/therm in late May to 96.69 p/th on 18 June. It has since bounced back toward ~99 p/th. Front-month and seasonal contracts have moved similarly. Wholesale markets typically reprice faster than supplier price books.

Has UK power moved before supplier books? UK power forward pricing not fully verified from public sources. The same lag dynamic is expected to apply -- supplier books may not have fully caught up.

How quickly do books update? Typically hours to days for prompt products; days to weeks for forward seasonal and calendar-year products. During high volatility, quote validity may shorten from 48 hours to 24 hours or same-day.

The bounce-back complicates timing. If wholesale has already started bouncing before supplier books caught the full down-move, the window for customers to benefit from the dip may be narrower than it first appeared.

Potential procurement window -- but fragile: If wholesale markets softened on deal hopes but have already started bouncing, customers may have a very short window to secure pricing. If the deal is signed as scheduled (19 June) and holds, books may reprice. If the deal falters, wholesale could spike and books may lag in the opposite direction. Either way, waiting reduces optionality.

Quote validity risk: Suppliers may shorten quote validity periods if volatility returns. The safest approach is to request quotes with the longest validity available and be prepared to decide within 24-48 hours during elevated volatility. Review now. Do not wait for perfect certainty, because perfect certainty usually arrives after the price has moved.

Market Interpretation

What the market is pricing now

The futures market is pricing a partial de-escalation via the US-Iran deal and early Hormuz movement, but not a full return to pre-war supply conditions. NBP at ~97 p/th is well below the ~140-160 p/th range seen during peak March-April panic, but still roughly double pre-war levels (~45-55 p/th). The bounce-back from 96.69 shows the market is not fully convinced.

Are traders fading headlines or taking escalation seriously?

The ~20% drop in NBP from late May and the ~15-20% drop in Brent from early June suggests traders are pricing in deal optimism. However, the bounce-back in gas and the fact that prices remain 50-100% above pre-war levels indicates caution. The IEA's warning that "the market will remain severely undersupplied through end of Q3" supports this view.

What would trigger the next leg up or down?

Down (lower prices): Successful deal signing and implementation; sustained Hormuz commercial traffic; no Lebanon escalation; European storage refill accelerates; talks progress smoothly.

Up (higher prices): Deal collapses or is delayed; Lebanon escalates further; Houthis escalate Red Sea attacks; European storage refill disappoints; talks interpretations diverge further.

Does the forward market support a longer fix?

Yes, conditionally -- but the window is fragile. Summer 2027 NBP at ~81 p/th is ~24% below Winter 2026 at ~106 p/th. Cal-2027 at ~82 p/th offers a discount to near-term. But this discount is only valuable if the customer acts while the window is open. If talks wobble or Lebanon escalates, the forward curve can reprice rapidly. If your contract end date falls in 2027 or 2028, the forward market may support a 2-3 year fix -- but this requires live supplier pricing to confirm.

Impact on UK Business Energy Prices

Why UK gas reacts to NBP, LNG and European storage risk: The UK is connected to European gas markets via interconnectors and imports LNG via three major terminals. NBP is the UK wholesale benchmark. When LNG supply is disrupted (via Hormuz or Red Sea), European competition for alternative cargoes intensifies, pushing TTF higher, which pulls NBP up with it.

Why electricity follows gas: Gas-fired generation sets the marginal price for UK electricity for a significant portion of hours. Even with record solar and strong wind, when renewable output drops, gas fills the gap. NBP therefore drives UK power pricing.

Why NBP matters to UK supplier pricing: Energy suppliers buy gas and power on wholesale markets (or hedge via futures). When NBP rises, their input costs rise. This flows through to business customer quotes, typically with a lag of days to weeks.

Why the bounce-back matters: If NBP is already bouncing from its low, supplier books may not have fully captured the dip. Customers waiting for "the bottom" may find it has already passed.

Why waiting is risky: If you are within 12 months of contract end and wait for "certainty," you give up the opportunity to contract into cheaper forward pricing. As time passes, that forward period becomes the near-term market and can absorb the same risk premium. The futures market is only useful while you can still buy that future delivery period. This is a review-now window, not a wait-and-see market.

Why early Hormuz movement is not normalisation: Even if the deal reopens Hormuz, shipping protocols, insurance costs, war-risk premiums, and shipowner caution will persist. Early LNG tanker movement does not equal normal European supply. LNG cargo scheduling, port congestion, and European storage refill all take time.

SMEs most exposed: Businesses on variable, deemed, rollover or out-of-contract rates are most exposed to wholesale volatility. Multi-site operators, hospitality, care, manufacturing, and retail with thin margins should prioritise securing fixed pricing.

Commercial Takeaway for UK Businesses

Prices have dropped because the market has been given hope, and there is now some real shipping movement to support that hope. But last night showed the risk has not disappeared. UK gas has already shown signs of bouncing after dipping lower. Talks have shown strain. Lebanon is escalating. These prices could still be lost as fast as they were gained.

Hope has moved prices. Now the market needs proof. Early vessel movement through Hormuz is positive. But one night of movement does not prove the market is safe. Full normalisation of shipping, insurance, storage refill, and supplier pricing will take weeks or months -- if it happens at all.

Cheaper further out does not mean cheaper later. The futures market allows customers to buy energy for a future delivery period. If that future delivery period is cheaper today, the customer may be able to access that pricing now if their contract timing allows it. But if the customer waits, that future period moves closer and can become the current market. If volatility, supply risk or supplier caution is still present, the cheaper price may disappear. The window is open, but it is obviously fragile.

Waiting is not neutral. It is a bet that the deal holds, Hormuz fully normalises, Lebanon does not escalate, supplier books stay favourable, talks recover, and the same prices remain available later. That is a lot of bets to place simultaneously.

For customers within 12 months of contract end date, the sensible step is to review immediately, test live supplier pricing, compare short-term and longer-term pricing, and decide while the market is giving them an opportunity.

This is a review-now window, not a wait-and-see market.
