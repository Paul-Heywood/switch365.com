Executive Summary

The World Bank Group's baseline forecast of 24% higher energy prices in 2026—even assuming the major Middle East disruption ends in May—creates a structurally repriced market where delay amplifies rather than reduces risk for UK business customers. Current forward curves for UK gas and power remain elevated above pre-crisis levels, with supplier price books lagging wholesale movements by days to weeks.

"The war is hitting the global economy in cumulative waves: first through higher energy prices, then higher food prices, and finally, higher inflation, which will push up interest rates and make debt even more expensive." — Indermit Gill, World Bank Chief Economist [178]

The commercial imperative is immediate: customers within 12 months of contract end should review procurement now, and where forward pricing and risk profiles align, consider fixing for 36+ months before further repricing closes the window. The World Bank's baseline—which assumes the most acute disruptions end in May—already validates this approach, as it embeds a structural price reset that will persist through 2026 regardless of diplomatic outcomes.

The World Bank's baseline forecast of 24% higher energy prices in 2026 is not a worst-case scenario—it's the central case assuming the most acute disruptions end in May. This means:

Delay Increases Risk, Not Reduces It
