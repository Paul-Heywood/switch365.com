Forward Market Analysis

Is the Market Cheaper Further Out?

02UK Gas Forward Curve — NBP

The forward curve shows what the market is charging for energy delivered in future periods. When further-out contracts are cheaper, the market is pricing today's risk premium higher than tomorrow's expected conditions.

What This Means

The futures market prices energy for delivery in future periods. When near-term contracts (Jun-26, Win-26) are expensive but further-out contracts (Sum-27, Cal-28) are cheaper, it means today's price carries more immediate risk — supply pressure, shipping uncertainty, geopolitical premium.

Why Waiting Is Risky

Customers often assume that if the 12-month or 18-month market is cheaper today, they can wait and pick it up later. That is not how the market works. As time passes, that future period becomes the near-term market and can absorb the same risk premium.

Commercial Message

If your contract end date allows, you may be able to contract into a market that is cheaper than near-term pricing. That can support fixing longer. But waiting does not guarantee those cheaper future prices become available later.

Cheaper further out does not mean cheaper later.

The future market is only useful if you act while it is still future pricing. Waiting means the customer stays exposed while today's future market becomes tomorrow's current market. For a customer whose contract starts 12–18 months from now, the relevant question is not only what energy costs today — it is what the market is offering for their delivery period. If that future period is cheaper, the customer may be able to contract into that lower forward market now. But if they wait, that future period moves closer and can pick up the same risk premium that today's prices carry. Waiting is not neutral. It is a decision to remain exposed.

Ceasefire Reality Check

10No Major Escalation Is Not the Same as Real De-escalation

The ceasefire announced on 7 April remains technically in place. But what does that actually mean for energy markets and shipping?

What the Ceasefire Says

A temporary two-week ceasefire was announced on 7 April 2026. It has been repeatedly extended. Talks are ongoing in Doha with Qatari mediators.

What Is Excluded or Disputed

Nuclear issues are excluded from current negotiations per Iran's Baghaei. Lebanon/Hezbollah is a point of contention — Iran wants it included, US says Israel can defend itself. The Abraham Accords expansion was rejected by Pakistan.

What Actually Happened on the Ground

US CENTCOM conducted strikes on Iranian missile sites in southern Iran on 25 May — during ceasefire. Hormuz traffic remains at ~10/day. 1,550+ vessels stranded. Qatari LNG production remains disrupted (Ras Laffan damage, force majeure declared March 4).

Commercial Interpretation

Temporary diplomatic language has created headline-driven market softness. But physical supply conditions have not materially improved. This is a potential procurement window, not proof the crisis is over.

Headline Relief vs Physical De-escalation

A paused attack, diplomatic optimism, Gulf-state mediation, or a deal looking closer may soften markets temporarily, but it does not reduce customer risk unless physical flows, shipping access, insurance premiums, LNG availability, wholesale pricing and supplier price books also improve. Temporary market softness should be treated as a potential procurement window, not proof the crisis is over.

Procurement Dynamics

Supplier Price-Book Lag and Procurement Window

14Are Supplier Books Catching Up?

When wholesale markets move quickly, supplier price books often lag. This creates potential windows of opportunity — and risk.

What We Know

Ofgem confirmed on 27 May that the price cap will rise 13% from July, reflecting wholesale costs from the 18 February–18 May assessment window. Business pricing typically moves ahead of the domestic price cap. The near-term wholesale softening of the past 3 days may not yet be fully reflected in live supplier quotes.

What Is Uncertain

Supplier book lag is not fully verified from public sources. Individual suppliers update at different frequencies — some daily, some weekly. Quote validity periods may shorten if headlines worsen. Fixed offers may be withdrawn without warning.

There May Be a Short Procurement Window

If wholesale markets have moved lower on weather-driven demand destruction but supplier books have not fully caught up, customers may have a short window to secure pricing before supplier repricing lands. That window can close quickly if escalation headlines return.

Forward market consideration: If your contract end date is 12+ months away, the Cal-28 gas price of 71.85p/th is materially cheaper than near-term contracts. This forward market opportunity is only available while that delivery period remains in the future. As time passes, that future period becomes the current market and can absorb the same risk premium.

UK Commercial Context

Impact on UK Business Energy Prices

16Why This Matters for UK Businesses

Gas and Electricity Linkage

UK gas prices react directly to European LNG markets and global shipping disruption. The UK is a net gas importer via LNG and Norwegian pipelines. When Hormuz disruption reduces global LNG availability, European and UK gas prices rise.

Electricity follows gas because gas-fired generation is the marginal price-setter for most hours in the UK power market. Even with strong renewables, gas determines the wholesale electricity price.

Shipping Hits Delivered Cost

Disruption to LNG shipping routes increases freight costs, extends delivery times, and reduces vessel availability. These costs flow through to delivered gas prices and ultimately to supplier pricing for business customers.

War-risk insurance cancellations mean ships that do sail charge significantly higher rates. Even if wholesale commodity prices stabilise, shipping premiums can keep delivered costs elevated.

The Repricing Window Can Close Quickly

Supplier pricing is not static. When wholesale markets move, supplier books typically update within hours to days. Quote validity periods can be shortened or withdrawn entirely during volatile periods.

Customers who wait for "more clarity" often find that the market has moved against them by the time they act.

SMEs Are Most Exposed

Businesses on variable, deemed, rollover, or out-of-contract rates have no protection from wholesale volatility. SMEs approaching renewal are particularly exposed because they typically have less procurement resource than large corporates.

Unlike large businesses with dedicated energy traders, most SMEs rely on brokers or suppliers to time their procurement. In volatile markets, that timing becomes critical.

What Customers Should Do

18Guidance for UK Businesses and SMEs

Waiting Is Not Neutral. It Is a Bet That the Risk Is Being Overstated.

This guidance is for Switch 365 Ltd customers and prospects. It is general commercial guidance, not site-specific advice. Individual circumstances vary.

Customers Within 12 Months of Contract End

Review immediately. Test available supplier pricing. Compare short-term and longer-term options. Do not wait for perfect clarity. The market can move before you realise.

If your contract end date allows, check whether you can access the cheaper forward market (Cal-27, Cal-28). The spread between near-term and further-out pricing is significant — 71.85p/th for Cal-28 vs 118.47p/th for Jun-26.

High-Consumption and Gas-Intensive Users

Manufacturing, hospitality, care homes, and other gas-intensive sectors face the greatest exposure. A 10p/therm move in wholesale gas can translate to thousands of pounds on annual bills for high-consumption sites.

These customers should prioritise securing longer-term fixes (36+ months) where forward pricing and risk profile support it.

Multi-Site Operators

Staggered renewal dates create complexity but also opportunity. Sites with renewal dates further out may be able to access cheaper forward pricing now. Sites with near-term renewals face the most urgent decision.

Consider portfolio approaches: fix near-term sites now, lock forward pricing for future sites where possible.

On Variable, Deemed, or Rollover Rates

These customers have no price protection and are fully exposed to wholesale volatility. The Ofgem price cap rise of 13% from July is a leading indicator — business rates typically move faster and further than the domestic cap.

These customers should fix immediately if viable pricing is available.

The Core Commercial Message

If the customer can currently contract into a cheaper future market, waiting does not protect that price. Waiting simply moves the customer closer to delivery, where today's risk premium can become tomorrow's contract price. Cheaper further out does not mean cheaper later. It means there may be an opportunity now, while that period is still priced as future delivery.

For customers within 12 months of contract end date, the commercially sensible step is to review immediately, test available supplier pricing, compare short-term and longer-term pricing, and — where pricing and risk profile support it — consider fixing now and fixing longer before supplier books move further.
