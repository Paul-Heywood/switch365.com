Why the Futures Market Matters

The futures market shows the price for energy delivered in future periods. A customer renewing in September 2027 is not only interested in today's price. They are interested in what the market is currently offering for September 2027 onwards.

If 2027, 2028 or 2029 delivery periods are cheaper than the current market, the customer may be able to use that future pricing now, depending on supplier availability and contract rules.

The risk with waiting is that those future periods move closer every day. When they become the near-term market, they can pick up current risk, supplier caution, non-commodity changes, weather pressure or geopolitical premium.

Supplier Price-Book Lag and Procurement Window

Why wholesale moves and supplier pricing do not always move together

What is price-book lag?

Supplier price-book lag occurs when wholesale markets move (up or down) but the prices that suppliers offer to business customers have not yet fully adjusted. This can create a short window where customers can secure pricing before supplier repricing catches up.

Current assessment

  • NBP has moved higher (intraday spike to 123.45p on 8 Jun) [Trading Economics]
  • UK power has moved higher (day-ahead to £114/MWh on 8 Jun) [Smart Energy UK]
  • Brent has been volatile ($93-96/bbl range) [Trading Economics]
  • Whether supplier books have caught up is not publicly verifiable

Supplier Book Lag — Not Verified

Supplier book lag is not fully verified from public sources, but it is a normal commercial risk when wholesale markets move quickly. Customers may have a short window to secure pricing before supplier repricing lands. The window may have already narrowed or closed if books have caught up.

Why this matters to customers within 12 months of renewal

  • If wholesale has risen but supplier books have not yet repriced, there may be a brief window to lock in before the increase
  • If wholesale has risen and books have already repriced, waiting for a dip is a high-risk strategy
  • If forward markets are cheaper than near-term (backwardation), a longer fix may access cheaper future pricing now
  • Quote validity periods may shorten during volatile periods — a quote valid for 5 days may become 48 hours
  • Fixed offers may be withdrawn if volatility spikes further

Market Interpretation

What the market is pricing now — and what it means

What NBP is telling us

NBP is currently in backwardation — near-term contracts (Win-26 at 119.70p) trade at a premium to longer-dated contracts (Cal-27 at 93.70p, Cal-28 at 71.98p). This means the market is pricing higher risk into the immediate period and assuming some normalisation further out.

However, backwardation does not guarantee that future prices will remain low. If escalation continues, the risk premium currently in Win-26 can roll forward into Sum-27 and Win-27 as those periods become the near-term market.

What UK power is telling us

UK power mirrors the gas curve: Win-26 at £103.03 vs Cal-27 at £84.33. The power market is also backwardated, reflecting the same assumption that near-term geopolitical risk will ease over time. But the same warning applies — waiting does not protect the cheaper Cal-27 price.

  • Major LNG supply disruption (Qatari, UAE)

What would trigger the next leg down

Key point: Temporary diplomatic relief or headline softness is not the same as physical de-escalation. The market can soften on rhetoric, but if supply risk has not actually improved, prices can snap back quickly.

Impact on UK Business Energy Prices

Why global events end up on UK business energy bills

Why UK gas reacts to Middle East events

  • The UK imports ~50% of its gas, mostly via LNG from Qatar, US and global markets [UK Parliament]
  • NBP is the UK wholesale benchmark — when NBP moves, supplier gas pricing moves
  • LNG is a globally traded commodity — disruption in the Middle East tightens global supply and pushes UK prices higher
  • European storage levels (currently 42.1%) affect UK prices through interconnectors and TTF linkage

Why electricity follows gas

  • Gas-fired generation still sets the marginal price in most UK settlement periods
  • When gas prices rise, electricity prices follow — typically with a multiplier effect
  • Carbon costs (UK ETS) add to fossil fuel generation costs, feeding into power pricing
  • UK power futures matter because suppliers use them to price fixed-term electricity contracts

Ofgem Price Cap Impact

The domestic price cap will rise to ~£1,850 in July 2026 (13% increase) and ~£1,929 in October [Cornwall Insight]. Business customers do not have a price cap — their exposure is direct. For businesses on rollover, deemed or out-of-contract rates, the wholesale increase flows through immediately.

Commercial Takeaway for UK Businesses

Volatility is real. NBP is a key UK gas benchmark. UK power futures are key for electricity renewal decisions. The market can move before supplier books fully reprice.

Buyers within 12 months of contract end should be reviewing immediately. Where appropriate, the commercial case is to fix now and fix long (36+ months) before further repricing hits.

If NBP, UK power or the wider market further out is cheaper, this can strengthen the case for a longer-term fix — especially for SMEs that want budget certainty.

Cheaper further out does not mean cheaper later. The futures market is only useful while the customer can still buy that future delivery period. Waiting can allow the cheaper future market to become the more expensive current market.

If the World Bank baseline is already for materially higher energy prices in 2026 even assuming disruption eases, that makes delay more dangerous, not less. If the EIA now assumes disruption lasts longer, the World Bank baseline may be looking optimistic.

Waiting is not neutral. It is a bet that the World Bank baseline, current NBP pricing, current UK power pricing, unresolved military escalation, shipping risk, supplier price-book lag and the unsettled diplomatic position are all overstating the risk.

For customers within 12 months of contract end date, the commercially sensible step is to review immediately, test available supplier pricing, compare short-term and longer-term pricing, and, where pricing and risk profile support it, consider fixing now and fixing longer before supplier books move further.

If the customer can currently contract into a cheaper future market, waiting does not protect that price. Waiting simply moves the customer closer to delivery, where today's risk premium can become tomorrow's contract price.

If the NBP forward market is cheaper than the near-term market, that does not mean the customer can wait and get the same price later. It means the customer may have an opportunity now, while that period is still priced as future delivery.

If the UK power forward market is cheaper than the near-term market, that does not mean the customer can wait and get the same price later. It means the customer may have an opportunity now, while that period is still priced as future delivery.
