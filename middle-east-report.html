UK Wholesale Energy Update

What moved in UK gas and power markets over the primary research window (12-16 June 2026).

1
NBP Natural Gas

NBP day-ahead fell to 101.01 p/therm on 15 June, down 9.74% from the previous session (111.91 p/therm) as the preliminary US-Iran deal triggered a sharp risk-premium reduction. Over the past month, NBP has fallen approximately 18%, though it remains roughly 13.7% higher than a year ago. The front-month (July 2026) contract trades near 102.00 p/therm, with Q3 2026 at 101.99.

Winter 2026 retains a premium at ~107.50 p/therm, reflecting residual supply-risk concerns despite the de-escalation headline. The forward curve remains in backwardation for near-term contracts, with seasonal spreads widening into winter. European gas storage levels at 44.34% full (501.24 TWh as of 15 June, per AGSI) are significantly below year-ago levels, providing underlying price support even as the geopolitical risk premium compresses.

TTF Dutch Gas (the European benchmark) front-month trades at ~44.39 EUR/MWh, down ~5% on the session, tracking NBP lower. The TTF-NBP spread remains relatively narrow, indicating shared continental and UK market sentiment on the deal.

2
UK Baseload Power

UK day-ahead baseload power is quoted at £99.10/MWh on 16 June, down from approximately £103.48/MWh previously. The month-ahead (July 2026) contract has dropped sharply to ~£69.12/MWh, reflecting both the gas-price move and improved wind-generation forecasts. Winter 2026 power trades near £85/MWh, down from peaks around £109/MWh in March 2026.

Power has moved less aggressively than gas because electricity incorporates carbon costs (UK ETS), network charges, and generation-mix dynamics that do not respond instantly to oil/gas headlines. The day-ahead market remains volatile: Catalyst Commercial reported day-ahead power at £100.59/MWh on 9 June, with Norwegian compressor failures and Gulf risk still supporting the curve even as Brent sold off.

Forward power for Summer 2027 is offered near £70/MWh, with Winter 2027 near £82/MWh. The seasonal discount for summer contracts reflects lower demand and higher solar output, though low-wind periods can still push day-ahead prices sharply higher when gas-fired generation sets the marginal cost.

3
Brent & WTI Crude Oil

Brent crude settled at $82.93/bbl on 16 June, down from $87.33 on 12 June and down dramatically from the $126.10 peak on 30 April 2026. WTI trades at $80.50/bbl, down from $84.88 on 12 June. Both benchmarks fell approximately 5% on Monday 15 June alone as news of the preliminary US-Iran deal broke.

The oil move represents expectation pricing rather than proven physical supply restoration. Brent has now fallen 34% from its April peak, but this reflects the market pricing in anticipated Hormuz reopening, not confirmed full flow restoration. As investingLive noted: "Oil markets are pricing in relief that may arrive well ahead of actual supply normalisation."

Brent forward curve: Aug 2026 $83.63, Sep 2026 $82.64, Oct 2026 $81.65, Nov 2026 $80.75, Dec 2026 $79.95, Jan 2027 $79.15. The backwardated structure suggests the market expects near-term supply improvement but retains longer-term uncertainty.

Futures Market Explanation

The day-ahead price reflects physical delivery tomorrow -- it is the most volatile and responds immediately to weather, demand, and news. The front month is the nearest traded contract, reflecting near-term market expectations. Seasonal contracts (Winter/Summer) bundle six months of delivery: Winter covers October-March (peak heating demand), Summer covers April-September.

When day-ahead falls faster than Winter-26, the market is saying "the immediate crisis is easing, but we are not sure about January yet." That is exactly the pattern now. The wide gap between day-ahead (£99/MWh power) and front-month (£69/MWh) reflects the deal-driven risk premium compression in near-term contracts.

Market Drivers

The factors moving UK wholesale energy prices right now, ranked by current influence.

1
US-Iran Preliminary Deal Reported

The announcement of a preliminary peace deal / MOU on 14 June triggered the sharpest single-day oil and gas price falls since early April. Markets are pricing expected Hormuz reopening and blockade relief. However, the deal is not yet formally signed (expected 19 June) and operational implementation remains uncertain. This is the dominant price driver but it is sentiment-driven, not supply-driven.

2
Hormuz Operational Status Partial

Only one LNG tanker (Disha, operated by India's Petronet) has crossed Hormuz since the deal. ~155-215 tankers remain in the Gulf. BIMCO says transit remains "very risky." Mine clearance needs 40-50 days minimum. The physical supply risk has not fully cleared even if the political signal is positive.

3
European Gas Storage Verified

EU storage at 44.34% full is significantly below year-ago levels and the lowest start to injection season since 2018. This provides structural price support even as geopolitical risk premium compresses. Without Qatari LNG (blocked by Hormuz), Europe faces a difficult refill season.

4
Shipowner & Insurer Caution Verified

War-risk insurance remains at 1-4% of vessel value (vs. <0.1% pre-war). Japanese, German, and Danish shippers have all stated they will wait for confirmed safety before resuming normal operations. Mitsui O.S.K.: "We will only resume navigation once safety has been fully confirmed."

5
Norwegian Supply & Outages Verified

Norwegian compressor failures and maintenance outages continued to support NBP and UK Power curves through mid-June. Even as Gulf risk premium eased, North Sea supply constraints provided a price floor. This domestic factor partially offset the geopolitical relief.

6
Red Sea / Yemen / Houthis Ongoing

On 8 June, Yemen's Houthis threatened to ban Israel-linked ships from the Red Sea. Even if Hormuz improves, Bab el-Mandeb / Red Sea risk can remain separate. The Houthis launched a missile at Israel on 28 March. This is a dual-choke-point risk that the US-Iran deal does not directly address.

Ceasefire Reality Check

Preliminary peace deal does not equal complete de-escalation.
Formal signing still matters.
Operational implementation matters more than political wording.
Shipping confidence matters more than social media claims.
Lower oil prices are encouraging, but they are not proof that supplier books, LNG flows, NBP or UK Power have fully normalised.

The gap between political announcement and physical normalisation is the central risk for energy markets this week. On Sunday 14 June, the US and Iran announced a preliminary deal. On Monday 15 June, Trump declared Hormuz open. Yet on the same day, the US military confirmed the blockade remains until 19 June, BIMCO called transit "very risky," only one LNG tanker crossed, and 155-215 tankers remained stuck in the Gulf.

This is not a contradiction -- it is a sequence. Political agreement comes first. Formal signing follows. Operational implementation (mine clearance, insurance normalisation, shipowner confidence) comes third. Physical supply normalisation comes last. Markets, however, price all stages simultaneously, which creates the current disconnect: oil and gas prices have fallen on stage one (political agreement) while physical supply remains at stage zero (blocked).

For UK business energy buyers, the key question is not whether the deal will hold -- it is whether supplier price books have caught up with the wholesale move. If they have not, there may be a short procurement window. If they have, the opportunity is already narrowing.

Shipping and Choke Point Status

The physical reality of Hormuz, Red Sea, and Bab el-Mandeb transit.

H
Strait of Hormuz

Status: Politically promised to reopen; operationally partially restricted. Partial

Vessel Movements: Only 1 LNG tanker (Disha) crossed 15 June. Pre-war: 100-140 transits/day. Current estimate: 12-15/day in recent weeks. Kpler projects 40/day within a month if deal holds.

Tanker Backlog: 155-215 tankers in Mideast Gulf. 300 fully loaded + 250 empty stranded in Gulf. 300 empty waiting in Gulf of Oman. Oil Brokerage: backlog clears in 8-10 days under unrestricted navigation; Kpler estimates 4-8 weeks realistically.

Mine Clearance: 40-50 days minimum using minesweepers and underwater drones; up to 6 months for comprehensive sweep (Kpler). CENTCOM confirms mine-clearance efforts ongoing.

Insurance: War-risk premiums at 1-4% of vessel value per transit (vs. <0.1% pre-war). For a $200M vessel: up to $8M per crossing. Underwriters "quick to raise, slow to lower."

Toll Dispute: Iran wants 60-day toll-free window then joint administration with Oman collecting fees. US insists permanently toll-free. Paying fees to a sanctioned entity creates legal exposure for shippers.

R
Red Sea / Bab el-Mandeb

Status: Separate risk from Hormuz. Houthi threats ongoing. Ongoing

Houthi Position: On 8 June, Houthis threatened to ban Israel-linked ships from Red Sea. Called ceasefire a "great victory" for Iran but warned of further attacks depending on developments. Launched missile at Israel on 28 March.

Traffic: Daily traffic through Bab el-Mandeb remains significantly below pre-war levels as of May 2026. Suez Canal transits down sharply since 2023 Houthi campaign.

Commercial Impact: Even if Hormuz fully reopens, Red Sea risk can remain separate. Dual-choke-point risk: Hormuz for oil/LNG (20% of global supply), Bab el-Mandeb for Suez/transit shipping. Rerouting around Cape of Good Hope adds ~10 days and $1M+ in fuel costs per voyage.

Insurance: Red Sea war-risk premiums remain elevated from 2024-2025 Houthi campaign. Combined Hormuz + Red Sea risk has created a permanent repricing event for marine war risk, per Lloyd's and Howden Re.

Key Distinction: Access to Iranian ports and access through Hormuz are not the same thing. The US blockade applied to Iranian ports; Hormuz closure was an Iranian action. The deal addresses both, but implementation timelines may differ. Blockade remains until 19 June signing; Hormuz reopening depends on mine clearance and shipowner confidence.

Supplier Price-Book Lag

Supplier price-book lag is not fully verified from public sources, but it is a normal commercial risk when wholesale markets move quickly. Based on standard industry practice and Switch 365 market monitoring:

  • Oil and wholesale markets softened sharply (Brent -5%, NBP -9.74%) on the 15 June deal headline.
  • Supplier price books may not move at the same speed. Some suppliers reprice daily; others weekly or on a triggering threshold.
  • Customer quotes may still reflect the previous risk premium, particularly for fixed-term contracts priced before 15 June.
  • Some supplier books may reprice down, hold steady, or move cautiously depending on their hedging strategy and risk appetite.
  • If the deal fails or implementation stalls, supplier books can move back up quickly -- possibly faster than they came down.
Supplier price-book lag can work both ways. It can create a short opportunity after market relief, but it can also close quickly if the headline fails.
Waiting for certainty can mean missing the price movement. The best action is to test live pricing now and compare against your current contract.

For UK business customers, the practical implication is: request fresh quotes this week. Even if your renewal is 3-6 months away, understanding where supplier books currently sit gives you a baseline. If quotes are lower than your existing rate, you have a decision to make. If they are not, you have confirmation that either (a) your supplier has already repriced, or (b) the wholesale move has not fully transmitted.
