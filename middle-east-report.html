NESO and marginal pricing — why power stays exposed

NESO is the National Energy System Operator. It watches the electricity system in real time and makes sure there is enough power available to meet demand. A margin notice is not a blackout warning, but it does mean the system is tighter and NESO wants more available power.

Under marginal pricing, the market price can be influenced by the last and often more expensive unit of generation needed to meet demand. During heatwaves, cooling demand can rise while evening solar output falls, so the system may need more flexible generation. That can support higher UK power prices and balancing costs. NESO issued electricity margin notices during the June and July heatwaves — including an emergency import purchase near £1,400/MWh on 24 June — and a fresh notice during the third summer heatwave on 9–10 July.

Forward Market And Backwardation Explained

What the forward market is

The forward market shows what gas or power is currently priced at for future delivery periods. That means a customer starting a new contract in 2027, 2028, 2029 or 2030 may not be buying the same market as a customer renewing in 2026.

Backwardation means the near-term market is more expensive than the future market. Today's UK curve is deeply backwardated: Winter-26 gas offered at 148.50p/th against Summer-27 at 96.00p/th and Summer-28 at 67.00p/th (internal offered range, Wed 22 Jul AM).

Why it matters commercially

2027 matters because it shows whether value starts before the longer-dated 2028 / 2029 / 2030 opportunity.

Backwardation is not a reason to wait. It is a reason to check whether the customer can act. A customer renewing into the front of this curve is buying the expensive part of the market; a customer with a later CED may be able to secure a cheaper future delivery period now.

Section 7

Futures Market Explanation

Cheaper further out does not mean cheaper later.

The future market is only useful while the customer can still buy that future delivery period. Futures contracts trade today at today's view of tomorrow — and that view can reprice overnight when a strait is threatened or a strike cycle extends.

Waiting lets the market make the decision for the customer. If the conflict normalises, the back of the curve may not fall much further — it is already cheap. If the conflict worsens, the back of the curve rises towards the front. The asymmetry favours acting while the discount exists.

Duration Risk Assessment

Does this now look like a longer conflict?

The evidence points to a higher probability of a rolling conflict lasting weeks or months, unless a ceasefire is implemented and shipping normalises. This is an assessment, not a certainty — do not read it as a prediction that the war will run for months.

  • Casualty–retaliation loop: US deaths in Jordan and Iraq have been followed by harder retaliation language and continued strikes — rising casualties make it harder for either side to step back without a visible outcome.
  • Trump language: "many times greater" retaliation pledge; ceasefire declared "over"; talks framed as likely a waste of time.
  • Repeated strike cycle: eleven consecutive nights, with targets widening geographically and by type.
  • War-cost estimate: ~$37.5bn spent or committed through the fiscal year end — a figure that itself plans beyond a single strike cycle.
  • Supplemental funding request: ~$88bn formally sent to Congress on 24 June (~$67bn for the Pentagon), largely Iran-war related.
  • Proposed 2027 defence budget: $1.5tn request — nearly 50% above current levels — signals a longer-force-posture shift.
  • Military stretch: lawmakers openly questioning munitions depletion, readiness and the absence of an exit strategy; US assets managing Hormuz and now a Houthi/Red Sea front simultaneously.
  • Second front: Houthi Saudi-facing blockade threat adds Bab el-Mandeb to an already abnormal Hormuz.
  • No route normalisation: Hormuz crossings remain a fraction of pre-war levels; Red Sea insurance repricing higher; no implemented ceasefire.
Duration risk now matters as much as escalation risk.
This is no longer only about what happened last night. It is about how long the market believes this can continue.
Markets price duration when political, military and shipping signals all point the same way.
US funding requests do not prove the war will last months, but they do show that Washington is preparing for costs beyond a single strike cycle.

Do not present duration risk as certainty. Present it as the market risk now being priced.

LNG Choke-Point Check

  • The Saudi-facing Houthi blockade is mainly an oil-route problem, not a direct Saudi LNG export problem.
  • Saudi Arabia is not a major LNG exporter. The blockade does not directly remove LNG supply.
  • The bigger LNG choke point remains Hormuz. Qatari LNG and UAE LNG need Hormuz to exit the Gulf — and Hormuz traffic is at a near-standstill, delaying the anticipated recovery in Qatari LNG exports.
  • Bab el-Mandeb matters for LNG because it damages Red Sea / Suez confidence and adds insurance, routing and shipping-friction risk — a second layer, not a direct supply cut.
  • Do not convert oil-exposure figures into LNG-exposure figures. The "~7% of global oil supply" Bab el-Mandeb scenario figure refers to oil, not LNG.
  • If Hormuz remains abnormal, that is the direct LNG problem. If Bab el-Mandeb also becomes unreliable, that is the second-layer shipping-confidence problem. The LNG market can price both layers at once.

Section 17 — Mandatory

Red Sea / Suez / SUMED Route Risk

Bab el-Mandeb is the southern gate of the Red Sea route. Suez is the northern gate. Yanbu is Saudi's Red Sea pressure valve — fed by the East-West Pipeline and currently handling millions of barrels per day that can no longer move through Hormuz. SUMED is part of the workaround for crude that cannot move cleanly through Suez on fully laden VLCCs.

  • VLCC: a Very Large Crude Carrier, typically carrying around 2 million barrels of crude (larger units ~2.1–2.2m depending on size, crude density, draught and loading). 2 million barrels is roughly 318 million litres.
  • ULCC: an Ultra Large Crude Carrier, around 3–3.7 million barrels — roughly 477–588 million litres.
  • A fully laden VLCC is not a clean Suez solution — draught restrictions mean fully laden VLCCs generally cannot transit Suez directly. A ULCC is even less of a clean Suez solution.
  • The workaround is not simple trucking — the cargo is far too large to move practically by road; the truck example is only useful to show scale, not as a real logistics solution. The real workaround is pipeline, lightering, partial offload, SUMED and vessel logistics: unload part of the cargo, pump it through the SUMED pipeline to the Mediterranean, or transit lighter.
  • Rerouting Asia-bound cargoes via Suez and the Mediterranean then around Africa adds weeks to voyages — the two tankers that U-turned face exactly that.
  • Suez and SUMED can help, but they add friction — time, cost, capacity constraints and timing risk. In a stressed market, friction is enough to support risk premium.
Suez becomes more important, but it is not a clean replacement for Bab el-Mandeb. The workaround adds friction, and friction gets priced.
