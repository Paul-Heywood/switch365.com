Executive Summary

Overnight Risk Recalibration

The 24-hour period from 20–21 April 2026 has witnessed a sharp deterioration in the operational security environment of the Strait of Hormuz. The decisive event was the confirmed interception by US naval forces of an Iranian-flagged cargo vessel on 19 April, which triggered an immediate Iranian response: Tehran rescinded its 17 April declaration that the Strait of Hormuz was "completely open" and reimposed transit restrictions. [462]

The speed of this reversal—open, closed, reopened, and closed again within 72 hours—has injected extreme uncertainty into shipping schedules, insurance markets, and commodity pricing. [453]

Critical Transmission Mechanism

For UK business energy buyers, this is not a distant geopolitical spectacle; it is a direct transmission mechanism for higher gas and electricity costs, operating through LNG supply disruption, European storage refill risk, and forward curve repricing.

The market reaction was immediate and severe. Brent crude surged to $96.64/barrel in early trading on 20 April—a 6.9% single-session move that erased the previous week's risk reduction. [453]

Ceasefire Reality Check

The "Phantom Ceasefire" Dynamic

The temporary ceasefire agreed on 7 April 2026 is scheduled to expire on "Wednesday evening Washington time" (22 April), according to Trump's 20 April statement. Trump added that it is "highly unlikely" he would extend the truce further if a deal is not reached. [461]

However, the ceasefire's effects on the ground have been limited from the outset. Hapag-Lloyd CEO Rolf Habben Jansen stated publicly that "no noticeable change" to Strait of Hormuz access occurred following the ceasefire announcement, with vessel traffic remaining severely constrained. [444]

The conflict is now threatening a second maritime choke point. On 21 April at 01:15 UTC, Houthi official media announced that "all vessels in Red Sea and Gulf of Aden affiliated with hostile states are legitimate targets." [281]

This spillover risk matters because simultaneous Hormuz and Bab el-Mandeb disruption would create a global energy supply crisis, affecting crude, refined products, and LNG flows to Europe and Asia, with direct impact on UK wholesale gas and power prices.
