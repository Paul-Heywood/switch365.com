ℹ️ What This Means

The futures market is the price for energy delivered in a future period. When near-term is expensive but further-out is cheaper, it means today's price contains more immediate risk — supply pressure, shipping uncertainty, weather risk, storage concern and geopolitical premium.

For a customer whose contract starts 12 months from now, the relevant question is not only what energy costs today. It is what the market is offering for their delivery period. If that future period is cheaper, the customer may be able to contract into that lower forward market now.

The key point: waiting does not mean today's cheaper future price becomes tomorrow's available price. As time passes, that future period becomes the near-term market. If the same risks are still present, or if supplier books reprice, that cheaper future price can disappear.

⚠️ Why Waiting Can Be Expensive

Customers often assume that if the 12-month or 18-month market is cheaper today, they can wait and pick it up later. That is not how the market works. The future price is available because the customer is buying that future delivery period now. If the customer waits, the delivery period moves closer, current risk gets built into the price, and supplier books can change.

In simple terms: the cheaper forward market can become the expensive current market.

🎯 Commercial Message for Customers

If your contract end date is far enough out, you may be able to contract into a market that is cheaper than the near-term market. That can support fixing longer where the price, contract date, usage profile and risk position make sense.

Do not let the market make the decision for you by holding off. Review the position now, test live supplier pricing, compare short-term and longer-term options, and decide while the forward market is still available.

"Cheaper further out does not mean cheaper later."
"The future market is only useful if you act while it is still future pricing."
"Waiting means the customer stays exposed while today's future market becomes tomorrow's current market."
This is not automatic advice to fix. It is a reason to review now.

Supplier Price-Book Lag & Procurement Window

Why the gap between wholesale and supplier pricing matters for your renewal

ℹ️ What Is Supplier Price-Book Lag?

Supplier price books are the internal rates that energy suppliers use to quote fixed-term contracts to business customers. These books are updated periodically based on wholesale market movements — but not instantly. When wholesale markets move quickly (up or down), there can be a lag of hours, days or even weeks before supplier books fully reflect the new wholesale level.

📈 When Wholesale Rises

Supplier books may still show "old" lower prices for a short window. Customers can sometimes secure pricing before books update upward. This window is typically 24-72 hours.

📉 When Wholesale Falls

Supplier books may hold elevated prices even as wholesale softens. This is frustrating for buyers — but it also means the "procurement window" on the way down is narrower, because suppliers protect margin.

⚠️ Current Situation (4 June 2026)

Wholesale markets have moved. UK NBP gas Jul-26 fell 3.6% on 2 June settlement. Brent has fallen from $138 to $97 over the past month. Power is mixed — near-term down day-on-day but up 8.6% over 30 days.

Supplier book status: Not fully verified from public sources. However, normal commercial behaviour suggests:

  • If wholesale fell 3.6% on 2 June, supplier repricing would typically begin within 24-48 hours
  • If Brent's month-long decline has not fully fed through, some supplier books may still carry elevated oil-linked gas pricing
  • Quote validity periods are likely shortening in volatile conditions — from 7-14 days to 24-48 hours
  • Fixed offers may be withdrawn if wholesale moves sharply intraday

🎯 The Procurement Window Assessment

"Customers may have a short window to secure pricing before supplier repricing fully lands."

If wholesale markets have softened but supplier books have not fully caught up, there may be a brief opportunity to access relatively competitive fixed pricing. That window closes as soon as books update — or as soon as wholesale reverses direction on new headlines.

Conversely, if supplier books have already caught up with the wholesale softening, the window may be narrowing. The only way to know is to test live supplier pricing now.

If supplier book evidence cannot be verified: "Supplier book lag is not fully verified from public sources, but it is a normal commercial risk when wholesale markets move quickly."

Why global events show up on your business energy bill — and what to do about it

🎯 Why Waiting for Certainty Can Mean Losing the Cheaper Forward Curve

If your contract end date is in Q4 2026, Q1 2027 or beyond, the forward market (Cal-27, Cal-28) may currently be cheaper than near-term pricing. That is a potential opportunity. But if you wait for "certainty" — for the deal to be signed, for Hormuz to fully reopen, for markets to stabilise — two things happen:

  1. The delivery period moves closer, and the same risk premium that exists today gets built into your renewal period
  2. Supplier books reprice, and the forward advantage may disappear

Waiting does not protect you. It removes your option to act.

📊 The Futures Market Logic — Plain English

"Cheaper further out does not mean cheaper later."

The futures market allows customers to buy energy for a future delivery period. If that future delivery period is cheaper today, the customer may be able to access that pricing now if their contract timing allows it. But if the customer waits, that future period moves closer and can become the current market. If volatility, supply risk or supplier caution is still present, the cheaper price may disappear.

"If the customer can currently contract into a cheaper future market, waiting does not protect that price. Waiting simply moves the customer closer to delivery, where today's risk premium can become tomorrow's contract price."
