Market Drivers
Why the market is behaving this way

● Geopolitical Risk

  • Trump paused a scheduled attack on Iran (planned for 19 May) after requests from Qatar, Saudi Arabia and UAE. "Serious negotiations" are now taking place. Euronews
  • Hormuz remains effectively closed. The EIA assumes the strait stays shut until late May with flows picking up in June. The IEA reports 14 mb/d of oil shut in and cumulative losses exceeding 1 billion barrels. EIA IEA
  • Trump warned "the clock is ticking." He told the military to remain ready for a "full, large scale assault" if talks fail. The pause is conditional, not a policy reversal. Jerusalem Post
  • Iran is allowing selective Chinese vessel passage through Hormuz under its "management protocols" — not normal commercial reopening. China Daily

● UK Power Market

  • Gas still sets the marginal price for most hours in the UK. When wind output drops, gas-fired generation fills the gap and lifts power prices directly.
  • Wind generation has been variable through May. Day-ahead power climbed to £116/MWh on 20 May following a drop in wind.
  • Nuclear output is steady but inflexible. The UK relies on gas for swing supply, which is why wholesale gas and power move together. TEAM Energy

● Price Volatility

  • Prices remain structurally elevated but have softened from recent peaks on diplomatic headlines. This is headline relief, not physical de-escalation.
  • Supplier pricing remains cautious. Fixed contract offers reflect elevated wholesale curves and risk premiums. Rollover and deemed rates are exposed to further moves.
  • The spread between fixed contracts and out-of-contract rates matters for SMEs. Fixed offers currently provide budget certainty; rollover rates do not.

Analyst View

Market Interpretation

What the market is pricing now and what would trigger the next move.

What the Market Is Pricing Now

The market is pricing a diplomatic window with a military backstop. Brent at ~$106 and NBP at 120p reflect a view that talks might produce a deal but the risk of failure-and-escalation remains material. The divergence between Brent (up 0.69%) and WTI (down ~4%) on 21 May shows traders splitting on timing and regional impact.

The forward curve shape — near-term elevated, further-out cheaper — implies the market expects supply conditions to improve but not immediately. This is the "limbo" pricing environment.

Risk Premium: Stabilising or Softening?

The risk premium has softened from March-April peaks (Brent hit $138 in early April) but remains substantial. The IEA notes that "benchmark oil prices have posted wild swings in response to conflicting signals" and that North Sea Dated was around $110/bbl at time of writing.

The premium is now driven by uncertainty about timing rather than certainty of prolonged closure. That makes it more fragile — susceptible to both positive and negative headline shocks.

⬇️ What Would Trigger the Next Leg Down

  • Verified full reopening of Hormuz to all commercial traffic
  • Signed US-Iran agreement on nuclear programme and sanctions
  • Sustained increase in LNG cargo availability
  • EU storage refilling at or above seasonal norms
  • Forward market softening across 12m and 36m pricing

⬆️ What Would Trigger the Next Leg Up

  • Trump resuming military action after talks fail
  • Israel launching unilateral strikes on Iran
  • Hormuz closure extending into June or beyond
  • Houthi escalation closing Bab el-Mandeb further
  • Major energy infrastructure damage in the Gulf

Scenario Planning

Duration Scenarios

Three plausible disruption timelines and their implications for UK business energy procurement.

Short Disruption — 1 to 2 Weeks

Hormuz reopens to commercial traffic by early June. US-Iran deal signed. Gulf production ramps up quickly.

  • Brent: Falls toward $85-90/bbl as supply returns
  • TTF/NBP: Moderates but storage deficit keeps floor elevated
  • UK Power: Eases with gas but storage refill demand supports prices
  • Supplier books: Reprice downward within 1-2 weeks
  • Quote validity: Extends as volatility subsides
  • Customer action: Fixed offers from current suppliers may improve; compare before committing

Probability assessment: Low-to-moderate. A comprehensive deal in days looks optimistic given the gaps on nuclear programme, sanctions and Hormuz sovereignty.

Medium Disruption — 1 to 3 Months

Diplomatic limbo continues through June-July. Hormuz partially reopened under Iranian management. Gradual supply recovery.

  • Brent: $100-115/bbl range persists. EIA forecasts $106 May-June
  • TTF/NBP: Remains elevated through summer injection season
  • UK Power: Gas-led pricing keeps baseload above £90-100/MWh near-term
  • Supplier books: Continue repricing upward as elevated wholesale persists
  • Quote validity: Remains short due to ongoing uncertainty
  • Customer action: Fixing longer (36m) at current further-out pricing may provide budget certainty

Probability assessment: Moderate. This aligns with EIA assumptions (closed until late May, gradual recovery) and Goldman Sachs pushed Hormuz normalisation to end-June.

Severe Escalation — Beyond 3 Months / Regional Widening

Talks fail. Trump resumes military action. Israel acts unilaterally. Hormuz closed into Q3. Houthi escalation at Bab el-Mandeb.

  • Brent: World Bank upside scenario $115/bbl+; IEA warns of "protracted case" deficits
  • TTF/NBP: Could spike on winter storage fears and LNG competition
  • UK Power: Significant upside risk as gas sets marginal price
  • Supplier books: Rapid upward repricing. Some suppliers may withdraw fixed offers
  • Quote validity: Very short or suspended for volatile periods
  • Customer action: Customers without fixed contracts face severe exposure. Fixing before escalation is critical

Probability assessment: Low-to-moderate but tail risk is significant. Trump has shown willingness to escalate; Netanyahu prefers military pressure.

📊 Scenario Comparison with World Bank and EIA

The short disruption scenario is closest to the World Bank baseline (acute disruptions end in May). The medium scenario aligns with the EIA's May STEO (closed until late May, gradual recovery). The severe scenario approaches the World Bank upside case ($115/bbl Brent) and the IEA's "protracted case" with cumulative deficits twice the base case.

Current market pricing (Brent ~$106) sits between the medium and severe scenarios — suggesting traders assign meaningful probability to extended disruption.

Evidence That Would Reduce Risk
  • Verified full reopening of Hormuz to all commercial vessels, not just Chinese or approved traffic
  • Normal commercial traffic returning without selective passage, tolls, or Iranian management protocols
  • War-risk premiums falling materially across shipping insurance markets
  • Confirmed US-Iran signed agreement covering nuclear programme, sanctions relief and Hormuz access
  • US blockade of Iranian ports lifted

  • EU gas storage refilling at or above seasonal norms
⚠️
Evidence That Would Increase Risk
  • Trump announcing resumed military action after talks fail
  • Israel launching unilateral strikes on Iranian nuclear or military facilities
  • Iran attacking Gulf state energy infrastructure (UAE, Saudi Arabia, Qatar)
  • Houthi escalation closing Bab el-Mandeb further
  • Major damage to Hormuz transit infrastructure (mines, ship sinkings)
  • China entering conflict directly to protect energy supply lines
  • EU storage failing to refill before winter, forcing emergency measures
  • Supplier withdrawals of fixed offers due to extreme volatility

⚡ The Critical Distinction

Temporary diplomatic language is not enough. A Trump tweet about "progress" or a Gulf state expressing "optimism" does not reduce physical supply risk. What matters is: Are ships moving through Hormuz without restriction? Is LNG flowing normally? Are supplier prices falling? Is storage rebuilding? Until those physical and commercial indicators improve, headline relief should be treated as a potential procurement window — not proof the crisis is over.

Action Guidance

Commercial Takeaway for UK Businesses

Direct, practical guidance for Switch 365 Ltd customers and prospects.

⚡ The Core Message

Waiting is not neutral. It is a bet that the World Bank, the EIA, current market pricing, Gulf-state intervention, Trump's warning, the delayed military threat, Hormuz disruption, supplier price-book lag and the unresolved peace proposal are all overstating the risk.

Markets remain structurally volatile. The headline relief from Trump's paused attack has not normalised physical supply conditions. Hormuz is still closed. LNG shipping rates are still at crisis levels. European storage is still below seasonal norms. Supplier pricing is still trending upward.

The forward curve shows cheaper pricing further out — Summer 2027 gas at 88.07p is 30% below Jun-26 at 126.65p. Power shows the same shape: Summer 2027 at £76.52 vs Jun-26 at £104.58. If a customer's contract end date allows access to 2027 delivery, the market is offering a discount for patience — but only if the customer fixes now before supplier books reprice further.

For Customers Within 12 Months of Contract End Date

  • Review immediately. Do not wait for the geopolitical picture to clear.
  • Test available supplier pricing. Compare short-term and longer-term options.
  • Where pricing and risk profile support it, consider fixing now and fixing longer (36+ months).
  • The market further out may be cheaper than near-term pricing — this can strengthen the case for a longer-term fix.
  • Do not wait for perfect clarity. By the time clarity arrives, the window may have closed.

Why the World Bank Baseline Matters Even If You're Optimistic

The World Bank baseline already projects a 24% energy price rise in 2026 even assuming the major disruption ends in May. The EIA now assumes disruption lasts longer. Current Brent at ~$106 is already above the World Bank's $86 baseline.

Even in the optimistic case, energy buyers face higher prices. In the realistic case, they face significantly higher prices. The only way waiting makes sense is if you believe all major institutions are wrong and the disruption will end faster and cleaner than anyone expects. That is a speculative bet, not a procurement strategy.
