Why the Futures Market Matters

The futures market shows the price for energy delivered in future periods. A September 2027 customer is not only interested in today's price. They are interested in what the market is currently offering for September 2027 onwards.

If 2027, 2028 or 2029 delivery periods are cheaper than the current market, the customer may be able to use that future pricing now, depending on supplier availability and contract rules.

Cheaper further out does not mean cheaper later.

The risk with waiting is that those future periods move closer every day. When they become the near-term market, they can pick up current risk, supplier caution, non-commodity changes, weather pressure or geopolitical premium.

The future market is only useful while the customer can still buy that future delivery period. Waiting lets the market make the decision for the customer. This is not automatic advice to fix. It is a reason to review live prices now.

Supplier Price-Book Lag

Wholesale markets can move before supplier books. Supplier books may reprice after gas and power move. Quote validity can shorten. Fixed offers can be withdrawn. Today's gas bounce can reduce yesterday's opportunity.

Supplier price-book lag can work both ways. It can create a short opportunity after market relief, but it can also close quickly if the headline fails.
