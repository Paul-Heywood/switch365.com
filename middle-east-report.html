Forward Market and Backwardation Explained

What Is the Forward Market?

The forward market shows what gas or power is currently priced at for future delivery periods. That means a customer starting a new contract in 2028 may not be buying the same market as a customer renewing in 2026. The forward market turns timing into opportunity.

What Is Backwardation?

Backwardation means the near-term market is more expensive than the future market. If 2026 gas is around 127p/th and summer 2028 is around 75p/th, the customer can see that the future delivery period is cheaper than the near-term market. That does not mean wait. It means check whether that cheaper future period can be secured now.

The cheaper future price exists because it is still a future delivery period. Waiting until that period becomes the live market may remove the advantage. Backwardation is not a reason to wait. It is a reason to check whether the customer can act. The forward market turns timing into opportunity. Customers renewing now may be buying the expensive front of the market. Customers with later CEDs may be able to access a cheaper future market now.

Why the Futures Market Matters

The futures market shows the price for energy delivered in future periods. A September 2028 customer is not only interested in today's price. They are interested in what the market is currently offering for September 2028 onwards.

If 2028, 2029 or 2030 delivery periods are cheaper than the current market, the customer may be able to use that future pricing now, depending on supplier availability and contract rules.

The risk with waiting is that those future periods move closer every day. When they become the near-term market, they can pick up current risk, supplier caution, non-commodity changes, weather pressure or geopolitical premium.

Cheaper further out does not mean cheaper later. The future market is only useful while the customer can still buy that future delivery period. Waiting lets the market make the decision for the customer. This is not automatic advice to fix. It is a reason to review live prices now.

TTF / NBP Explained

TTF — The European Benchmark

TTF (Title Transfer Facility) is the main European wholesale gas benchmark, traded in the Netherlands. It reflects the price of gas for delivery across Europe. When LNG cargoes are disrupted, TTF moves because Europe competes for global LNG supply.

NBP — The UK Benchmark

NBP (National Balancing Point) is the main UK wholesale gas benchmark. It is the price that UK suppliers reference when building customer quotes. NBP can be influenced by TTF through LNG competition and interconnector flows.

Brent — Not Your Gas Quote

Brent is an oil benchmark, not a gas benchmark. While oil and gas prices can move together during geopolitical events, they are not the same market. Brent is not the customer's gas quote. LNG moves TTF, TTF can influence NBP, and NBP can move UK business gas prices.

The LNG Bridge

LNG is the physical link between Hormuz disruption and European/UK gas prices. When Hormuz is contested, LNG cargoes from Qatar and the UAE cannot flow normally. That tightens global LNG supply, which pushes TTF higher, which can pull NBP up with it.

Both US and Iran claim control over Hormuz. The reality is contested. The US Navy says the strait is open; Iran says it is closed. Ship-tracking data shows traffic is a fraction of pre-war levels (~40/day vs 125-140/day pre-war). Neither claim is fully credible.

Supplier Price-Book Lag

Wholesale markets can move before supplier books. Supplier books may reprice after gas and power move. Quote validity can shorten. Fixed offers can be withdrawn. Yesterday's softer market may already be gone. Today's gas move can reduce yesterday's opportunity.

The Opportunity

Supplier price-book lag can create a short window where current rates are still live even after wholesale markets have moved higher. For customers inside 90 days, this may be the last chance to secure before repricing.

The Danger

Once a supplier withdraws a rate, the customer cannot go back and buy yesterday's price. After last night's escalation, wholesale markets may have moved before supplier books have fully updated. That window can close without notice.

For 90-Day Customers

For customers inside 90 days, this is not only a market-risk issue. It is also an out-of-contract risk. If a new contract is not agreed in time, they may drift towards deemed, rollover or out-of-contract rates, which can be materially more expensive.

For Later-CED Customers

For customers with CEDs in 2028 or beyond, this is not only a risk issue. It may also be a forward-market opportunity. If the forward curve is cheaper in 2028/2029 and suppliers are willing to price it, they may secure a cheaper future delivery period now.

Supplier price-book lag is the opportunity and the danger. Wholesale markets move first. Supplier books often follow. Waiting for certainty can mean missing the price movement. These prices could be lost as fast as they were gained. Do not sell panic. Sell timing. Yesterday's market may already be disappearing. Supplier books may not have fully caught up yet, but after last night's escalation, we should assume rates can be pulled at short notice.

Impact on UK Business Energy Prices

Why UK Gas Reacts to Hormuz

UK gas reacts to NBP, which is linked to TTF through LNG competition. When Hormuz is contested, LNG cargoes from Qatar cannot flow normally. That tightens global LNG supply, which pushes TTF higher, which can pull NBP up. The UK does not need to buy that exact cargo — the global market tightens.

Why a Tanker Attack Matters

A UAE-associated tanker attack is stronger market evidence than political rhetoric. It directly affects shipping confidence, insurance premiums, and vessel routing decisions. Even if Hormuz is not fully closed, tanker attacks make shipowners cautious, which reduces traffic and tightens supply.

Why Brent Is Not Your Gas Quote

Brent is oil. Your business gas quote is built from NBP (UK wholesale gas) plus supplier costs, network charges, and margin. While oil and gas can move together during geopolitical events, they are not the same market. UK gas must be checked on its own.

Why Heat and NESO Matter for Power

During heatwaves, cooling demand rises while evening solar output falls. The system needs more flexible generation — usually gas-fired. Under marginal pricing, the most expensive unit needed to meet demand sets the price. That can lift UK power prices even when wholesale gas is stable.

Why 90 Days Is Critical

Customers inside 90 days of contract end face out-of-contract risk. If they don't secure a new contract before the current one ends, they may be placed on deemed or rollover rates — typically 30-50% more expensive than contracted rates.

Why Later CEDs May Be an Opportunity

Customers with CEDs in 2028 or beyond may be able to access cheaper future delivery periods now. The offered curve shows S-28 at 64.50p/th and S-29 at 59.00p/th — barely half the W-26 front price of 130.50p/th. Waiting does not protect that price.

If LNG cargoes are delayed, diverted or repriced, that can feed into TTF, NBP and UK power very quickly. If the customer can currently contract into a cheaper future market, waiting does not protect that price. Waiting simply moves the customer closer to delivery, where today's risk premium can become tomorrow's contract price. If supplier books have not fully caught up, the customer may have a short window where rates are still available. But once suppliers withdraw or reprice, that opportunity can disappear without notice. Because of marginal pricing, tight UK power conditions can pull more expensive generation into the stack and support higher electricity prices.
