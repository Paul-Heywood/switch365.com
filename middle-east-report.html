Executive Summary

What changed and why it matters for UK business energy buyers

Trump has paused a planned attack on Iran at the request of Gulf leaders. On 18 May, President Trump announced on Truth Social that Qatar, Saudi Arabia and the UAE had asked him to hold off a "scheduled" military attack planned for 19 May, to allow serious negotiations to continue. He instructed the US military to remain ready for a "full, large scale assault" at a moment's notice if an acceptable deal is not reached. He later told reporters the pause could be for "two or three days" because Gulf partners think they are close to a deal. (Verified: CBS, NPR, Axios, WSJ, Politico — all citing Trump's Truth Social post and White House statements)

This is not de-escalation. It is limbo. The Strait of Hormuz remains effectively closed. The EIA's May STEO now assumes Hormuz stays shut through late May, with production shut-ins peaking at 10.8 million barrels per day this month — worse than its April assumption of 9.1 mbd. The World Bank's baseline already forecasts energy prices surging 24% in 2026, with Brent at $86/bbl, and that baseline assumes the most acute disruptions end in May. Current Brent is trading around $109–110/bbl, well above the World Bank baseline. (Verified: EIA May STEO, World Bank Commodity Markets Outlook April 2026, Trading Economics)

The UAE's Barakah nuclear plant was struck by a drone on 17 May, an attack the UAE foreign ministry called a "treacherous terrorist attack" and a "dangerous escalation." Anwar Gargash, diplomatic adviser to the UAE president, stated the attack "whether carried out by the principal actor or through one of its proxies, represents a dangerous escalation." The IAEA confirmed normal radiation levels and emergency diesel generators powering Unit 3. (Verified: The National UAE, NPR, IAEA statements)

Israeli bombing in Lebanon continues despite the nominal ceasefire. The UK government's 8 May security bulletin confirmed that Lebanon was not part of the US-Iran ceasefire announced 7 April, and Israel retains the right to self-defence. Israeli strikes on 8 April ("Black Wednesday") killed at least 357 people in what the IDF called "Operation Eternal Darkness." Hezbollah has continued attacks on Israeli forces in southern Lebanon. (Verified: UK Gov, BBC, Reuters, ACLED, ISW-CTP)

Commercial bottom line: The Gulf-state intervention has created temporary market softness — Brent fell from ~$112 to ~$109 after Trump's announcement. But this softness is driven by diplomatic sentiment, not physical supply improvement. Hormuz remains restricted, Qatari LNG remains offline with force majeure declared, 20% of global LNG supply is cut off, and supplier price books may not have fully caught up with wholesale moves. For UK businesses within 12 months of contract end, this dip may represent a short procurement window before supplier repricing lands.

Ceasefire Reality Check

What the ceasefire says versus what is actually happening on the ground

  • US-Iran ceasefire announced 7 April 2026, originally for two weeks.
  • Trump extended the ceasefire indefinitely on 18 May at Pakistan's request.
  • Ceasefire covers US-Iran hostilities, but Lebanon is explicitly excluded.
  • Israel retains right to self-defence and continues operations in southern Lebanon.

What Actually Happened

  • Israeli bombing continued: "Black Wednesday" 8 April killed 357+ people in 100+ airstrikes. IDF dubbed it "Operation Eternal Darkness."
  • Hezbollah attacks continued: Hezbollah claimed 49 attacks on Israeli forces in Lebanon on 9–10 April alone.

Analyst Assessment

The ceasefire is operationally hollow. Trump delaying a strike at Gulf-state request does not make the ceasefire more durable — it makes it politically extended. The underlying conditions remain unresolved: Hormuz is still restricted, Qatari LNG is still offline, the US blockade continues, Hezbollah and Israel are still fighting, and Iran has not accepted the US proposal. Gulf-state intervention reduces immediate military risk for a matter of days, but it also prolongs uncertainty and keeps the market in limbo. The pause is conditional, time-limited, and reversible.

Dual Choke Point Assessment

A simultaneous disruption of Hormuz and Bab el-Mandeb would be catastrophic for UK energy markets. Europe relies on Bab el-Mandeb for Gulf oil and LNG imports, including from Qatar — Europe's second-largest LNG supplier. Qatar has already suspended LNG tanker transits through Bab el-Mandeb. If the Houthis act on their threat, European buyers would be forced to draw strategic reserves and compete intensely for alternative LNG cargoes, driving TTF and NBP prices significantly higher. The Cape of Good Hope route adds 4,000–6,000 nautical miles and up to 20 days to journeys, tightening global vessel availability and freight rates.

Supplier Price-Book Lag and Procurement Window

Why wholesale market movement may not yet be fully reflected in supplier pricing

The Lag Mechanism

  • 1. Wholesale moves first: TTF, NBP, and Brent respond in real-time to geopolitical developments.
  • 2. Supplier books lag: UK suppliers typically update price books on a cycle — daily, weekly, or when thresholds are breached.
  • 3. Quote validity shortens: During volatility, suppliers may reduce quote validity from 48 hours to 24 hours or less.
  • 4. Fixed offers withdrawn: At peak volatility (March–April), many suppliers withdrew fixed pricing entirely.
  • 5. Catch-up repricing: When suppliers do update, the move can be sudden and large, not gradual.

Current Assessment

Status: Possible Procurement Window

Brent dipped from ~$112 to ~$109 on Trump's pause announcement. TTF and NBP also softened slightly. However, this is headline-driven softness, not physical de-escalation. If supplier books have not fully caught up with the recent wholesale rally, customers may still be able to secure pricing before the next supplier update. If supplier books have already incorporated the higher wholesale levels, the window may be narrowing.

Risk: Window Closes Quickly

If talks fail and Trump resumes military preparations, or if Hormuz disruption extends into June (EIA's +$20/bbl scenario), wholesale prices could spike rapidly. Supplier books would then reprice higher, potentially withdrawing fixed offers again. The current softness is contingent on diplomatic optimism that may prove fleeting.

Commercial conclusion: If your business is within 12 months of contract end, this temporary market softness should be treated as a potential procurement window, not proof the crisis is over. Test available supplier pricing immediately. Where current forward pricing, supplier availability, and your risk profile support it, consider fixing now and fixing longer (36+ months) before further repricing lands.

Impact on UK Business Energy Prices

Why Middle East disruption flows through to UK business energy bills

The Transmission Chain

1. Hormuz Disruption
~20% of global oil and LNG flows restricted. Qatari LNG offline. Middle East production shut-ins at 10.8 mbd.
2. Global Price Spike
Brent rises 60%+ YTD. LNG prices in Asia and Europe surge. Competition for alternative cargoes intensifies.
3. UK Gas (NBP) Rises
UK imports ~50% of gas, much via LNG. Global LNG tightness pushes NBP higher. TTF-NBP arbitrage links UK to European gas.
4. UK Electricity Follows
Gas-fired power sets marginal price in UK. Wholesale electricity tracks gas. Business electricity now ~25.8p/kWh average.
5. Supplier Repricing
Suppliers pass through wholesale costs with a lag. Fixed contracts reprice when renewed. Pass-through contracts move immediately.

Why the World Bank Baseline Matters

The World Bank baseline already forecasts a 24% energy price surge in 2026, even assuming the major disruption ends in May and Hormuz shipping returns to normal by late 2026. This is not a worst-case scenario. It is the baseline.

If the EIA is correct that disruption lasts longer (through late May, possibly into June), the World Bank baseline becomes optimistic. If disruption extends beyond May, the World Bank's stress case — Brent up to $115/bbl — becomes more likely.

Current Brent at ~$110 is already above the World Bank baseline. This means the market is already pricing in more risk than the World Bank's base case assumes.

For UK businesses on flexible or pass-through contracts: You are already exposed to these wholesale moves. Every week of delay is a week of potential further price exposure.

For UK businesses on fixed contracts ending within 12 months: Your renewal price will be set by forward wholesale prices at the time of pricing, plus supplier margin. Those forward prices are currently elevated and could go higher.
