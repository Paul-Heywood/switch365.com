NESO and margin notices

“NESO is the National Energy System Operator. It watches the electricity system in real time and makes sure there is enough power available to meet demand. Marginal pricing and the heat

“Under marginal pricing, the market price can be influenced by the last and often more expensive unit of generation needed to meet demand. During heatwaves, cooling demand can rise while evening solar output falls, so the system may need more flexible generation. That can support higher UK power prices and balancing costs.” With Sizewell B (both units), Hartlepool 1 and Heysham 2-7 offline — and Torness 2 due off from 31 July — that marginal unit is usually gas.

Forward Market and Backwardation Explained

What the forward market is

“The forward market shows what gas or power is currently priced at for future delivery periods. That means a customer starting a new contract in 2027, 2028, 2029 or 2030 may not be buying the same market as a customer renewing in 2026.”

“Backwardation means the near-term market is more expensive than the future market.” Today's verified desk curve shows exactly that: Winter-26 gas at 138.75p/th versus Summer-27 at 95.25p/th and Summer-30 at 58.27p/th, and Winter-26 power at £116.25/MWh versus Summer-27 at £83.50/MWh and Summer-29 at £61.57/MWh.

Why 2027 is part of the story

“2027 matters because it shows whether value starts before the longer-dated 2028 / 2029 / 2030 opportunity.” On this curve it does: Summer-27 gas sits ~31% below Winter-26, and Summer-27 power ~28% below. The value story begins in 2027, not just in 2028.

“Backwardation is not a reason to wait. It is a reason to check whether the customer can act.”

Futures Market Explanation

Cheaper further out ≠ cheaper later

“Cheaper further out does not mean cheaper later.” A backwardated curve is a statement about today's relative prices, not a promise about tomorrow's. If the geopolitical risk premium bleeds further along the curve — exactly what a two-choke-point shock would do — the cheap seasons reprice upward first, because they are the ones with room to rise.

Future delivery is only useful while you can still buy it

“The future market is only useful while the customer can still buy that future delivery period.” Supplier appetite for future-start contracts depends on credit, usage profile and portfolio limits. None of those are guaranteed to be there next month.

Waiting lets the market decide

“Waiting lets the market make the decision for the customer.” A month ago NBP was ~31% lower. Customers who waited through June's ceasefire optimism are now looking at 130p+ gas — Winter-26 was offered at 138.75p on 17 July AM. The same logic applies to the cheap end of the curve.

Red Sea / Suez / SUMED Route Risk

The route map in one paragraph

“Bab el-Mandeb is the southern gate of the Red Sea route. Suez is the northern gate. Yanbu is Saudi's Red Sea pressure valve. SUMED is part of the workaround for crude that cannot move cleanly through Suez on fully laden VLCCs.” Saudi Arabia has already diverted around 70% of its energy exports through Yanbu, and the Red Sea route now carries roughly 7% of global energy supplies (Reuters, 16 July) — which is precisely why a Houthi threat to Bab el-Mandeb moves markets even before anything closes.

Why Suez is not a clean substitute

  • A VLCC is a Very Large Crude Carrier carrying around 2 million barrels — roughly 318 million litres. That volume is not practical to move by road.
  • Fully laden VLCCs are generally not a simple Suez Canal solution because of draught restrictions.
  • The workaround is not simple trucking. It is pipeline, lightering, partial offload, SUMED and vessel logistics — time, cost, capacity constraints and risk premium.
  • During the 2023–24 Houthi campaign, oil moving through Bab el-Mandeb fell from ~8.8m to ~4m barrels/day as majors rerouted around Africa (~14 extra days).
“Suez becomes more important, but it is not a clean replacement for Bab el-Mandeb.” “Fully laden VLCCs are not a simple Suez solution.” “The workaround adds friction, and friction gets priced.” “In a stressed market, friction is enough to support risk premium.”

Houthi / Saudi / UAE Claim Check

Mandatory correction discipline: separate the actors, separate the geography, separate confirmed attacks from claims.

Houthi ↔ Saudi — verified

Saudi warplanes struck Sanaa International Airport on 12 July; the Houthis launched missiles and drones at Abha International Airport on 13 July — the most significant Houthi–Saudi confrontation since the 2022 truce. On 16 July the Houthi leader said the group is ready to escalate against Saudi Arabia. The verified Houthi development is Saudi-related, not UAE-related. It still matters because it reopens Red Sea and Saudi-bypass risk while Hormuz is already under pressure.

UAE tankers — verified, different actor

The IRGC said it hit two UAE tankers in the Strait of Hormuz; the UAE confirmed an Indian crew member was killed and eight injured, calling the attack “brazen” (17 July). Separately, a vessel carrying US-branded cars from the UAE was hit near an Iraqi oil terminal. The UAE angle is connected to UAE-associated tankers near Hormuz and regional waters — it is not a confirmed Houthi attack on the UAE mainland.

The correction

“UAE was attacked by the Houthis” is not verified. Two separate risks are running at the same time: Houthi / Saudi / Red Sea risk and Iran / UAE tanker / Hormuz risk. Together they widen the energy-risk picture. Do not turn a Saudi Houthi attack into a UAE Houthi attack unless verified; do not turn a UAE-associated tanker attack into a direct UAE mainland attack unless verified. Unconfirmed reports of “booms” in Dubai on 16 July were denied by the Dubai Media Office.

Hormuz Controlled Corridor Check

Hormuz is being managed as a security corridor by both sides: the US blockades Iranian-linked shipping and escorts alternatives; Iran enforces its own route and attacks vessels that use routes it has not approved. That is not normality — it is contestation with traffic at crisis lows.

Corridor status — verified

  • US naval blockade of Iranian-linked shipping reimposed 14 July; enforcement includes disabling an unladen tanker (15 July) and boarding the M/T Wen Yao (16 July).
  • Iran declared the strait “closed until further notice” on 11 July and attacks vessels using the unapproved southern route along Oman — three vessels hit on 13 July, two ADNOC-operated (Mombasa B, Al Bahyah).
  • Traffic: 6 vessels on 13 July (five-week low), 9 on 15 July — versus ~100+ transits/day pre-war. No VLCCs and no LNG tankers visibly transited on 15 July (Kpler via Reuters).
  • Behavioural stress: some tanker captains refusing transits; most vessels dark or on Iranian routing (94% of March–May transits were outside the IMO lane — Kpler); India has told recruiters not to put Indian seafarers on Hormuz voyages.
  • UKMTO threat level “substantial” since late June; warnings cover projectiles, sea mines, GNSS interference and naval congestion.

What “open but not normal” means commercially

“Hormuz may be open, but open is not the same as normal.”
“If non-Iranian vessels need protected lanes while Iranian-linked vessels are blocked or restricted, the Strait is being managed as a security corridor.”
“The commercial issue is not only whether ships can pass. It is whether shipowners, insurers, LNG operators and suppliers trust the route enough to price it normally.”
“A protected route still carries risk premium.” “The market does not need full closure for prices to move.” “No VLCCs or LNG tankers moving through the Strait is not normal energy trade.”

Fee check (downgraded item): President Trump's 13 July proposal of a 20% “reimbursement” levy on cargo transiting Hormuz was reversed on 14 July, replaced with “Trade and Investment Deals” from Gulf states (Chatham House). Do not tell customers a 20% charge is being applied to their energy price — implementation is not verified. It remains useful only as background evidence that Hormuz has become a control, cost and security issue.

TTF / NBP Explained

TTF

“TTF is the European gas benchmark.” Priced in €/MWh out of the Netherlands, it clears the marginal molecule for continental Europe — which today means the marginal LNG cargo, because Russian pipe gas is gone and Qatari LNG is constrained by Hormuz.

NBP

“NBP is the UK gas benchmark,” priced in pence per therm. UK business gas contracts are built from it. NBP currently trades at a discount to TTF, so the IUK and BBL interconnectors are pulling gas out of the UK into Europe.

The chain to a UK bill

“LNG moves TTF, TTF can influence NBP, and NBP can move UK business gas prices.” Today's chain: Hormuz + Bab el-Mandeb risk → JKM at $19.93 and LNG scarcity → TTF €54.47 → NBP 131.98p with Winter-26 offered at 138.75p → supplier price books.

Supplier Price-Book Lag

The mechanic

“Supplier price-book lag is the opportunity and the danger.” “Wholesale markets move first. Supplier books often follow.” Supplier price books are refreshed on supplier schedules — daily for some, less frequently for others — and a market that has moved ~31% in a month, with Winter-26 up another 4.25p overnight to 138.75p, creates a wide gap between the live wholesale curve and rates still on the screen.

No supplier withdrawal or validity-shortening was publicly verifiable in this window, so this section is presented as commercial assessment, not as a verified supplier action. The wholesale evidence behind the assessment is fully verified.

What it means by customer position

  • Inside 90 days: act urgently. You are buying the front of a crisis curve; any rate still priced off older, lower wholesale marks can disappear on the next refresh. “Once a supplier withdraws a rate, the customer cannot go back and buy yesterday's price.”
  • Inside 12 months: review live options now. Compare like-for-like against today's curve, not against your renewal letter from June.
  • High-usage, outside 12 months: check future-start structures against the backwardated curve while supplier appetite and credit windows remain open.
“Waiting for certainty can mean missing the price movement.”
