Forward Market and Backwardation Explained

Why the futures market matters for your contract end date

The forward market shows what gas or power is currently priced at for future delivery periods. That means a customer starting a new contract in 2028 may not be buying the same market as a customer renewing in 2026.

What is Backwardation?

Backwardation means the near-term market is more expensive than the future market. If 2026 gas is around 110p and summer 2028 is around 69p, the customer can see that the future delivery period is cheaper than the near-term market. That does not mean wait. It means check whether that cheaper future period can be secured now.

Near-term market:
Win-26 gas ~110p/therm
Win-26 power ~£99/MWh

Future market:
Cal-28 gas ~69p/therm
Cal-28 power ~£67.51/MWh

The cheaper future price exists because it is still a future delivery period. Waiting until that period becomes the live market may remove the advantage. Backwardation is not a reason to wait. It is a reason to check whether the customer can act. The forward market turns timing into opportunity.

2028 / 2029 / 2030 Forward Value Check

Look beyond 2027. Check whether cheaper future delivery periods are available now.

Key question: If 2028 or 2029 is materially cheaper than the near-term market, customers with later CEDs may have a forward-market opportunity now. Not every customer can access it. Supplier appetite, credit, usage, start date and contract profile all matter. The correct action is to check, not guess.

Why the Futures Market Matters

The connection between forward curves and your contract decision

The futures market shows the price for energy delivered in future periods. A customer whose contract starts in September 2028 is not only interested in today’s price. They are interested in what the market is currently offering for September 2028 onwards.

If 2028, 2029 or 2030 delivery periods are cheaper than the current market, the customer may be able to use that future pricing now, depending on supplier availability and contract rules.

The risk with waiting is that those future periods move closer every day. When they become the near-term market, they can pick up current risk, supplier caution, non-commodity changes, weather pressure or geopolitical premium.

Cheaper further out does not mean cheaper later. The future market is only useful while the customer can still buy that future delivery period. Waiting lets the market make the decision for the customer. This is not automatic advice to fix. It is a reason to review live prices now.

Why usage threshold matters

A 1p movement on 1 million therms is £10,000. The same movement on 100,000 therms is £1,000. High usage amplifies every price point.

Why small forward movements matter

Even a small movement in 2028, 2029 or 2030 pricing can make a material difference across a high-usage contract.

Why the LOA is only a data check

The LOA does not switch the customer, commit the customer or agree a contract. It allows the consultant to confirm whether the opportunity applies.

Forward-thinking businesses may be able to secure a cheaper delivery period before it becomes the live market.

TTF / NBP Explained

Why Brent is not your gas quote

TTF (Title Transfer Facility) is the main European wholesale gas benchmark. NBP (National Balancing Point) is the main UK wholesale gas benchmark. They are not the same market, but they are closely linked because Europe and the UK compete for LNG cargoes and are connected through wider European gas flows.

Crude oil benchmark.
Not the customer’s gas quote.

NBP

Follows gas, carbon, weather.

LNG cargo availability affects TTF. TTF can influence NBP. NBP affects UK business gas pricing. Gas can influence UK power pricing. Forward NBP matters for future-start contracts.

Brent is not the customer’s gas quote. LNG moves TTF, TTF can influence NBP, and NBP can move UK business gas prices.

Supplier Price-Book Lag

The opportunity and the danger

Wholesale markets can move before supplier price books fully update. That can create a short window where current rates are still available, even though the wider market has already started moving. But when suppliers reprice, those rates can be withdrawn quickly and replaced with higher pricing.

The Opportunity:
Some rates may still be live before suppliers fully reprice. Customers may access pricing that does not yet reflect the full wholesale move.

The Danger:
That window may close this morning, later today, tomorrow, or without notice. Once suppliers withdraw rates, customers cannot go back and buy yesterday’s price.

Supplier price-book lag is the opportunity and the danger. Waiting for certainty can mean missing the price movement. These prices could be lost as fast as they were gained.

For customers inside 90 days, this is not only a market-risk issue. It is also an out-of-contract risk. For customers with later CEDs, this is not only a risk issue. It may also be a forward-market opportunity.

Customer-facing wording: “If your contract ends within 90 days, the issue is not whether the market might move later. The issue is whether supplier books move before you act.”

Internal closer wording: “Do not sell panic. Sell control. Supplier price-book lag means the customer may still have a short window, but once that window shuts, we cannot recreate it.”
