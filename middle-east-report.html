✓ What This Means

The futures market is the market price for energy delivered in a future period. When the near-term market is expensive but the further-out market is cheaper, it usually means today's price contains more immediate risk: supply pressure, shipping uncertainty, weather risk, storage concern and geopolitical premium.

For a customer whose contract starts 12 months from now, the relevant question is not only what energy costs today. It is what the market is offering for their delivery period. Summer 2027 gas at 85.64p/th is significantly below Winter 2026 at 119.84p/th. If that future period aligns with a customer's contract end date, they may be able to contract into that lower forward market now.

The key point: waiting does not mean today's cheaper future price becomes tomorrow's available price. As time passes, that future period becomes the near-term market. If the same risks are still present, or if supplier books reprice, that cheaper future price can disappear.

⚠ Why Waiting Can Be Expensive

Customers often assume that if the 12-month or 18-month market is cheaper today, they can wait and pick it up later. That is not how the market works. The future price is available because the customer is buying that future delivery period now. If the customer waits, the delivery period moves closer, current risk gets built into the price, and supplier books can change.

In simple terms: the cheaper forward market can become the expensive current market.

● Commercial Message for Customers

If your contract end date is far enough out, you may be able to contract into a market that is cheaper than the near-term market. That can support fixing longer where the price, contract date, usage profile and risk position make sense.

Do not let the market make the decision for you by holding off. Review the position now, test live supplier pricing, compare short-term and longer-term options, and decide while the forward market is still available.

Cheaper further out does not mean cheaper later.
The future market is only useful if you act while it is still future pricing.
Waiting means the customer stays exposed while today's future market becomes tomorrow's current market.
This is not automatic advice to fix. It is a reason to review now.

Supplier Price-Book Lag and Procurement Window

Why wholesale moves may not show in supplier pricing immediately

What Is Price-Book Lag?

Supplier price books are the internal pricing matrices that energy suppliers use to generate customer quotes. These books are typically updated on a schedule (daily, twice daily, or weekly) rather than in real-time. When wholesale markets move quickly, there can be a gap between the live wholesale price and the price a supplier offers to a customer.

Current Situation

This week saw sharp wholesale moves: gas down 3.9% on the week, power down 2.2%. These moves were driven by a record heatwave that will not persist. If supplier books have not fully caught up with these moves, customers may still be seeing elevated quotes that do not reflect the full wholesale easing.

✓ The Procurement Window

Customers may have a short window to secure pricing before supplier repricing lands. If wholesale prices have moved down but supplier books have not fully caught up, a customer requesting quotes now may see better pricing than they will next week after books update.

This window works in both directions. If geopolitical risk spikes and wholesale prices rise before books update, customers may briefly see cheaper pricing. But the current dynamic is: wholesale eased on weather, books may lag, customers can potentially lock in before books reprice higher on the underlying geopolitical risk.

⚠ Forward Pricing Can Be Lost

If the forward market is cheaper further out (Summer 2027 at 85.64p/th vs Winter 2026 at 119.84p/th), the customer should check whether their contract end date allows them to buy into that future period now. Waiting does not guarantee those cheaper future prices become available later. As time passes, that future period becomes the near-term market and may absorb the same risk premium.

Note: Supplier book lag is not fully verified from public sources, but it is a normal commercial feature of energy markets. Suppliers update pricing at different frequencies. This analysis is based on standard market behaviour, not specific supplier pricing evidence.

Impact on UK Business Energy Prices

Why global events flow through to UK business bills

Why UK Gas Reacts to Hormuz

The UK gets a significant portion of its gas via LNG. Qatar is a major supplier. With Ras Laffan damaged and Hormuz closed, LNG cargoes are diverted, delayed, or cancelled. The UK competes with Europe and Asia for the remaining supply, mostly from the US. This competition drives UK NBP prices higher. Even though the UK has North Sea production and Norwegian pipeline gas, LNG is the marginal source that sets the price.

Why Electricity Follows Gas

UK electricity pricing is dominated by gas-fired generation, which sets the marginal price in most settlement periods. When gas prices rise, electricity follows. Even with growing renewables (52% of generation in Q1 2026), gas still fills the gaps when wind and solar are low. Aurora Energy Research warns that prolonged Hormuz disruption could push UK winter power prices up 85% to £160/MWh.

Who Is Most Exposed

  • Businesses on variable or deemed rates
  • Customers within 12 months of renewal
  • High-consumption electricity users
  • Gas-intensive industries (manufacturing, hospitality, care)
  • Multi-site operators with staggered renewals
  • Customers on pass-through contracts

The Forward Market Opportunity

For customers whose contract end date falls in Summer 2027 or later, the forward market is currently offering significantly cheaper pricing than near-term markets. This is not a guarantee that those prices will be available later. It is a snapshot of what the market is offering today for that future delivery period. If the customer's contract timing allows, they may be able to lock in that cheaper forward pricing now.

● Why Waiting for Certainty Is Risky

If the World Bank baseline already points to materially higher energy prices in 2026 even assuming disruption eases, and the EIA now suggests disruption may last longer, while Hormuz remains restricted, Trump is warning Tehran that time is running out, and the forward market may be cheaper further out, waiting is not a neutral decision. It is a bet that all of these risk factors are being overstated.

For customers whose contract end date allows them to buy into that future period, this may strengthen the case for a longer-term fix. This is not automatic advice. It is a reason to review and test pricing now while that forward market is available.

⚠ Cheaper Further Out Does Not Mean Cheaper Later

The futures market allows customers to buy energy for a future delivery period. If that future delivery period is cheaper today, the customer may be able to access that pricing now if their contract timing allows it. But if the customer waits, that future period moves closer and can become the current market. If volatility, supply risk or supplier caution is still present, the cheaper price may disappear.

"Cheaper further out does not mean cheaper later. It means there may be an opportunity now, while that period is still priced as future delivery."
