NESO and marginal pricing — why tight systems cost more

NESO is the National Energy System Operator. It watches the electricity system in real time and makes sure there is enough power available to meet demand. A margin notice is not a blackout warning, but it does mean the system is tighter and NESO wants more available power.

Under marginal pricing, the market price can be influenced by the last and often more expensive unit of generation needed to meet demand. During heatwaves, cooling demand can rise while evening solar output falls, so the system may need more flexible generation. That can support higher UK power prices and balancing costs.

No verified new NESO electricity margin notice was found in the 17/07–20/07 window. The exposure remains: gas above 140p/th lifts the marginal megawatt-hour whenever gas sets the price.

Curve Education

Forward Market And Backwardation — Explained

The forward market shows what gas or power is currently priced at for future delivery periods. That means a customer starting a new contract in 2027, 2028, 2029 or 2030 may not be buying the same market as a customer renewing in 2026.

Backwardation means the near-term market is more expensive than the future market. Today's curve proves it. Winter-26 gas is offered at 141.50p/th while Summer-30 sits at 58.80p — the front costs almost two and a half times the 2030 summer. Power shows the same shape: £120.75/MWh at the front against £62.21 for Summer-29 (internal broker / supplier evidence, 20/07 AM). And S-28, S-29 and S-30 gas are at or below their levels of a year ago — the conflict premium is concentrated in the front of the curve.

2027 matters because it shows whether value starts before the longer-dated 2028 / 2029 / 2030 opportunity.
Backwardation is not a reason to wait. It is a reason to check whether the customer can act.
Cheaper further out does not mean cheaper later. It means there may be an opportunity now, while that period is still priced as future delivery.

The chart shows whether the market is backwardated. That matters because a customer renewing now may be exposed to the expensive front of the curve, while a customer with a later CED may be able to access a cheaper future delivery period now.

2027 matters because it shows whether value starts before the longer-dated 2028 / 2029 story. 2028, 2029 and 2030 matter because they show whether the future market still offers a lower-cost delivery opportunity.

Cheaper further out does not mean cheaper later. It means there may be an opportunity now, while that period is still priced as future delivery.

The mistake customers make is thinking cheaper 2027 / 2028 / 2029 pricing means they can wait. It does not. It means the cheaper part of the curve may be available now, and waiting gives the market more time to take it away.

Education

Futures Market — What It Means For A Business Customer

Cheaper further out does not mean cheaper later.

A futures price is a price you can act on today for delivery tomorrow. Once that delivery period becomes the live market, today's discount is gone.

The future market is only useful while the customer can still buy that future delivery period.

Access depends on supplier appetite, credit approval and usage profile — all three can tighten quickly in a repricing market.

Waiting lets the market make the decision for the customer.

Wholesale markets move first. Supplier books may follow quickly. Once suppliers withdraw rates, customers cannot go back and buy Friday's market.

Access is not automatic. Suppliers decide whether to price future-start contracts at all, and credit approval and usage profile both affect access. That is exactly why the check has to happen while the curve still offers the discount.

Route Risk

Red Sea / Suez / SUMED Route Risk

Bab el-Mandeb is the southern gate of the Red Sea route. Suez is the northern gate. Yanbu is Saudi's Red Sea pressure valve. SUMED is part of the workaround for crude that cannot move cleanly through Suez on fully laden VLCCs.

Current status

  • Saudi diversion is real: Riyadh is rerouting crude from the Gulf to the Red Sea via its East–West pipeline, shipping from Yanbu; other products are trucked across the desert to Red Sea ports. (Reuters / FDD)
  • Suez operating normally; northern Red Sea at MODERATE with periodic electronic interference reported near Yanbu. (JMIC)
  • Houthi–Saudi friction is rising: Saudi airstrike on Sanaa airport (13/07), Houthi missiles at Saudi Arabia, threats to "all Saudi oil facilities" — the bypass route itself carries escalation risk. (France 24 / FDD)
  • Vessel seized off Yemen by armed men last week — security on the Red Sea side is degrading. (Reuters)

VLCC / ULCC reality — why Suez is not a clean fix

  • A VLCC (Very Large Crude Carrier) typically carries ~2 million barrels (~318 million litres); larger units reach 2.1–2.2 million barrels depending on size, crude density and draught.
  • A ULCC (Ultra Large Crude Carrier) carries ~3–3.7 million barrels (~477–588 million litres).
  • A fully laden VLCC is not a clean Suez solution — canal draught limits mean deep-laden supertankers must lighten first. A ULCC is even less of a clean Suez solution.
  • The workaround is not simple trucking — the cargo is far too large to move practically by road (the truck example shows scale only). It is pipeline, lightering, partial offload, SUMED and vessel logistics. Suez and SUMED can help, but they add friction — and in a stressed market, friction is enough to support risk premium.
Suez becomes more important, but it is not a clean replacement for Bab el-Mandeb.
Yanbu matters because Saudi crude can be moved to the Red Sea side. SUMED matters because fully laden VLCCs are not a simple Suez solution.
The workaround adds friction — time, cost, capacity limits and timing risk — and friction gets priced.

Explainer

TTF / NBP Explained

The benchmarks

TTF is the European gas benchmark. NBP is the UK gas benchmark. TTF trades in euros per megawatt-hour; NBP in pence per therm. They price the same global LNG-tight, storage-hungry market through different delivery points.

The transmission chain

LNG moves TTF, TTF can influence NBP, and NBP can move UK business gas prices. With Qatari and UAE LNG trapped behind Hormuz, every European cargo competes harder — that competition is why a Gulf shipping story lands in a UK business gas quote within days.

LNG Risk: Current Shipping Evidence And Relevant Background

Current verified evidence

  • No LNG carrier has transited Hormuz since 11/07 (last: Adnoc's Al Hamra, laden from Das Island). (Kpler)

  • June's recovery has reversed: 40 LNG transits in June vs 8 in May and zero in March — July is tracking back toward crisis lows. (Kpler)

Why UK customers should care

Hormuz is the only maritime export route for Qatari LNG and critical for UAE LNG. Europe sources roughly an eighth of its LNG from Qatar, and EU storage is only ~53% full against a ~69% seasonal norm — every delayed Gulf cargo tightens the Europe–Asia competition that sets TTF and then NBP.

If Bab el-Mandeb is also disrupted, Red Sea rerouting, insurance and Suez confidence all tighten simultaneously — LNG shipping risk would no longer have a clean alternative corridor.

Oil is the headline. LNG is the customer risk.

Supplier Price-Book Lag

Supplier price-book lag is the opportunity and the danger.
Wholesale markets move first. Supplier books often follow.
Waiting for certainty can mean missing the price movement.
Once a supplier withdraws a rate, the customer cannot go back and buy Friday's price.

Wholesale gas has risen ~37% in a month and ~16% in the last week alone; Monday added another leg — internal offered ranges show W-26 gas at 141.50p (+2.75) and W-26 power at £120.75 (+£4.50) on Monday AM, with oil and TTF up again.
