Why the Futures Market Matters

The futures market shows the price for energy delivered in future periods. A September 2027 customer is not only interested in today's price. They are interested in what the market is currently offering for September 2027 onwards.

If 2027, 2028 or 2029 delivery periods are cheaper than the current market, the customer may be able to use that future pricing now, depending on supplier availability and contract rules.

The risk with waiting is that those future periods move closer every day. When they become the near-term market, they can pick up current risk, supplier caution, non-commodity changes, weather pressure or geopolitical premium.

Cheaper further out does not mean cheaper later.

The future market is only useful while the customer can still buy that future delivery period.

Waiting lets the market make the decision for the customer.

This is not automatic advice to fix. It is a reason to review live prices now.

TTF / NBP Explained

TTF stands for Title Transfer Facility. It is based in the Netherlands and is the main European wholesale gas benchmark. NBP stands for National Balancing Point. It is the main UK wholesale gas benchmark. They are not the same market, but they are closely linked because the UK and Europe compete for the same LNG cargoes and are connected through European gas infrastructure.

TTF (Europe)

Netherlands-based hub. Main European gas benchmark. Driven by LNG arrivals, storage levels, Norway flows, and Asian cargo competition.

NBP (UK)

UK virtual trading point. Main UK gas benchmark. Driven by TTF linkage, LNG arrivals, domestic production, and interconnector flows.

LNG Connection

Both markets draw from the same global LNG pool. When Asian buyers pay more, Europe gets fewer cargoes. TTF moves first, NBP follows.

Power Link

Gas still sets the marginal price for UK electricity. When gas rises, power tends to follow. NBP movements flow through to UK power quotes.

Key Sales Message

Brent is not the customer's gas quote. LNG moves TTF, TTF can influence NBP, and NBP can move UK business gas prices.

LNG Risk: IGU, Shell and QatarEnergy

IGU

IGU — Record Trade, Clouded Outlook

The International Gas Union published its report on 7 July 2026. Global LNG trade rose 6.3% to 436.98 million metric tons in 2025 — the fastest growth since 2022. The US remained the largest exporter at 110.74M tonnes, followed by Qatar (81.51M) and Australia (80.32M).

Europe recorded the largest import increase — up 26.1 million tons to 126.2 million tons — as it replenished inventories and replaced lower Russian gas flows. Asia Pacific remained the largest importing region at 168.7M tonnes.

However, IGU President Andrea Stegher warned: "The conflict in the Gulf has damaged LNG infrastructure, clouded the outlook for the region's expansion projects, and exposed Asian buyers to flow uncertainty and higher prices." The IGU said conflict in the Middle East could lead to a contraction in 2026.

IGU World LNG Report | Reuters 7 Jul 2026
SHEL

Shell — Hormuz Disruption Warning

Shell warned on 30 June 2026 that severe disruption to shipping through the Strait of Hormuz has shut in around one fifth of the world's monthly LNG supply since the conflict began. A total of 422 million tonnes of LNG was traded in 2025.

Shell said 2026 LNG trade could be similar to 2025 if shipping through Hormuz returns to normal this summer, with growth resuming in 2027. New North American liquefaction, better plant performance and lower Asian imports helped offset reduced Middle East supply.

Shell's warning is not new today, but it remains commercially relevant because it links Hormuz directly to LNG availability, TTF, NBP and UK power. Shell and IGU now support the same sales message.

Shell LNG Outlook 2026 | Reuters 30 Jun 2026
QE

QatarEnergy — Bangladesh Delivery Cuts

QatarEnergy has halved its scheduled LNG deliveries to Bangladesh for 2026, Petrobangla's acting chairman confirmed on 6 July. The supply cut slashes Bangladesh's scheduled 2026 deliveries from 40 cargoes down to 20.

QatarEnergy's force majeure has been extended until 16 July. Bangladesh has been forced to import 35 spot cargoes since March to replace lost long-term supply. The LNG subsidy burden has nearly tripled to Tk16,600 crore.

This is Shell's warning in real life: disrupted LNG routes force buyers into the spot market. Spot replacement buying increases cargo competition, which can support TTF, NBP and UK power prices.

Reuters 6 Jul 2026 | Petrobangla | TBS News

Combined LNG Assessment

These are three separate but connected points. IGU shows the global LNG market risk. Shell shows the Hormuz supply shock. QatarEnergy delivery cuts show the disruption is operational, not theoretical. Together, they explain why UK gas can remain exposed even when oil looks calmer.

Oil is calmer, but LNG is the reason gas is not safe.
