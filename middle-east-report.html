↺Forward Market and Backwardation Explained

The forward market shows what gas or power is currently priced at for future delivery periods. That means a customer starting a new contract in 2027, 2028, 2029 or 2030 may not be buying the same market as a customer renewing in 2026.

Backwardation means the near-term market is more expensive than the future market. When the market is backwardated, a customer with a later contract end date (CED) may be able to access cheaper future delivery periods than a customer renewing now at the expensive front of the curve.

?Futures Market Explanation

Cheaper further out does not mean cheaper later. The future market is only useful while the customer can still buy that future delivery period. Waiting lets the market make the decision for the customer.

Forward curves change. A backwardated market today can flatten or go into contango tomorrow if the near-term risk premium collapses or if future supply concerns emerge. The key question for any customer is not what the curve says today in isolation, but where their CED sits on that curve and whether they can act before the curve moves.

A customer with a CED in late 2027 or 2028 who waits until their renewal window opens may find that the cheap future period they saw on today's curve has become the expensive front period by the time they are ready to buy. The opportunity is in securing the cheaper future delivery now, while it is still forward-dated, not in waiting for it to become the live market.

★High-Usage Customers Outside 12 Months

Outside 12 months usually means monitor. Outside 12 months with high usage means check the forward curve.

High-usage customers with CEDs beyond the normal 12-month procurement window should not simply wait. If the forward curve shows material backwardation into 2028, 2029 or 2030, a Letter of Authority (LOA) can be used to check whether suppliers are pricing those future delivery periods at a discount to today's front market.

The LOA does not switch the customer, commit the customer or agree a contract. It allows the consultant to confirm whether the opportunity applies. For multi-site, high-consumption businesses, the volume can make even a modest per-unit saving material at scale.

The commercial risk is not only the fee itself. It is the wider signal that Hormuz has become a control, cost, insurance and shipping-confidence issue. ★TTF / NBP Explained

TTF — Title Transfer Facility

TTF is the European gas benchmark, traded on ICE. It represents gas delivered at the virtual trading point in the Netherlands. TTF prices reflect continental European supply-demand balance, LNG import competition, storage levels, and Norwegian pipeline flows. When LNG is disrupted, TTF rises because Europe loses a major supply source and must compete more aggressively for alternative cargoes.

NBP — National Balancing Point

NBP is the UK gas benchmark. It reflects gas delivered at the UK virtual trading point. The UK receives gas from Norwegian pipelines (largest source), UK Continental Shelf production, LNG imports via South Hook/Dragon/Milford Haven, and inter-connectors from Belgium and the Netherlands. NBP can be influenced by TTF through interconnector arbitrage, but has its own dynamics around LNG arrivals and UK storage.

LNG moves TTF, TTF can influence NBP, and NBP can move UK business gas prices. The transmission mechanism is: Hormuz disruption → Qatar/UAE LNG stranded → Asian competition for cargoes intensifies → European LNG arrivals fall → TTF rises → NBP follows → UK supplier books reprice.

🚢LNG Risk: IGU, Shell, QatarEnergy and Tankers Turning Back

Oil is the headline. LNG is the customer risk.

The Strait of Hormuz is the only maritime export route for Qatari LNG cargoes destined beyond the Middle East Gulf. Approximately 93% of Qatar's and 96% of UAE's LNG exports transit through the Strait, representing roughly 19-20% of global LNG trade. When Hormuz becomes a controlled security corridor rather than a normal shipping route, that LNG is effectively stranded or severely delayed.

QatarEnergy Disruption

Al Rekayyat (Qatar-flagged LNG) struck July 7. QatarEnergy tankers Al Rayyan and GasLog Shanghai entered Gulf July 9. No LNG carrier has exited since July 11. Two vessels remain berthed at Ras Laffan.

European Impact

European LNG arrivals forecast to fall to lowest since Sep 2024. Storage ~50% full, below seasonal average. Asian July LNG imports at 6-month high. Cargo competition intensifying.

Bank of America analysis (March 2026): Each month of lost LNG supply from Qatar and UAE amounts to roughly 10% of total European gas storage. Around 10 weeks of lost Gulf LNG supply could drive Q1-27 TTF prices above 2022 highs. BofA expected TTF to average €50/MWh in 2026 contingent on limited disruption — current prices (€55+) already exceed that.

Shell and IGU warnings remain relevant. The International Gas Union and major LNG traders including Shell have warned that prolonged Hormuz disruption would have severe consequences for global LNG supply chains. With 21 LNG carriers stuck inside the Gulf and no exits since July 11, those warnings are now being realised.

?Ceasefire / Talks Reality Check

Talks being alive is better than collapse, but no breakthrough is not implementation. Implementation matters more than political wording. Shipping confidence matters more than social media claims.

The US-Iran Memorandum of Understanding of 17 June 2026 reopened Hormuz for 60 days and began a negotiating period. That ceasefire broke down decisively after the July 7 attacks on commercial vessels. The US has now resumed strikes and reimposed its naval blockade. Iran has responded with retaliation claims and threats to close Hormuz again.

Chatham House assessment: The only viable solution to restoring shipping remains a negotiated settlement — a prospect made more difficult by renewed military conflict. China's pressure in favour of restoring maritime freedoms could be significant but has not materialised decisively. Europe, with UK leadership, could play a role in securing freedom of navigation once a deal is done.

For commercial purposes, the key metric is not whether diplomats are talking but whether shipowners, insurers and LNG operators have enough confidence to transit Hormuz without naval escort or war-risk premiums. Currently, they do not.

⏰Supplier Price-Book Lag

Supplier price-book lag is the opportunity and the danger. Wholesale markets move first. Supplier books often follow. Waiting for certainty can mean missing the price movement. Once a supplier withdraws a rate, the customer cannot go back and buy yesterday's price.

Energy suppliers typically hedge their exposure in wholesale markets before offering fixed prices to customers. When wholesale markets move sharply higher, suppliers may withdraw quotes, shorten validity windows, or reprice before customers can act. This creates a lag where the customer's available retail price may still reflect yesterday's wholesale market while today's market has already moved.

★Market Interpretation

The market has not normalised. Hormuz may be open in places, but the evidence points to controlled disruption, not normal trade. If non-Iranian Gulf energy cargoes need protected or managed lanes, and Iranian-linked vessels are being blocked, restricted or treated differently, Hormuz is functioning as a managed security corridor. That keeps risk premium in oil, LNG, gas, power and supplier pricing.

"Hormuz open" does not mean "Hormuz normal." A protected lane is not normal trade. A controlled shipping corridor still carries risk premium. The market does not need Hormuz to be fully closed for prices to move. Shipping confidence matters as much as physical movement. No VLCCs or LNG tankers moving through the Strait is a stronger market signal than political wording.

The customer decision is not only about today's headline. It is about where the customer's CED sits on the curve, whether supplier books have caught up, and how quickly the current window can close. Oil is the headline, but LNG, gas and power are the customer risk.
