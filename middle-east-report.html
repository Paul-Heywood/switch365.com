? What This Means

The futures market is the market price for energy delivered in a future period. When the near-term market is expensive but the further-out market is cheaper, it usually means today's price contains more immediate risk: supply pressure, shipping uncertainty, weather risk, storage concern or geopolitical premium.

For a customer whose contract starts 12 months from now, the relevant question is not only what energy costs today. It is what the market is offering for their delivery period. If that future period is cheaper, the customer may be able to contract into that lower forward market now.

The key point: waiting does not mean today's cheaper future price becomes tomorrow's available price. As time passes, that future period becomes the near-term market. If the same risks are still present, or if supplier books reprice, that cheaper future price can disappear.

! Why Waiting Can Be Expensive

Customers often assume that if the 12-month or 18-month market is cheaper today, they can wait and pick it up later. That is not how the market works. The future price is available because the customer is buying that future delivery period now. If the customer waits, the delivery period moves closer, current risk gets built into the price, and supplier books can change.

In simple terms, the cheaper forward market can become the expensive current market.

£ Commercial Message for Customers

If your contract end date is far enough out, you may be able to contract into a market that is cheaper than the near-term market. That can support fixing longer where the price, contract date, usage profile and risk position make sense.

Do not let the market make the decision for you by holding off. Review the position now, test live supplier pricing, compare short-term and longer-term options, and decide while the forward market is still available.

Cheaper further out does not mean cheaper later.
The future market is only useful if you act while it is still future pricing.
Waiting means the customer stays exposed while today's future market becomes tomorrow's current market.
This is not automatic advice to fix. It is a reason to review now.

Ceasefire Reality Check

What the ceasefire actually means on the ground.

📋What the Ceasefire Says

The two-week ceasefire agreed 8 April 2026 was extended indefinitely by Trump on 21 April. It covers US-Iran hostilities but does not include Lebanon, where Israel-Hezbollah fighting continues. The framework calls for halting hostilities, reopening Hormuz, and a 15-20 day negotiation period.

Key sticking point: nuclear enrichment. Trump demands no enrichment and removal of all "deeply buried nuclear dust." Iran's 10-point counter-proposal demands war reparations, sanctions lifting, and sovereignty over Hormuz management.

⚠What Is Excluded or Disputed

  • • Lebanon is not included — Trump confirmed: "Because of Hezbollah. They were not included in the deal."
  • • Israeli bombing continues — strikes on Iranian and Iran-linked targets persist
  • • US defensive strikes ongoing — CENTCOM hit missile sites 25 May
  • • Nuclear terms unresolved — Trump seeking amendments; Iran in "caves," response delayed
No major escalation is not the same as real de-escalation. The absence of a full-scale US attack does not mean the crisis is resolved. Hormuz remains restricted, military assets remain deployed, and the core dispute over Iran's nuclear programme is still being negotiated.

🎯Commercial Interpretation

Trump delaying a strike means the military option is deferred, not cancelled. Gulf-state mediation reduces immediate risk but prolongs uncertainty. The market is in limbo: soft enough to create a potential procurement window, but volatile enough that the window can close quickly if talks fail.

For UK businesses, this means: treat current market softness as a potential procurement window, not proof the crisis is over. If a deal is signed and Hormuz reopens fully, prices could soften further. If talks fail and strikes resume, prices can spike rapidly. Either outcome favours having a procurement plan in place.

Supplier Price-Book Lag and Procurement Window

Why wholesale movements may not immediately appear in supplier pricing — and what that means for customers.

⏳Current Situation

Wholesale gas prices have softened approximately 5.7% over the past week. Day-ahead NBP is down from recent peaks. Summer 2027 forward contracts remain significantly cheaper than near-term delivery.

However, supplier price-book movement is not fully verified from public sources. This is normal: suppliers typically update pricing based on averaged or lagged wholesale inputs, and may maintain cautious premiums during periods of geopolitical uncertainty even as wholesale spot prices ease.

Supplier book lag is a normal commercial risk when wholesale markets move quickly. When wholesale falls rapidly, there may be a short window where supplier pricing has not yet caught up. When wholesale rises rapidly, supplier pricing may jump ahead of customer expectations.

🎯What This Means Commercially

  • • If supplier books have not caught up with wholesale softening, customers may have a short window to secure pricing before repricing lands
  • • If supplier books have already caught up, the window may have narrowed or closed
  • • Quote validity may shorten in volatile conditions — fixed offers can be withdrawn with limited notice
  • • Forward market pricing (12-36 months) may still offer value even if near-term supplier pricing has adjusted
Customers may have a short window to secure pricing before supplier repricing lands. This is not a guarantee of savings. It is a reason to test pricing now rather than assume the market will wait.

⚠Why Forward Pricing Can Be Lost

A cheaper 12-month or 18-month market today may not be available later because:

  1. The delivery period moves closer and picks up current risk premium
  2. Supplier books reprice to reflect changed wholesale conditions
  3. Geopolitical events can reverse market softness instantly
  4. Quote validity periods expire, and new quotes reflect new market levels

The futures market is only useful while the customer can still buy that future delivery period. Waiting simply moves the customer closer to delivery, where today's risk premium can become tomorrow's contract price.

UK Business Context

Impact on UK Business Energy Prices

Why global developments translate directly into UK business energy bills.

💵Why UK Gas Reacts to LNG and European Storage Risk

The UK imports approximately 50% of its gas, with LNG making up a growing share. When Hormuz disruption constrains Middle Eastern LNG exports, the UK must compete with Asia for alternative supplies. This competition drives up the global LNG price, which feeds directly into NBP (UK gas benchmark) pricing.

European gas storage is a key buffer, but refilling rates depend on affordable LNG imports. If Hormuz constraints persist through summer, European storage may not reach comfortable levels before winter, keeping prices elevated through Q4 2026 and Q1 2027.

⚡Why Electricity Follows Gas

Despite growing renewables, gas-fired power plants still set the marginal price for UK electricity during most hours. When gas prices rise, electricity prices follow. Even with strong wind and solar output, gas is the backup that kicks in when renewables dip.

Carbon allowance prices add another layer. At €76.90/t, carbon provides independent support to power prices even when gas softens. This explains why power has "decoupled" from gas weakness in recent sessions.

Commercial Action

Customer Action — Commercial Takeaway

Direct guidance for UK businesses and SMEs reviewing energy procurement.

⚠ For UK Business Customers

Waiting is not neutral. It is a bet that the World Bank, the EIA, current market pricing, Gulf-state intervention, Trump's warning, the delayed military threat, Hormuz disruption, supplier price-book lag and the unresolved peace proposal are all overstating the risk.

Here is what the current evidence says:

  • The World Bank baseline already forecasts +24% energy prices in 2026 even if disruption eases in May
  • The EIA assumes disruption lasts longer than the World Bank, making the WB baseline look optimistic
  • Hormuz is selectively open to Chinese vessels under IRGC management — this is not normalisation
  • Gulf states have asked Trump not to attack Iran, which may reduce immediate risk but leaves the market in limbo
  • Trump has delayed, not cancelled, a planned strike. The military option remains live
  • A deal appears closer but is not agreed. Amendments are still being negotiated
  • Wholesale markets may have moved before supplier books fully caught up
  • The market further out (12-36 months) is cheaper than near-term pricing

For customers within 12 months of contract end date, the commercially sensible step is to review immediately, test available supplier pricing, compare short-term and longer-term pricing, and where pricing and risk profile support it, consider fixing now and fixing longer (36+ months) before supplier books move further.

Cheaper further out does not mean cheaper later. It means there may be an opportunity now, while that period is still priced as future delivery. If the customer can currently contract into a cheaper future market, waiting does not protect that price. Waiting simply moves the customer closer to delivery, where today's risk premium can become tomorrow's contract price.

This is guidance Switch 365 Ltd can use with its customers. It is not advice to Switch 365 Ltd about its own energy procurement. Every customer's situation is different: contract end date, consumption profile, risk tolerance, and budget constraints all matter. The right approach is to review the position now while options are available, not to wait for perfect clarity that may never arrive.
