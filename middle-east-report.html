Market Further Out

Is the market cheaper further out? Yes — and that matters for procurement decisions.

UK NBP Gas Forward Curve (p/therm) — Based on ICE settlement data and Argus assessments. Argus Media ICE

WHAT THIS MEANS

The futures market is the price for energy delivered in a future period. When near-term is expensive but further-out is cheaper, it means today's price contains more immediate risk — supply pressure, shipping uncertainty, geopolitical premium. For a customer whose contract starts 12 months from now, the relevant question is what the market is offering for their delivery period.

WHY WAITING CAN BE EXPENSIVE

Customers often assume that if the 12-month market is cheaper today, they can wait and pick it up later. That is not how the market works. The future price is available because you are buying that future delivery period now. If you wait, the delivery period moves closer, current risk gets built into the price, and supplier books can change. The cheaper forward market can become the expensive current market.

COMMERCIAL MESSAGE

If your contract end date is far enough out, you may be able to contract into a market that is cheaper than near-term pricing. That can support fixing longer where the price, contract date, usage profile and risk position make sense. Do not let the market make the decision for you by holding off. Review the position now.

Key Point — Cheaper Further Out Does Not Mean Cheaper Later

The futures market allows customers to buy energy for a future delivery period. If that future period is cheaper today, the customer may be able to access that pricing now if their contract timing allows it. But if the customer waits, that future period moves closer and can become the current market. If volatility, supply risk or supplier caution is still present, the cheaper price may disappear. Waiting does not protect that price. Waiting simply moves you closer to delivery.

Supplier Price-Book Lag and Procurement Window

Why wholesale moves and supplier pricing don't always align

How Price-Book Lag Works

Energy suppliers build their customer pricing from wholesale market data, but there is always a lag. When markets are volatile, this lag can stretch from hours to days or even weeks. Suppliers may:

  • Use averaged wholesale data over a period (e.g., 5-day or 30-day average)
  • Add a risk premium during volatile periods
  • Shorten quote validity to limit exposure
  • Withdraw fixed offers entirely when uncertainty is high
  • Update books on a schedule (daily, weekly) rather than continuously

This means wholesale prices can move significantly before supplier books reflect the change. In the current environment, with Brent implied volatility at 78%, this lag is a real commercial factor.

Current Assessment

Wholesale movement: Markets have softened from recent peaks. Brent down 6.24% over the month. NBP and TTF both easing. This should, in theory, create some room for supplier pricing to improve.

Supplier books: Not fully verified from public sources. Normal commercial behaviour suggests books may not have fully caught up. Suppliers are likely maintaining cautious pricing given the ongoing uncertainty (Trump's "clock ticking", Hormuz restricted, LNG offline).

Quote validity: Risk of shortening. In volatile markets, 30-day validity can shrink to 7-14 days. Customers should confirm validity periods when receiving quotes.

Procurement Window Assessment

Customers may have a short window to secure pricing before supplier repricing fully lands. If wholesale has softened but books haven't caught up, there may be an opportunity. Conversely, if talks fail and wholesale spikes, books will reprice upwards quickly. The direction of the next move is uncertain, but the risk of waiting is clear: you are exposed to whatever the market does next, and the forward market (Cal-27, Cal-28) is currently cheaper than near-term pricing. Supplier book lag is not fully verified from public sources, but it is a normal commercial risk when wholesale markets move quickly.

Impact on UK Business Energy Prices

Why global developments reach your energy bill

The Transmission Chain

UK business energy prices are not set in a vacuum. The chain from Hormuz to your bill works like this:

  1. Hormuz disruption reduces global oil and LNG supply
  2. Brent crude rises on supply fear (currently +52% YoY)
  3. LNG cargoes are diverted to highest bidders — Asia competes with Europe

Guidance for UK businesses and SMEs

What UK Business Customers Should Do Now

Waiting Is Not Neutral

Waiting is not neutral. It is a bet that the World Bank, the EIA, current market pricing, Gulf-state intervention, Trump's warning, the delayed military threat, Hormuz disruption, supplier price-book lag and the unresolved peace proposal are all overstating the risk. That bet might be right. But if it is wrong, the cost of waiting is materially higher energy bills, reduced supplier options, and the loss of access to currently cheaper forward pricing.

For Customers Within 12 Months of Renewal
  • Review immediately. Do not wait for perfect clarity.
  • Test live supplier pricing. Compare quotes from multiple suppliers.
  • Compare short-term vs longer-term options. Cal-27 and Cal-28 may be cheaper.
  • Consider fixing longer (36+ months) where pricing and risk profile support it.
  • Check quote validity periods. These may be shorter than usual.
  • Understand your usage profile. High gas users face different risk than high electricity users.
Why the Futures Market Matters

The futures market allows customers to buy energy for a future delivery period. If that future delivery period is cheaper today, the customer may be able to access that pricing now if their contract timing allows it. But if the customer waits, that future period moves closer and can become the current market.

Cheaper further out does not mean cheaper later. It means there may be an opportunity now, while that period is still priced as future delivery. Waiting simply moves the customer closer to delivery, where today's risk premium can become tomorrow's contract price.

This is not automatic advice to fix. It is a reason to review now, test pricing, compare options, and make an informed decision while the forward market is still available.

High gas users (manufacturing, hospitality, care, food processing): Most directly exposed to NBP movements. Review immediately. Consider longer fixes if Cal-27/28 pricing is favourable.

High electricity users (retail, offices, data centres): Power follows gas. Same logic applies. Summer 2027 forward at GBP 74.86/MWh may be attractive vs current elevated levels.

Multi-site operators: Complexity compounds. Start with sites closest to renewal. Staggered approach may capture different forward periods.

SMEs on variable/rollover rates: Most exposed to immediate movements. Fixing provides budget certainty. Even a 12-month fix removes short-term volatility risk.

Charities, churches, community organisations: Budget certainty often more important than chasing lowest possible price. Longer fixes may be particularly suitable.

Flexible/pass-through contract holders: Already exposed to wholesale. Consider layering in fixed volume or switching to full fix if risk tolerance is low.
