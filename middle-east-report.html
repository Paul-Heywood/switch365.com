● Why the Futures Market Matters

The futures market shows the price for energy delivered in future periods. A customer renewing from September 2027 is not only interested in today’s price. They are interested in what the market is currently offering for September 2027 onwards.

If 2027, 2028 or 2029 delivery periods are cheaper than the current market, the customer may be able to use that future pricing now, depending on supplier availability and contract rules.

Cheaper further out does not mean cheaper later. The future market is only useful while the customer can still buy that future delivery period.

Waiting lets the market make the decision for the customer. As time passes, future periods become the near-term market and can pick up current risk, supplier caution, weather pressure or geopolitical premium.

This is not automatic advice to fix. It is a reason to review live prices now.

The Red Sea / Yemen risk remains separate from Hormuz. According to the Australian DFAT and Wikipedia sources, no reported Houthi attacks in the Red Sea have occurred since the Gaza ceasefire in October 2025. Even if Hormuz improves, Red Sea risk can remain separate. The dual-choke-point risk means Hormuz for oil/LNG and Bab el-Mandeb/Red Sea for shipping and rerouting.

Supplier Price-Book Lag and Procurement Window

Why wholesale relief may not immediately reach customer quotes

NBP has moved from around 120p/therm at peak stress to ~99-100p/th currently. Oil has dropped from $126 to $77.50. But supplier price books may not move at the same speed as wholesale markets.

Wholesale Has Moved

NBP fell from ~120p to ~99p/th. Brent from $126 to $77.50. TTF from peaks near €60 to ~€42/MWh.

Supplier Books May Lag

Customer quotes may still reflect previous risk premium. Some suppliers may reprice down, hold, move cautiously, or withdraw offers quickly.

Window Can Close Fast

If Hormuz worsens, talks fail, LNG competition rises or Norway flows reduce, suppliers can reprice back up sharply.

Supplier price-book lag can work both ways. It can create a short opportunity after market relief, but it can also close quickly if the headline fails. Waiting for certainty can mean missing the price movement. These prices could be lost as fast as they were gained.

Supplier book lag is not fully verified from public sources, but it is a normal commercial risk when wholesale markets move quickly. The best action is to test live pricing now.

Impact on UK Business Energy Prices

Why this matters for commercial gas and electricity procurement

UK business energy prices are driven by wholesale gas (NBP), wholesale power (UK Baseload), network costs, policy costs, supplier margin and risk premium. The current Middle East situation affects the wholesale component most directly.

Why NBP Matters to Business

NBP is the main UK wholesale gas benchmark. When NBP moves, supplier gas pricing moves. Because gas-fired generation still dominates UK electricity, NBP also affects power pricing. A 10p move in NBP can meaningfully change a business gas quote.

Why the Buying Window Matters

When wholesale prices drop sharply but supplier books haven't caught up, businesses may secure pricing below where the market settles. This window can close within days if volatility returns.

Why Forward Pricing Matters

A business renewing in September 2027 should look at what the market offers for Sep 2027, 2028 and 2029 delivery — not just today's spot price. The futures market shows these prices now.

Who Is Most Exposed

SMEs on variable, deemed, rollover or out-of-contract rates are most exposed to wholesale volatility. Multi-site operators, hospitality, care, manufacturing and retail face budget uncertainty.

UK gas around £1 per therm creates a live procurement window but not certainty. The Ofgem domestic price cap rises 13% from July (to £1,862), reflecting earlier wholesale increases. Business contracts are not capped — they track wholesale markets directly.

Commercial Takeaway

For UK businesses and SMEs — what to do now

Last night helped keep the buying window open. It did not make the market safe.

Sanctions waivers, lower oil and improving Hormuz traffic are positive, but they are not full normalisation. The gas market itself is still tight enough to move quickly. Storage refill, LNG competition, Norway flows and supplier book lag can pull NBP back up even if oil calms.

What the Customer Should Know

  • The customer should not only look at when their current contract ends. They should look at what the future market is offering now.
  • If the customer renews from September 2027, the key question is whether the September 2027, 2028 and 2029 delivery periods are currently cheaper than the near-term market.
  • If they are, the customer may be able to use a longer contract to access those cheaper future periods now.
  • Cheaper further out does not mean cheaper later. It means there may be an opportunity now, while that period is still priced as future delivery.
  • Waiting is not neutral. It is a bet that the risk is being overstated.
  • These prices could be lost as fast as they were gained.

Recommended Customer Action

For customers within 12 months of contract end date, the commercially sensible step is to review immediately, test available supplier pricing, compare short-term and longer-term pricing, and, where pricing and risk profile support it, consider fixing now and fixing longer (36+ months) before supplier books move further.

If longer-term gas or power pricing is cheaper than near-term pricing, this may strengthen the case for fixing longer, especially for SMEs that want budget certainty.

This is a review-now window, not a wait-and-see market.

This guidance is for Switch 365 Ltd's customers and prospects. It is not advice to Switch 365 Ltd about its own energy procurement. Each business should assess its own consumption profile, contract timing and risk appetite.
