Market Further Out — Is the market cheaper further out?

The forward curve shows whether energy for delivery in future periods is priced higher or lower than near-term delivery. This matters commercially because a customer whose contract starts in a future period may be able to buy into that market now.

?
What This Means

The futures market is the market price for energy delivered in a future period. When the near-term market is expensive but the further-out market is cheaper, it usually means today's price contains more immediate risk: supply pressure, shipping uncertainty, weather risk, storage concern or geopolitical premium.

For a customer whose contract starts 12 months from now, the relevant question is not only what energy costs today. It is what the market is offering for their delivery period. If that future period is cheaper, the customer may be able to contract into that lower forward market now.

!
Why Waiting Can Be Expensive

Customers often assume that if the 12-month or 18-month market is cheaper today, they can wait and pick it up later. That is not how the market works. The future price is available because the customer is buying that future delivery period now. If the customer waits, the delivery period moves closer, current risk gets built into the price, and supplier books can change.

In simple terms: the cheaper forward market can become the expensive current market.

Cheaper further out does not mean cheaper later. The future market is only useful if you act while it is still future pricing. Waiting means the customer stays exposed while today's future market becomes tomorrow's current market. This is not automatic advice to fix. It is a reason to review now.

Supplier Price-Book Lag & Procurement Window

Why wholesale moves may not yet show in supplier pricing

▼
How Supplier Price Books Work

Suppliers buy energy on wholesale markets and then build price books for customer quotes. There is always a lag between wholesale market movement and supplier book updates. When markets move quickly — as they have since the Hormuz closure — that lag creates windows of opportunity (or risk) for buyers.

● Window Opportunity

If wholesale markets have moved but supplier books have not fully caught up, customers may secure pricing before the next round of supplier repricing lands. This window can be days or weeks. It closes when suppliers refresh their books.

● Window Risk

If wholesale prices rise and supplier books then catch up, customers who waited face higher pricing. Quote validity may shorten from 30 days to 7-14 days. Fixed offers may be withdrawn during volatile periods.

Current assessment: Wholesale markets have been volatile since late February. Supplier books are likely in various stages of adjustment. Some may have fully repriced; others may still be catching up. The safest commercial assumption is that the window is narrowing, not widening. Customers within 12 months of renewal should test live pricing now rather than assume books haven't moved.

Note: Supplier book lag is not fully verified from public sources. It is inferred from normal market behaviour and supplier pricing patterns. Use live supplier or broker pricing before making customer recommendations.
