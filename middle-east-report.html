What the Offered Price Range Means

Gas and power prices are not one single number. The boards show different future delivery periods. If the later periods are cheaper than the near-term contracts, this can support reviewing longer fixed options where the customer's contract start date allows it. Cheaper further out does not mean cheaper later. It means there may be an opportunity now, while that period is still priced as future delivery.

Why the Futures Market Matters

The futures market shows the price for energy delivered in future periods. A September 2027 customer is not only interested in today's price. They are interested in what the market is currently offering for September 2027 onwards.

If 2027, 2028 or 2029 delivery periods are cheaper than the current market, the customer may be able to use that future pricing now, depending on supplier availability and contract rules.

The risk with waiting is that those future periods move closer every day. When they become the near-term market, they can pick up current risk, supplier caution, non-commodity changes, weather pressure or geopolitical premium.

Cheaper further out does not mean cheaper later. The future market is only useful while the customer can still buy that future delivery period. Waiting lets the market make the decision for the customer. This is not automatic advice to fix. It is a reason to review live prices now.

Ceasefire Reality Check

Preliminary peace deal does not equal complete de-escalation.

Formal signing still matters. The ceremony scheduled for 19 June in Switzerland is a political milestone, but the agreement's operational value depends on what is actually signed.

Operational implementation matters more than political wording. Trump can authorize Hormuz opening, but carriers, insurers, and shipowners make the operational decisions. As of 16 June, none of the four major global carriers have announced resumption plans.

Shipping confidence matters more than social media claims. BIMCO, the International Chamber of Shipping, INTERCARGO and INTERTANKO jointly condemned recent attacks and urged caution. Mine clearance is estimated at ~6 months.

Lower oil prices are encouraging, but they are not proof that supplier books, LNG flows, NBP or UK Power have fully normalised. Brent at $81.40 on 16 June is still ~$11 above pre-war levels.

No major escalation is not the same as real de-escalation. The market has shifted from active escalation to conditional de-escalation. Conditional is the key word.

Commercial Comparison

If the World Bank baseline already points to materially higher energy prices even with disruption easing, then waiting only makes sense if the customer believes the risk is being overstated. Current Brent at $81.40 on 16 June is below the World Bank $86 average forecast, suggesting the market is pricing some probability of smoother normalisation. However, if the deal falters, the higher-risk scenario ($95-$115) becomes more relevant.

For UK businesses, the key point is that even the baseline scenario implies sustained higher energy costs through 2026. The preliminary deal does not eliminate this risk — it only changes the probability distribution.

Supplier Price-Book Lag and Procurement Window

Oil and wholesale markets may have softened quickly on the US-Iran deal headlines. Supplier books may not move at the same speed. Customer quotes may still reflect previous risk premium. Some supplier books may reprice down, hold, or move cautiously.

If the deal fails, supplier books can move back up quickly. The best action is to test live pricing now.

Supplier price-book lag can work both ways. It can create a short opportunity after market relief, but it can also close quickly if the headline fails. Waiting for certainty can mean missing the price movement.

Supplier book lag is not fully verified from public sources, but it is a normal commercial risk when wholesale markets move quickly. Live supplier or broker pricing is the only way to confirm current availability.

If wholesale markets have moved but supplier books have not fully caught up, customers may have a short window to secure pricing before supplier repricing lands. If supplier books have already caught up, the window may have narrowed or closed.

Commercial Takeaway

The customer should not only look at when their current contract ends. They should look at what the future market is offering now.

If the customer renews from September 2027, the key question is whether the September 2027, 2028 and 2029 delivery periods are currently cheaper than the near-term market. If they are, the customer may be able to use a longer contract to access those cheaper future periods now.

The risk with waiting is that those future periods do not stay future periods forever. As time passes, they become the current market and can absorb the same risk premium that exists today.

Cheaper further out does not mean cheaper later. Waiting is not neutral. The customer either reviews the numbers now, or lets the market decide the price later.

Waiting is not neutral. It is a bet that the preliminary deal will become full operational normalisation, that Hormuz will fully reopen, that shipping confidence will return, that supplier books will move favourably, and that the cheaper forward market will still be available later.

For customers within 12 months of contract end date, the commercially sensible step is to review immediately, test available supplier pricing, compare short-term and longer-term pricing, and, where pricing and risk profile support it, consider fixing now and fixing longer before supplier books move further.

If the NBP forward market is cheaper than the near-term market, that does not mean the customer can wait and get the same price later. It means the customer may have an opportunity now, while that period is still priced as future delivery.
