💡
Why the Futures Market Matters

The futures market shows the price for energy delivered in future periods. A customer renewing in September 2027 is not only interested in today's price. They are interested in what the market is currently offering for September 2027, 2028 and 2029 delivery.

If 2027, 2028 or 2029 delivery periods are cheaper than the current market, the customer may be able to use that future pricing now, depending on supplier availability and contract rules.

The risk with waiting is that those future periods move closer every day. When they become the near-term market, they can pick up current risk, supplier caution, non-commodity changes, weather pressure or geopolitical premium.

⚠ Key Principles

Cheaper further out does not mean cheaper later. The future market is only useful while the customer can still buy that future delivery period. Waiting lets the market make the decision for the customer. This is not automatic advice to fix. It is a reason to review live prices now.

Ceasefire Reality Check

What the ceasefire was understood to cover: The April 8 ceasefire halted full-scale US-Israeli warfare with Iran. It did not end all hostilities, did not reopen Hormuz to normal commercial traffic, and did not resolve the Lebanon-Hezbollah front.

What is excluded or disputed: The ceasefire never covered Israel-Lebanon operations. Iran has consistently linked any peace deal to a Lebanon ceasefire. Israel has insisted Lebanon should be treated separately. This disconnect is now the primary fuse for wider escalation.

What actually happened on the ground (5-10 June):

  • US and Iran exchanged direct military fire on 5 June (drones, missiles, airstrikes)
  • Israel struck Beirut's southern suburbs on 7 June (Hezbollah targets)
  • Iran launched ballistic missiles at Israel on 7 June — first since April ceasefire
  • Israel struck military targets inside Iran including Mahshahr petrochemical on 8 June
  • Houthis renewed Red Sea blockade on Israeli shipping on 8 June
  • US Apache helicopter went down near Oman on 9 June; US struck Iran; Iran retaliated against Jordan, Kuwait, Bahrain on 9-10 June

⚠ Commercial Point

No major escalation is not the same as real de-escalation. The events of 5-10 June represent the most intense exchange since the April ceasefire. A conditional pause announced on the afternoon of 8 June was broken by the 9-10 June US-Iran exchange. Direct US-Iran military action, Iranian retaliation against Gulf states, energy infrastructure strikes, and shipping route disruption are all forms of escalation — even if they are described as "limited" or "proportional."

Shipping and Choke Point Status

Strait of Hormuz: The Strait remains under a selective, permission-based transit regime. JMIC assesses the threat level as CRITICAL. Daily transits average 3-4 vessels versus a historical norm of approximately 138. The overnight Apache incident and subsequent US-Iran strikes occurred in the Hormuz / Oman coast area. Commercial vessels are routing south of the traffic separation scheme via Omani territorial waters. Mine risk remains credible. The US blockade of Iranian ports remains in force. This is not normal shipping access.

Red Sea / Bab el-Mandeb: On 8 June, Yemen's Houthis announced a "complete and total ban" on Israeli maritime navigation in the Red Sea. This creates a dual choke-point crisis: Hormuz AND Bab el-Mandeb. LNG and oil flows face disruption on both corridors. Container shipping remains severely disrupted.

Gulf and regional base risk: The 9-10 June escalation expanded the conflict to Jordan, Kuwait and Bahrain — all countries that host US military presence. Jordan intercepted five missiles. Kuwait and Bahrain activated air defences. This raises the risk that shipping, logistics and energy infrastructure near these bases could be affected by debris, interceptions, or secondary targeting.

Impact on LNG: Qatar declared force majeure on long-term LNG contracts after Iranian retaliation damaged Ras Laffan facilities. The Asian LNG benchmark rose 94% in March. European LNG competition intensifies. If Qatar disruptions continue through end-2026, a global LNG supply shortfall of 26 bcm could arise (ACER estimate).

Impact on UK energy: The UK now competes globally for LNG cargoes. Disrupted Hormuz and Red Sea routes mean longer voyage times, higher freight costs, and increased insurance premiums. All of these feed into NBP sentiment and ultimately UK business energy bills.

⚠ Key Point

Hormuz risk is not theoretical when aircraft, drones, radar sites, regional bases and shipping routes are all part of the same escalation chain. The 9-10 June events show that the conflict can expand to Gulf states, not just Iran and Israel. Selective access through Hormuz is not the same as normal global shipping access. Chinese priority or approved vessel passage does not mean Europe or the UK has normalised supply conditions.

Supplier Price-Book Lag and Procurement Window

Has NBP moved before supplier books? NBP front month gap-up to 123.74 p/th on 8 June before settling back to 116.93. The overnight 9-10 June US-Iran escalation adds further near-term risk premium. Whether supplier price books have fully reflected this volatility cannot be verified from public sources. It is a normal commercial risk when wholesale markets move quickly.

Has UK power moved before supplier books? UK power front month is up 13.6% over 30 days. The trend is clearly upward. Supplier pricing typically lags exchange settlement by hours to days. During periods of high volatility, the lag can create a short window where live broker pricing reflects yesterday's settlement rather than today's spike.

After a direct US-Iran military exchange, supplier books may not immediately reflect every wholesale movement, but they can reprice quickly. Customers who wait for perfect clarity risk missing the window between market movement and supplier repricing.

Quote validity risk: If volatility rises, suppliers may shorten quote validity periods from 24-48 hours to same-day or even intra-day. Fixed offers may be withdrawn without notice.

⚠ Commercial Conclusion

If wholesale markets have moved but supplier books have not fully caught up, customers may have a short window to secure pricing before supplier repricing lands.

If supplier books have already caught up, the window may have narrowed or closed.

Supplier book lag is not fully verified from public sources, but it is a normal commercial risk when wholesale markets move quickly. The commercially sensible step is to request live quotes immediately, compare 1, 2 and 3-year options, and be prepared to decide quickly.

Market Interpretation

What the market is pricing now: NBP is pricing in elevated geopolitical risk premium, European storage deficit, Norwegian maintenance, and LNG supply uncertainty. The overnight US-Iran escalation near Hormuz adds fresh near-term risk that may not yet be fully reflected in settlement data. The near-term market (Win-26 at 119.70 p/th) carries significantly more risk premium than future delivery periods (Sum-27 at 85.54 p/th).

What NBP futures suggest: The backwardated curve — where near-term prices exceed future prices — suggests the market expects some normalisation of supply conditions over time. But this normalisation is not guaranteed, and the discount for future periods can shrink if risk persists. The 9-10 June escalation is a reminder that near-term risk can increase quickly.

What UK power futures suggest: The same backwardated structure applies. Win-26 at £103.03 vs Sum-27 at £78.09. The ~£25/MWh spread is significant and may support longer-dated fixed contracts for customers whose renewal dates align.

Is temporary softness diplomatic relief or physical improvement? Any pause in strikes is conditional and reversible. It is diplomatic relief, not physical de-escalation. Hormuz remains restricted, Red Sea blockade was renewed, and no shipping normalisation has occurred. The 9-10 June exchange broke the 8 June conditional pause.

What would trigger the next leg up? Further direct strikes on energy infrastructure, complete closure of Hormuz to even selective transit, expansion of Houthi attacks to non-Israeli shipping, or confirmation that the conditional pause has fully collapsed.

What would trigger the next leg down? Verified full reopening of Hormuz, sustained reduction in military exchanges, a signed US-Iran agreement with implementation timeline, or demonstrated recovery in LNG flows.

Impact on UK Business Energy Prices

Why UK gas reacts to NBP, LNG and European storage: NBP is the main UK wholesale gas benchmark. The UK no longer has significant domestic gas production relative to demand. We import via pipeline from Norway and via LNG from Qatar, the US and elsewhere. When LNG routes are disrupted, European storage is low, or Norway has maintenance, NBP moves — and supplier gas pricing follows.

Why electricity follows gas: Gas-fired generation still sets the marginal price for UK electricity much of the time. Even with growing renewables, when wind is low and demand is high, gas plants ramp up and set the wholesale price. This is why NBP risk affects electricity quotes too.

Why shipping disruption hits delivered cost: When Hormuz and the Red Sea are restricted, LNG cargoes take longer routes, insurance costs rise, and fewer cargoes are available. The UK competes with Asia for global LNG. Higher shipping costs feed into NBP, which feeds into supplier pricing.

Why the repricing window can close quickly: After a direct US-Iran military exchange, supplier pricing departments may update more frequently. A customer who waits for "more clarity" may find that clarity arrives in the form of higher prices.

Why SMEs on renewal, variable, deemed or rollover rates are most exposed: Businesses on fixed contracts are protected until renewal. Businesses on variable, deemed or rollover rates are exposed to wholesale market movements in real time. The July Ofgem price cap announcement (up 13% from 1 July) shows how retail pricing follows wholesale with a lag.

Why waiting for certainty can mean losing the cheaper forward curve: If the NBP forward market is cheaper than the near-term market, that does not mean the customer can wait and get the same price later. It means the customer may have an opportunity now, while that period is still priced as future delivery.

What Would Change This View?

Evidence that would reduce risk:

  • Verified full reopening of Hormuz to normal commercial traffic (not selective transit)
  • War-risk premiums falling materially
  • Confirmed US-Iran agreement with implementation timeline
  • Qatari LNG normalisation and force majeure lifted
  • TTF/NBP sustained decline backed by physical flows, not headlines
  • Supplier price books reducing, not merely holding
  • European gas storage refilling at or above seasonal norms
  • No further direct US-Iran, Iran-Israel or Israel-Iran strikes over several trading sessions
  • Verified reduction in Lebanon, Yemen and Red Sea escalation risk

Evidence that would increase risk:

  • Further strikes on Iranian or Israeli energy infrastructure
  • Complete closure of Hormuz to all transit
  • Houthi expansion of Red Sea attacks to non-Israeli shipping
  • Breakdown of US-Iran talks with no alternative diplomatic track
  • Gulf states drawn into direct conflict
  • European gas storage failing to refill during summer injection season

⚠ Important

Temporary diplomatic language is not enough. A conditional, reversible pause in strikes is not de-escalation. The 9-10 June exchange demonstrated that even a announced "suspension" can be broken within 24 hours. What matters for UK business energy prices is physical supply improvement, shipping normalisation, and sustained wholesale price reduction — not statements of intent.

Customer Action

Volatility is real. NBP is a key UK gas benchmark. UK power futures are key for electricity renewal decisions. The market can move before supplier books fully reprice. Buyers within 12 months of contract end should be reviewing immediately.

Where appropriate, the commercial case is to fix now and fix long — 36+ months — before further repricing hits. If NBP, UK power or the wider market further out is cheaper, this can strengthen the case for a longer-term fix.

Cheaper further out does not mean cheaper later. The futures market is only useful while the customer can still buy that future delivery period. Waiting can allow the cheaper future market to become the more expensive current market.

For customers inside 12 months of contract end date:

Review immediately. Test live supplier pricing. Compare short-term and longer-term options. Do not wait for the market to settle before checking the numbers.

For customers with future renewal dates (e.g. 2027):

Do not ignore the future market. If future delivery periods are cheaper than the near-term market, the customer may be able to secure that future price now. Waiting does not mean the same future price will still be available later.

For electricity customers:

UK power futures should be checked directly. Do not rely only on gas-market commentary. Electricity customers need to compare the current power market with future power delivery periods.

For gas customers:

NBP current and future pricing should be checked directly. Do not rely on generic gas commentary where NBP data is available.
