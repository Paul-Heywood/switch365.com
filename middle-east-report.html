WHAT THE POWER TABLE MEANS

Electricity prices are heavily influenced by gas, generation mix, weather, carbon costs and wider risk premium. The table shows what the market is offering for future power delivery periods, not just today's price. Cheaper power further out does not mean cheaper power later. It means the customer may be able to access a future delivery period before it becomes the current market. Do not assume UK power has fully followed gas lower unless live UK power and supplier pricing confirms it.

Why the Futures Market Matters

The futures market shows the price for energy delivered in future periods. A September 2027 customer is not only interested in today's price. They are interested in what the market is currently offering for September 2027 onwards. If 2027, 2028 or 2029 delivery periods are cheaper than the current market, the customer may be able to use that future pricing now, depending on supplier availability and contract rules.

Cheaper further out does not mean cheaper later

The future market is only useful while the customer can still buy that future delivery period. As time passes, that future period becomes the near-term market and can pick up current risk, supplier caution, and geopolitical premium.

Waiting lets the market decide for the customer

Waiting does not guarantee those cheaper future prices become available later. Waiting means the customer gives up the chance to contract into the cheaper forward market and risks that future period becoming the more expensive current market.

The risk with waiting is that those future periods move closer every day. When they become the near-term market, they can pick up current risk, supplier caution, non-commodity changes, weather pressure or geopolitical premium. This is not automatic advice to fix. It is a reason to review live prices now.

Ceasefire Reality Check

What the interim agreement covers, what it does not, and what happened over the weekend

The 60-day roadmap is progress, not a finished settlement.

The US and Iran signed a Memorandum of Understanding on 18 June 2026, starting a 60-day window to reach a final peace deal. Talks are scheduled in Doha on Tuesday 30 June. The US Treasury temporarily lifted sanctions until mid-August. But the weekend proved the ceasefire is fragile.

What the Deal Covers
  • Cessation of direct US-Iran hostilities (agreed Saturday-Monday)
  • 60-day negotiation roadmap for final settlement
  • Temporary US sanctions waiver until mid-August 2026
  • Iranian oil sales temporarily permitted
  • $12 billion in frozen Iranian funds to be released
  • Four negotiating groups on nuclear, sanctions, and other issues
  • Doha talks scheduled 30 June 2026
What is Excluded or Disputed
  • IAEA inspection access to bombed sites — IAEA says yes, Iran says no
  • Uranium enrichment limits — unresolved
  • Lebanon-Israel conflict — separate track, remains violent
  • Gaza — not directly addressed in US-Iran framework
  • Red Sea / Yemen Houthi risk — separate from Hormuz
  • Long-term Hormuz administration — Iran asserts control
  • "Free passage" vs "controlled passage" — Iran says never returning to pre-war norms
What the Weekend Proved
  • Both sides exchanged strikes within 48 hours of "ceasefire"
  • A tanker was hit in Hormuz despite the deal
  • UKMTO paused escorts — operational confidence damaged
  • Iran asserted route control, not free passage
  • Bahrain was targeted — regional escalation risk remains
  • The deal survived, but safety did not

Supplier Price-Book Lag and Procurement Window

Why the gap between wholesale and supplier pricing matters

Wholesale gas has moved quickly. NBP dropped from 120.6p/th on 11 June to 97.8p/th on 26 June — a verified fall of nearly 19% in two weeks. UK power has followed gas lower, with Win-26 power easing from ~£103/MWh to ~£94/MWh over the same period.

But supplier books do not move at the same speed as wholesale markets. When wholesale prices fall rapidly, there is typically a lag of days to weeks before supplier pricing fully reflects the move. Some suppliers reprice quickly; others hold their books steady; some may withdraw offers if volatility returns.

This lag creates a short procurement window. Customers may be able to access pricing that still reflects some of the previous risk premium, or at minimum, pricing that has softened from the peak but has not yet been fully repriced upward after weekend incidents.

Supplier book lag can work both ways

It can create a short opportunity after market relief, but it can also close quickly if the headline fails. If Hormuz worsens, ship incidents continue, or nuclear inspections derail, suppliers can reprice back up within hours.

Waiting for certainty can mean missing the price

These prices could be lost as fast as they were gained. The weekend proved that a single ship incident can reintroduce risk premium. Customers who wait for full clarity may find the window has closed.

Impact on UK Business Energy Prices

Why global events reach UK business energy bills

Why UK gas reacts to NBP, LNG and European storage risk. The UK National Balancing Point (NBP) is the main UK wholesale gas benchmark. Around 50% of UK gas supply comes from the North Sea and Norwegian pipelines; the rest is imported as LNG or via interconnectors from Europe. When European storage is low (currently 47.96%, ~12pp below norm), the UK competes with Europe for LNG cargoes. If Hormuz disrupts Qatari LNG, UK supply diversity is reduced and prices rise.

Why electricity follows gas. Gas-fired power stations still set the marginal price for UK electricity in most settlement periods. When NBP rises, the cost of generating electricity from gas rises, and wholesale power prices follow. Even with growing renewables, gas remains the price-setter during peak demand and low-wind periods. The UK power forward curve (Win-26 at ~£94/MWh, Sum-27 at ~£75/MWh) directly reflects this gas-to-power link.

Why shipping disruption hits delivered cost. The UK imports LNG from Qatar, the US, and other sources. When Hormuz is disrupted, Qatari LNG is delayed or diverted, and the UK must compete for alternative cargoes at higher prices. Even when flows improve, if shipping confidence is damaged (as the weekend proved), insurers charge higher war-risk premiums, shipowners add contingency costs, and supplier pricing reflects this uncertainty.

Why the repricing window can close quickly. Supplier price books are not updated in real-time. When wholesale markets move rapidly — as they have in the past two weeks (NBP down 19%) — there is a lag before customer quotes fully reflect the move. But if volatility returns after weekend incidents, suppliers can shorten quote validity, withdraw offers, or reprice upward within hours. The window to access softer pricing is only open while wholesale markets remain calm and supplier books have not fully adjusted.

Why UK gas around or below £1 creates a live procurement window. At ~98p/th, NBP is at levels last seen in February 2026 — before the major Hormuz escalation. This is a material improvement from the 120p+ levels of early June. For businesses within 12 months of renewal, this creates an opportunity to review pricing. For businesses within 90 days, it creates urgency — failing to act risks drifting into deemed or out-of-contract rates that can be 30-50% above contracted pricing.

Why waiting for certainty can mean losing the cheaper forward curve. The futures market shows Sum-27 gas at ~78.5p/th and Cal-27 at ~84.6p/th — both below current spot. A customer renewing into 2027 may be able to access these forward prices now. But if they wait, and the market re-absorbs risk premium, those future periods could become more expensive as they approach. Waiting allows the market to make the decision for the customer.
