Why the Futures Market Matters

The futures market shows the price for energy delivered in future periods. A customer renewing in September 2027 is not only interested in today's price. They are interested in what the market is currently offering for September 2027 onwards.

If 2027, 2028 or 2029 delivery periods are cheaper than the current market, the customer may be able to use that future pricing now, depending on supplier availability and contract rules.

Cheaper further out does not mean cheaper later. The future market is only useful while the customer can still buy that future delivery period. As time passes, that future period becomes the near-term market and can pick up the same risk premium that exists today. Waiting lets the market make the decision for the customer. This is not automatic advice to fix. It is a reason to review live prices now.

Ceasefire Reality Check

What the agreement covers — and what it does not

Signed interim agreement does not equal complete de-escalation. Implementation matters more than political wording. Shipping confidence matters more than social media claims.

Lower gas prices are encouraging, but they are not proof that supplier books, LNG flows, NBP or UK Power have fully normalised. Prices have dropped because confidence improved. If confidence breaks, those prices can disappear quickly.

No major escalation is not the same as real de-escalation. Lebanon can break the de-escalation narrative even if Hormuz talks continue.

Supplier Price-Book Lag and Procurement Window

Why wholesale moves may not yet show in customer quotes

Supplier price-book lag can work both ways. It can create a short opportunity after market relief, but it can also close quickly if the headline fails. Waiting for certainty can mean missing the price movement. These prices could be lost as fast as they were gained.

What Has Moved

  • ›NBP gas: ~120p/th → below £1/therm → bounced above £1
  • ›UK power: Followed gas lower, Win-26 from ~£102 to £99.50
  • ›Brent: $92.78 (5 Jun) → $79.24 (22 Jun)
  • ›TTF: ~50 EUR/MWh → 42.51 EUR/MWh

Why Supplier Books Lag

  • ›Suppliers hedge positions in advance — cannot reprice instantly
  • ›Risk teams may wait for volatility to settle before adjusting
  • ›Some suppliers may hold prices to maintain margin
  • ›Non-commodity costs (green levies, network, policy) do not move with wholesale
  • ›Quote validity may shorten if volatility returns
If wholesale markets have moved but supplier books have not fully caught up, customers may have a short window to secure pricing before supplier repricing lands. If supplier books have already caught up, the window may have narrowed or closed. Supplier book lag is not fully verified from public sources, but it is a normal commercial risk when wholesale markets move quickly.

Impact on UK Business Energy Prices

How global developments translate to UK contract pricing

Why NBP Matters to UK Businesses

NBP is the main UK wholesale gas benchmark. When NBP moves, supplier gas pricing can move. Because gas-fired generation still influences UK power prices, electricity pricing can also be affected.

  • ›Gas dropped from ~120p to below £1/therm, then bounced above £1 — volatility is real
  • ›The fall created a window; the bounce showed fragility
  • ›Supplier books may not reflect the full wholesale move yet
  • ›Customers on variable, deemed or rollover rates are most exposed

Why UK Power Futures Matter

UK power futures show what the electricity market is offering for future delivery. A customer renewing in September 2027 should look at what the market offers for September 2027, 2028 and 2029.

  • ›Win-26 at £99.50 vs Sum-27 at £78.00 = ~£21.50/MWh potential saving
  • ›For a 1MWh annual consumer, that's £20,000/year difference
  • ›Carbon costs and non-commodity charges will also affect final pricing
  • ›Waiting does not guarantee these future prices stay available

SME-Specific Guidance

High-consumption electricity users (manufacturing, data centres, cold storage) should prioritise reviewing power contracts. The backwardated curve offers meaningful savings for later start dates.

Gas-intensive users (hospitality, care homes, food production) should monitor NBP closely. The below-£1 level may offer relief but the bounce signals caution.

Multi-site operators should review portfolio-wide renewal dates. Staggering fixes across periods can reduce concentration risk.

Customers on flexible or pass-through contracts are already exposed to wholesale moves. Fixed options may provide budget certainty.

Commercial Takeaway

What UK businesses should do now

Hormuz is not properly normal if shipping still needs permission or coordination, Lebanon is now the clearest spoiler to the clean de-escalation story, and gas has already shown signs of bouncing after dropping below £1/therm.

The gas market itself is still tight enough to move quickly. Storage refill, LNG competition, Norway flows and supplier book lag can pull NBP back up even if oil calms.
