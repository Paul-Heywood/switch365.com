Futures Market Explanation

Why the Futures Market Matters

The futures market shows the price for energy delivered in future periods. A September 2027 customer is not only interested in today's price. They are interested in what the market is currently offering for September 2027 onwards. If 2027, 2028 or 2029 delivery periods are cheaper than the current market, the customer may be able to use that future pricing now, depending on supplier availability and contract rules.

Cheaper further out does not mean cheaper later. The future market is only useful while the customer can still buy that future delivery period. Waiting lets the market make the decision for the customer. The risk with waiting is that those future periods move closer every day. When they become the near-term market, they can pick up current risk, supplier caution, non-commodity changes, weather pressure or geopolitical premium.

Key Principle
⏳ Time Erodes Opportunity
A Summer 2027 contract is only available while it remains a forward period. As each month passes, it becomes the current market and absorbs today's risk premium.
Key Principle
📉 Backwardation = Opportunity
When future periods trade below near-term prices, longer contracts may lock in lower rates. But this only works if the customer acts before the market reprices.
Key Principle
⚠️ Waiting is Not Neutral
This is not automatic advice to fix. It is a reason to review live prices now. The customer either reviews the numbers now, or lets the market decide the price later.

Gas Price Reality Check

Why UK Gas Needs Its Own Evidence

The headline says oil is lower after Doha. The gas market says be careful. UK gas must be checked on its own. Do not assume gas has followed oil lower. The £1 per therm line matters, but it must be verified from current gas data, not inferred from oil.

Critical Level
🇬🇧 UK Gas £1 Line
UK gas at ~102p/therm on 1 July Trading Economics UK Gas. The buying window is open, but the line is fragile. If gas holds above £1, the bounce is visible. If it drops below, the window opens wider but is not safe. Gas dropped 5.7% in June but downside limited by tight physical conditions. Trading Economics UK Gas
LNG Risk
🚢 Shell LNG Warning
Shell warned that severe Hormuz disruption shut in around one fifth of global monthly LNG supply. This is not new today, but it remains commercially relevant because it links Hormuz directly to LNG availability, TTF, NBP and UK power. LNG is the bridge between Hormuz and UK gas.
Cargo Competition
🌏 US LNG Cargo Competition
US LNG exports to Europe fell below 50% in June for the first time since July 2024 Reuters LNG Flows. Asian JKM at $17.33/mmBtu vs TTF at $13.19/mmBtu pulled cargoes eastward. Egypt paid premiums and imported a record 1.06 MT. Europe and UK compete with Asia for LNG.
Hidden Risk
📦 Storage Refill
EU storage at 49.32% on 1 July AGSI Storage — well below 56% last year and 61% five-year average Trading Economics UK Gas. UK storage only 18.04% full AGSI Storage. Europe still needs meaningful injection before winter. Storage refill is the hidden gas risk that headlines ignore.
Demand Risk
🌡️ Heat & Gas Burn
An intense heatwave across Europe is boosting demand from electricity providers to meet increased cooling needs Trading Economics UK Gas. Heat can turn a soft gas market into a firmer one by increasing power-sector gas demand. Low wind periods add further gas burn.
Supply Risk
🇳🇴 Norway Flow Risk
Norway supplies a material share of European gas demand. Gassco flows currently stable, but Norway needs checking every morning. Unplanned outages at Troll, Oseberg or Nyhamna have moved NBP quickly in the past. New Norway/Troll investment helps later, not today.
Timing Risk
📋 Supplier Book Lag
Supplier price-book lag can work both ways. It can create a short opportunity after market relief, but it can also close quickly if the headline fails. Waiting for certainty can mean missing the price movement. These prices could be lost as fast as they were gained.
⚠️ The Core Message

If UK gas remains above £1 per therm, the bounce is still visible. If UK gas drops below £1 per therm, the window is open, but still not safe. The window is real, but it is not safe. Do not over-link NBP to oil. NBP needs its own gas-specific evidence. Gas has dropped from panic levels before, but the gas market itself can still bounce quickly on LNG disruption, cargo competition, storage refill, heat risk, Norway risk, or supplier repricing.

Supplier Price-Book Lag and Procurement Window

How Wholesale Moves Reach Customer Quotes

Wholesale gas has moved quickly. NBP dropped 5.7% in June and 18.5% in Q2 Trading Economics UK Gas, but the pace of supplier repricing varies. Some suppliers reprice daily; others operate on weekly or monthly book cycles. When wholesale markets move faster than supplier books, a pricing window opens — but it can close just as quickly.

Current Situation
📉 Wholesale Moved Before Books
NBP fell from crisis highs toward £1 per therm. UK power followed partially. Oil at four-month lows may not have fully flowed through to gas and power supplier pricing. Customer quotes may still reflect previous risk premium.
The Lag Effect
⏱️ Quote Validity Can Shorten
If Hormuz control risk worsens, LNG disruption continues, Asian cargo competition increases, nuclear implementation fails, heat increases or Norway flows reduce, suppliers can reprice back up sharply. Quote validity may shorten from 30 days to 7 days or less.
Customer Risk
⚠️ 90-Day Out-of-Contract Danger
Customers inside 90 days face added risk because failing to agree a new contract can push them towards deemed, rollover or out-of-contract rates. These can be materially more expensive than contracted pricing. The best action is to test live pricing now.
The Opportunity
💡 A Short Window May Exist
If wholesale markets have moved but supplier books have not fully caught up, customers may have a short window to secure pricing before supplier repricing lands. This is not a guarantee — it is a reason to check now, not wait.
⚡ Key Commercial Points

Supplier price-book lag is not fully verified from public sources, but it is a normal commercial risk when wholesale markets move quickly. Supplier price-book lag can work both ways. It can create a short opportunity after market relief, but it can also close quickly if the headline fails. Waiting for certainty can mean missing the price movement. These prices could be lost as fast as they were gained.

Impact on UK Business Energy Prices

Why This Matters to Your Energy Bill

UK business energy prices are not set by the oil headline alone. They are set by a chain of linked markets: NBP for gas, UK power futures for electricity, LNG competition for European supply, and supplier pricing behaviour for the final customer quote. Understanding these linkages is essential for making informed procurement decisions.

Gas Price Linkage
🔥 NBP → Supplier Gas Pricing
UK gas suppliers benchmark their wholesale purchases against NBP. When NBP moves — whether from LNG disruption, storage levels, or European competition — supplier costs change. Those changes flow through to customer quotes, usually with a lag of days to weeks.
Power Price Linkage
⚡ Gas → Power → Supplier Pricing
Gas-fired power stations set the marginal price for UK electricity in most periods. When gas prices rise, power prices follow. UK power at ~£95/MWh Trading Economics UK Power reflects this gas linkage. Carbon prices, wind output and interconnector flows add further variables.
LNG Competition
🌏 Global LNG → European Gas → NBP
When Asia pays a premium for LNG (JKM at $17.33 vs TTF at $13.19 Reuters LNG Flows), cargoes move east. Europe must compete harder for supply. This tightens the European gas market and supports TTF, which influences NBP through price arbitrage.
Shipping Risk Premium
🚢 Hormuz → LNG → TTF → NBP → Bills
Hormuz disruption affects LNG cargo movements. Even if crude oil flows resume, LNG-specific risk can keep gas prices elevated. This is why Shell's LNG warning matters: it turns an oil headline into a direct gas price risk.
Supplier Behaviour
📋 Quote Validity & Repricing
Suppliers may hold quotes during volatile periods, shorten validity, or withdraw offers entirely. When wholesale moves quickly, the price a customer saw last week may not be available this week. The repricing window can close quickly.
Out-of-Contract Risk
⏰ The 90-Day Danger Zone
SMEs on renewal, variable, deemed, rollover or out-of-contract rates are most exposed. Customers within 90 days who fail to agree a new contract may drift to deemed rates, which can be materially more expensive than contracted pricing.
