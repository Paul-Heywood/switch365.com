Why the Futures Market Matters
How forward pricing works in plain English
The Basic Principle
The futures market shows the price for energy delivered in future periods. A September 2027 customer is not only interested in today’s price. They are interested in what the market is currently offering for September 2027 onwards.
The Opportunity
If 2027, 2028 or 2029 delivery periods are cheaper than the current market, the customer may be able to use that future pricing now, depending on supplier availability and contract rules.
The Risk with Waiting
Those future periods move closer every day. When they become the near-term market, they can pick up current risk, supplier caution, non-commodity changes, weather pressure or geopolitical premium.
What NBP Tells Us
NBP matters because it is the main UK wholesale gas benchmark. When NBP moves, supplier gas pricing can move, and because gas-fired generation still influences UK power prices, electricity pricing can also be affected.
⚠ Key Commercial Point

Cheaper further out does not mean cheaper later. The future market is only useful while the customer can still buy that future delivery period. Waiting lets the market make the decision for the customer. This is not automatic advice to fix. It is a reason to review live prices now.

Gas Price Reality Check
Why the headline says calmer but the gas market says be careful
⚠ The Core Message

The headline says prices are calmer because hope is holding. The gas market says be careful. UK gas around £1 per therm keeps the buying window open, but it does not prove the market has found a safe floor. The window is real, but it is not safe.

🔥 UK Gas Around £1
100.29p/th
NBP day-ahead at 100.29p/therm, up 0.35% from Tuesday. Fallen 12.56% over the past month from elevated levels. The £1 line is psychologically important — it represents a significant drop from the ~120p+ area seen during peak crisis anxiety. But this move is driven by diplomatic hope, not fully by physical supply normalisation.

🎯 Storage Refill
45.3% Full
EU gas storage at 45.3% (June 16), approximately 13 percentage points below the 5-year seasonal norm. While the trajectory is on pace for 84-86% by November 1, this remains the hidden gas risk. If refill slows or demand rises unexpectedly, storage anxiety can push NBP back up quickly — even if oil stays calm.
🔗 AGSI+/GIE
🚢 LNG Competition
Active Risk
Seven empty Qatar-linked LNG tankers have entered the Strait in recent weeks — the first such voyages since February. Qatar expects to reach 80% of full LNG production over the next two months. However, Asian demand competition for LNG cargoes remains a persistent risk that can pull European gas prices higher regardless of oil sentiment.
🔗 Reuters via MarineLink
🌍 Norway Flow Risk
Monitor Daily
Norway supplies a material share of European gas demand. No material outages or maintenance affecting flows were reported today, but Norway needs checking every morning. Any unplanned outage at Troll, Kollsnes, Nyhamna or other key infrastructure can move NBP within hours. Gassco outage notices should be checked daily.
Analyst watch point — verify at Gassco
📄 Supplier Book Lag
Critical
Wholesale gas has moved quickly. NBP dropped from around 120p to the high-90s. Supplier books may not move at the same speed as wholesale markets. Customer quotes may still reflect previous risk premium. Some supplier books may reprice down, hold, move cautiously or withdraw offers quickly if volatility returns.
Switch 365 commercial watch point
💡 Why This Matters Commercially

Gas prices are not only reacting to Trump, Iran and Hormuz. NBP also reacts to storage refill, LNG competition, Norway flows, weather, wind generation, TTF movement, interconnector flows and supplier book behaviour. Do not over-link NBP to oil. Oil can drive sentiment, but NBP needs gas-specific evidence. The bounce matters more than the drop. Gas has dropped on hope, but the gas market itself is still structurally tight enough to bounce quickly.

Sanctions waivers are positive, but they are not the same as full implementation. A 60-day roadmap is progress, not a finished settlement. Implementation matters more than political wording. Shipping confidence matters more than social media claims. Lower oil prices are encouraging, but they are not proof that supplier books, LNG flows, NBP or UK Power have fully normalised. Hormuz improving is not the same as Hormuz normal.

⚠ The Lag Effect

Wholesale gas has moved quickly. NBP dropped from around 120p to the high-90s per therm over recent weeks — a fall of roughly 15-20%. UK power has dropped over 10% week on week. But supplier books may not move at the same speed as wholesale markets. Customer quotes may still reflect previous risk premium. Some supplier books may reprice down slowly, hold steady, move cautiously, or withdraw offers quickly if volatility returns.

📈 Wholesale Movement
Fast
NBP fell ~12.56% over past month. UK power dropped >10% week on week. Oil fell to four-month lows. Markets react to headlines and shipping data in real-time.
📄 Supplier Book Movement
Slower
Supplier pricing systems typically review wholesale movements over days or weeks, not minutes. Risk premiums may persist even after wholesale softens.
⏰ Quote Validity
Shortening Risk
If volatility returns, suppliers can shorten quote validity periods or withdraw offers entirely. The window to secure favourable pricing can close within hours.
💡 The Opportunity
Short Window
If wholesale markets have moved but supplier books have not fully caught up, customers may have a short window to secure pricing before supplier repricing lands.
💡 Commercial Conclusion

Supplier price-book lag can work both ways. It can create a short opportunity after market relief, but it can also close quickly if the headline fails. Waiting for certainty can mean missing the price movement. These prices could be lost as fast as they were gained. Supplier book lag is not fully verified from public sources, but it is a normal commercial risk when wholesale markets move quickly.

Impact on UK Business Energy Prices
Why global developments reach UK supplier quotes
🔥 Why NBP Matters
NBP is the main UK wholesale gas benchmark. When NBP moves, supplier gas pricing can move. NBP reacts to global LNG prices, European storage levels, Norway flows, weather, and geopolitical risk — not just UK supply and demand. A Hormuz disruption raises global shipping and insurance costs, which feeds into delivered LNG prices, which affects NBP.
⚡ Why UK Power Follows Gas
Gas-fired generation still provides a significant share of UK electricity. When gas prices rise, the marginal cost of electricity production rises, pushing power prices up. Even with growing renewables, gas sets the price during peak demand periods. This is why NBP risk affects electricity quotes too.
📈 Why the Forward Curve Matters
A customer renewing in September 2027 should not only look at today’s price. They should look at what the market is offering for September 2027, 2028 and 2029 delivery. If those future periods are cheaper than today’s market, there may be an opportunity to fix longer at a lower rate — but only while that future period remains available.
⏰ Why Timing Matters
Supplier books lag wholesale markets. When wholesale falls quickly, there can be a short window where customer quotes have not yet caught up. Conversely, when wholesale spikes, suppliers can reprice or withdraw offers within hours. Waiting is not neutral — it is a decision to remain exposed to these movements.
🏞 Who is Most Exposed
Most exposed: Businesses within 12 months of contract end date; high-consumption electricity users; gas-intensive users (manufacturing, hospitality, care); multi-site operators; customers on variable, deemed, rollover or out-of-contract rates; businesses without budget certainty requirements.
💡 The Commercial Case for Action
If your contract ends within 12 months, review live pricing now. Compare 1-year, 2-year and 3-year fixes. If forward markets are cheaper than near-term, a longer fix may offer budget certainty at a lower rate. Do not assume prices will keep falling — the window can close quickly if confidence breaks.

💡 The Threshold for Comfort

Temporary diplomatic language is not enough. A sanctions waiver is not enough. A 60-day roadmap is not enough. Improving traffic is not enough if traffic remains materially below normal.
