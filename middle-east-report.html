Market Further Out

Is the market cheaper further out? The forward curve suggests yes — but that opportunity is only available while the delivery period remains in the future.

Futures Market Explained

What This Means

The futures market is the market price for energy delivered in a future period. When the near-term market is expensive but the further-out market is cheaper, it usually means today's price contains more immediate risk — supply pressure, shipping uncertainty, weather risk, storage concern or geopolitical premium.

For a customer whose contract starts 12 months from now, the relevant question is not only what energy costs today. It is what the market is offering for their delivery period. If that future period is cheaper, the customer may be able to contract into that lower forward market now.

Why Waiting Can Be Expensive

Customers often assume that if the 12-month or 18-month market is cheaper today, they can wait and pick it up later. That is not how the market works. The future price is available because the customer is buying that future delivery period now.

If the customer waits, the delivery period moves closer, current risk gets built into the price, and supplier books can change. In simple terms: the cheaper forward market can become the expensive current market.

✓

Commercial Message for Customers

If your contract end date is far enough out, you may be able to contract into a market that is cheaper than the near-term market. That can support fixing longer where the price, contract date, usage profile and risk position make sense. Do not let the market make the decision for you by holding off. Review the position now, test live supplier pricing, compare short-term and longer-term options, and decide while the forward market is still available.

“Cheaper further out does not mean cheaper later.” “The future market is only useful if you act while it is still future pricing.” “Waiting allows the market to make the decision for the customer.”

Hormuz — Selective Access, Not Normalisation

The Strait is not fully closed — but it is not open for normal commercial traffic either. The IRGC is managing passage on a selective basis. Supplier Price-Book Lag & Procurement Window

Wholesale markets and supplier price books do not move in perfect sync. When wholesale shifts rapidly — whether up on escalation fears or down on diplomatic hopes — supplier pricing typically lags by hours to days. This creates potential windows where customer pricing may not yet reflect the latest wholesale move.

How the Lag Works

  • 1. Wholesale market moves on news/event
  • 2. Supplier risk desks assess and reprice
  • 3. New pricing uploaded to quote systems
  • 4. Existing quotes may be withdrawn
  • 5. Brokers update customer-facing prices

Each step adds time. In volatile markets, the whole cycle can take 24-72 hours.

Current Assessment

Supplier book lag is not fully verified from public sources, but it is a normal commercial risk when wholesale markets move quickly. Based on market evidence:

  • • Suppliers re-entered market after March withdrawal
  • • Pricing remains cautious and adjusts frequently
  • • Quote validity periods may be shortening
  • • Fixed offers can be withdrawn with little notice

Impact on UK Business Energy Prices

Why UK Gas Reacts to Hormuz

The UK imports ~50% of its gas, increasingly as LNG. When Hormuz closes, Qatari LNG exports are disrupted. The UK must then compete with Europe and Asia for alternative LNG cargoes from the US, Algeria, and other sources. This bidding war pushes up the price the UK pays for every unit of gas, whether imported or domestically produced.

National Gas forecasts UK LNG imports to increase 65% this summer to 2.7 bcm, with the US as the dominant source. But global competition for those cargoes is fierce. National Gas

Why Electricity Follows Gas

Gas-fired power stations set the marginal price in most UK settlement periods. Even with growing renewables, when wind drops or demand peaks, gas generators are called upon. Their fuel cost — driven by NBP gas prices — becomes the electricity price.

NESO expects sufficient summer supply and even periods of renewable surplus, but low-wind days with high demand will still see gas setting a high marginal price. NESO

SMEs Most at Risk

Businesses on rollover, deemed, variable or out-of-contract rates are most exposed to wholesale volatility. Those approaching renewal within 12 months should not assume prices will fall back to pre-crisis levels.

The Ofgem domestic price cap is rising 13% to GBP 1,862 from July. Business energy is unregulated — no cap applies. Supplier pricing can move faster and further.
