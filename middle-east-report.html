What This Means

The futures market is the market price for energy delivered in a future period. When the near-term market is expensive but the further-out market is cheaper, it usually means today's price contains more immediate risk — supply pressure, shipping uncertainty, weather risk, storage concern or geopolitical premium.

For a customer whose contract starts 12 months from now, the relevant question is not only what energy costs today. It is what the market is offering for their delivery period. If that future period is cheaper, the customer may be able to contract into that lower forward market now.

The key point is that waiting does not mean today's cheaper future price becomes tomorrow's available price. As time passes, that future period becomes the near-term market. If the same risks are still present, or if supplier books reprice, that cheaper future price can disappear.

Why Waiting Can Be Expensive

Customers often assume that if the 12-month or 18-month market is cheaper today, they can wait and pick it up later. That is not how the market works. The future price is available because the customer is buying that future delivery period now. If the customer waits, the delivery period moves closer, current risk gets built into the price, and supplier books can change.

In simple terms: the cheaper forward market can become the expensive current market.

Commercial Message for Customers

If your contract end date is far enough out, you may be able to contract into a market that is cheaper than the near-term market. That can support fixing longer where the price, contract date, usage profile and risk position make sense.

Do not let the market make the decision for you by holding off. Review the position now, test live supplier pricing, compare short-term and longer-term options, and decide while the forward market is still available.

Procurement Intelligence

Supplier Price-Book Lag and Procurement Window

Why wholesale moves may not be reflected in supplier pricing yet

What Is Price-Book Lag?

Supplier price books are the rates that energy suppliers offer to business customers. They do not update in real-time with wholesale markets. There is typically a lag of hours to days (sometimes weeks) between wholesale price movements and supplier book updates.

Why Does It Matter?

If wholesale prices soften on headlines but supplier books have not yet repriced, there may be a short window to secure pricing at levels that no longer reflect the full risk premium. That window closes when suppliers update their books.

Current Assessment

Supplier book lag is not fully verified from public sources, but it is a normal commercial risk when wholesale markets move quickly. Based on normal market behaviour, suppliers may not have fully caught up to recent wholesale softening.

Procurement Window Assessment

There may be a short window to secure pricing before supplier repricing lands. If wholesale markets have moved down on diplomatic headlines but physical supply conditions have not materially improved, suppliers may still be offering elevated rates — or they may be about to reprice.

Either way, customers within 12 months of contract end should not wait for perfect clarity. The window to act may be narrow, and the penalty for waiting (higher prices, shorter quote validity, withdrawn offers) can exceed the benefit of perfect timing.

What the Market Is Pricing Now

The market is pricing a partial, conditional de-escalation — not a resolution. Brent at ~$97/bbl reflects optimism about a potential deal and Hormuz reopening, but it is still 44% higher than a year ago and well above the World Bank baseline of $86/bbl. Risk Premium: Rising, Stabilising or Softening?

Near-term risk premium has softened from April extremes but remains elevated. The EIA maintains a risk premium on crude prices throughout its forecast period. Implied Brent volatility has averaged 78% since the conflict began — far above the sub-30% levels seen before February 2026. This tells us traders still see significant uncertainty.

Temporary Softness: Diplomatic Relief or Physical Improvement?

Headline relief, not physical improvement. Hormuz is still restricted. Production is still shut in. LNG flows are still disrupted. The softness in UK gas and power markets reflects hope about a deal, not evidence that supply conditions have normalised. If talks fail, prices can reverse quickly.

What Would Trigger the Next Leg?

Up: Talks collapse, Trump orders strikes, Hormuz closure extended, major production facility damage, escalation to Bab el-Mandeb.

Down: Confirmed Hormuz reopening, signed peace deal, normal commercial traffic resumes, war-risk premiums fall, European storage fills above seasonal norms.

Does the Forward Market Support a Longer Fix?

Yes, where contract dates allow. The backwardation in UK power (near-term expensive, further-out cheaper) means customers whose contract end dates align with Cal-27 or Cal-28 delivery may be able to lock in materially lower rates. But this only works if they act while those periods are still forward markets.

UK Impact

Impact on UK Business Energy Prices

How global developments flow through to UK business bills

Why UK Gas Reacts to LNG and Shipping Risk

The UK imports significant volumes of LNG, much of which normally transits Hormuz (Qatari LNG). When that route is disrupted, global LNG supply tightens, European prices rise, and UK gas prices follow. Even gas that does not physically come from the Middle East is priced in a global market where Middle East disruptions affect every molecule.

Why Electricity Follows Gas

In the UK, gas-fired power stations are often the marginal (most expensive) source of generation needed to meet demand. When gas prices rise, electricity prices rise with them — even if wind and solar are producing. This is why UK power and gas prices move together.

Why Shipping Disruption Hits Delivered Cost

Higher freight rates, war-risk insurance, rerouting around the Cape of Good Hope, and port congestion all add cost to every barrel and every LNG cargo. Those costs flow through to wholesale prices, then to supplier price books, then to business customer bills.

Why the Repricing Window Can Close Quickly

Supplier price books can update within hours if wholesale volatility is high. Quote validity periods may shorten from weeks to days. Fixed offers can be withdrawn without notice. Customers who delay may find the price they saw yesterday is no longer available today.

Why SMEs on Renewal Are Most Exposed

Businesses on variable, deemed, rollover or out-of-contract rates are paying the highest prices and have no price certainty. Those with contracts ending within 12 months face the immediate risk of repricing into an elevated market. Waiting for "stability" means accepting whatever the market offers when the current deal ends.

Commercial Takeaway

What UK Businesses Should Do

Direct, practical guidance for SME energy buyers

Core Message

Waiting is not neutral. It is a bet that the World Bank, the EIA, current market pricing, Gulf-state intervention, Trump's warning, the delayed military threat, Hormuz disruption, supplier price-book lag and the unresolved peace proposal are all overstating the risk.

For Customers Within 12 Months of Contract End

Review immediately. Test available supplier pricing. Compare short-term and longer-term options. Where pricing and risk profile support it, consider fixing now and fixing longer (36+ months) before supplier books move further. The forward market may offer cheaper pricing for future delivery periods — but only while those periods remain forward markets.

For Customers on Variable, Deemed or Rollover Rates

You are exposed to every wholesale price movement. If you have been waiting for prices to "settle," the World Bank baseline says energy prices are already +24% this year even in the best case. Fixing into a fixed-term contract provides budget certainty that variable rates cannot.

For High-Consumption and Multi-Site Users

The absolute cost of waiting is highest for you. A £10-20/MWh difference on a large consumption profile translates to tens of thousands of pounds. Review procurement strategy now. Consider whether a longer fix (36+ months) at current forward rates provides value.

For Hospitality, Care, Manufacturing and Retail

Sectors with thin margins and predictable consumption profiles benefit most from budget certainty. Energy cost volatility directly impacts profitability. The forward market discount for Cal-27 and Cal-28 may be particularly valuable for businesses that can align contract dates.

Key Commercial Explanation

If the customer can currently contract into a cheaper future market, waiting does not protect that price. Waiting simply moves the customer closer to delivery, where today's risk premium can become tomorrow's contract price.

Cheaper further out does not mean cheaper later. It means there may be an opportunity now, while that period is still priced as future delivery.
