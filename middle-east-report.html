NESO and marginal pricing — why it matters for business power

NESO is the National Energy System Operator. It watches the electricity system in real time and makes sure there is enough power available to meet demand. A margin notice is not a blackout warning, but it does mean the system is tighter and NESO wants more available power.

Under marginal pricing, the market price can be influenced by the last and often more expensive unit of generation needed to meet demand. During heatwaves, cooling demand can rise while evening solar output falls, so the system may need more flexible generation. That can support higher UK power prices and balancing costs.

Forward Market and Backwardation Explained

The forward market shows what gas or power is currently priced at for future delivery periods. That means a customer starting a new contract in 2027, 2028, 2029 or 2030 may not be buying the same market as a customer renewing in 2026.

Backwardation means the near-term market is more expensive than the future market. That is what the verified data shows today: NBP Winter-26 at 142.50p/th against Summer-27 at 93.50p/th and Summer-30 at 58.68p/th (internal broker / supplier evidence, 21 July AM), and Brent's front month near $88 against January-27 near $80 (ICE, 16–17 July).

2027 matters because it shows whether value starts before the longer-dated 2028 / 2029 / 2030 opportunity.
Backwardation is not a reason to wait. It is a reason to check whether the customer can act.

Futures Market Explanation

Futures and forward contracts are how the market prices energy for delivery in a future month, season or year. Suppliers buy this way, which is why the price a business is offered for a 2027 or 2028 start is set by the forward curve — not by today's headline.

Cheaper further out does not mean cheaper later.
The future market is only useful while the customer can still buy that future delivery period.
Waiting lets the market make the decision for the customer.

When geopolitical risk rises, the front of the curve usually moves first and fastest. The verified oil curve shows exactly that shape: Brent's front month near $88/bbl against ~$80 for January-27 and the high-$60s for 2028 WTI strips. The internal gas and power curves show the same shape — and it steepened overnight: W-26 gas rose to 142.50p/th while Summer-27 fell to 93.50p/th and Summer-29 to 58.00p/th. The curve is telling customers that the market expects risk to fade — the commercial question is whether a customer can lock that expectation before the market reprices it.

The LOA, in plain terms

The LOA does not switch the customer, commit the customer or agree a contract. It allows the consultant to confirm whether the opportunity applies.

  • Check usage profile and CED against the seasonal curve.
  • Confirm supplier appetite for future-start contracts — credit approval and usage profile affect access.
  • If the numbers work, a longer fix can still protect value; if they do not, the customer reverts to monitor with a defined trigger.

Section 14

Red Sea / Suez / SUMED Route Risk

Bab el-Mandeb is the southern gate of the Red Sea route. Suez is the northern gate. Yanbu is Saudi's Red Sea pressure valve. SUMED is part of the workaround for crude that cannot move cleanly through Suez on fully laden VLCCs.

VLCCs, ULCCs and why Suez is not a clean fix

  • A VLCC is a Very Large Crude Carrier. A VLCC typically carries around 2 million barrels of crude oil — larger VLCCs can be closer to 2.1 to 2.2 million barrels depending on ship size, crude density, draught and loading conditions.
  • A ULCC is an Ultra Large Crude Carrier. A ULCC can carry around 3 million to 3.7 million barrels of crude oil.
  • 2 million barrels is roughly 318 million litres; 3 million barrels is roughly 477 million litres; 3.7 million barrels is roughly 588 million litres. That volume is not practical to move by road.
  • Fully laden VLCCs are generally not a simple Suez Canal solution because of draught restrictions. ULCCs are even less of a clean Suez solution.
  • The workaround can involve partial offload, lightering, SUMED pipeline use, Suez transit lighter, or smaller tankers. This adds time, cost, capacity constraints and risk premium.
A fully laden VLCC is not a clean Suez solution.
A ULCC is even less of a clean Suez solution.
The cargo is too large to move practically by road. The truck example is only useful to show scale. It is not a real logistics solution.
The workaround is not simple trucking. It is pipeline, lightering, partial offload, SUMED and vessel logistics.
Suez becomes more important, but it is not a clean replacement for Bab el-Mandeb. Suez and SUMED can help, but they add friction. The workaround adds friction, and friction gets priced.
In a stressed market, friction is enough to support risk premium.

Supplier Price-Book Lag

Wholesale markets re-priced through June and July: NBP Winter-26 at ~140p/th, TTF near €58/MWh, Brent up ~14% in a month. Supplier price books typically move after wholesale, not before — that lag is where the commercial window sits. No verified evidence in this run shows supplier books have fully caught up with the latest leg of the move, but in fast markets books can reprice within days and quote validity windows shorten without announcement.

Supplier price-book lag is the opportunity and the danger.
Wholesale markets move first. Supplier books often follow.
Waiting for certainty can mean missing the price movement.
Once a supplier withdraws a rate, the customer cannot go back and buy yesterday's price.
  • Inside 90 days: act urgently — a live rate today can be withdrawn tomorrow morning.
  • Inside 12 months: review live options now while books may still lag the curve.
  • High usage, outside 12 months: test the backwardated forward curve for a future-start opportunity before it re-flattens upward.
