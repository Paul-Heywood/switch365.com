WHY THE FUTURES MARKET MATTERS

The futures market shows the price for energy delivered in future periods. A customer renewing in September 2027 is not only interested in today's price. They are interested in what the market is currently offering for September 2027 onwards. If 2027, 2028 or 2029 delivery periods are cheaper than the current market, the customer may be able to use that future pricing now, depending on supplier availability and contract rules.

Cheaper Further Out ≠ Cheaper Later
The future market is only useful while the customer can still buy that future delivery period.
Waiting Lets the Market Decide
As delivery periods move closer, they can absorb the same risk premium as today's market.
Review Now, Not Later

But supplier books may not move at the same speed as wholesale markets. Customer quotes may still reflect previous risk premium. Some supplier books may reprice down, hold, move cautiously, or withdraw offers quickly.
OPPORTUNITY
If wholesale markets moved down but supplier books have not fully caught up, customers may have a short window to secure pricing before supplier repricing lands.
RISK
If gas bounces back (as it has above £1), supplier books can reprice up quickly. Quote validity may shorten. Fixed offers may be withdrawn.
URGENCY
For customers within 90 days, failing to agree a new contract can push them toward deemed, rollover or out-of-contract rates.
Supplier book lag is not fully verified from public sources, but it is a normal commercial risk when wholesale markets move quickly. Supplier price-book lag can work both ways. It can create a short opportunity after market relief, but it can also close quickly if the headline fails. Waiting for certainty can mean missing the price movement. These prices could be lost as fast as they were gained. The best action is to test live pricing now.

How global developments reach UK business bills

UK gas reacts directly to NBP, which is influenced by TTF (European gas), LNG availability, storage levels, Norway flows, and weather. LNG disruption matters to UK gas because the UK imports significant LNG — reduced global supply means higher competition for cargoes, which pushes NBP up. Electricity follows gas because gas-fired generation sets the marginal price around 85% of the time in the UK. When NBP moves, supplier gas pricing moves. Because gas still influences UK power prices, electricity pricing can also be affected.

The most probable near-term path is fragile phased normalisation — talks continue but implementation is slow and disputed. This keeps a risk premium in energy markets. UK business customers should plan for continued volatility, not a quick return to pre-crisis pricing.
WHAT WOULD CHANGE THIS VIEW?

Evidence that would reduce risk or prove real de-escalation

Evidence That Would Reduce Risk
  • Verified direct US-Iran talks with signed implementation terms
  • Hormuz fully reopened with no route controls or permits required
  • No vessels stranded — all ships moving normally
  • IMO resumes escort operations safely
  • Shell or credible source confirms LNG supply fully normalised
  • IAEA and Iran agree inspection dates, sites and access terms
  • Israel agrees Lebanon withdrawal timeline
  • Houthis withdraw Red Sea threats
  • EU storage refills to above 60% by August
  • NBP holds below 95p/thm for several sessions
  • Supplier books reprice down across multiple suppliers
Evidence That Would Increase Risk
  • Direct talks permanently break down, military rhetoric escalates
  • New vessel attack in Hormuz closes strait more firmly
  • Qatar LNG restart delayed beyond stated timeline
  • IAEA inspection dispute deepens — no access agreed
  • Israel launches major Lebanon operation
  • Houthis expand Red Sea attacks beyond Israeli-linked vessels
  • European heatwave intensifies, storage refill stalls
  • Major Norway outage (Troll, Kollsnes, Nyhamna)
  • NBP spikes above 110p/thm on supply fears
  • Supplier books reprice up or withdraw offers
What is NOT enough to call the crisis over: Temporary diplomatic language is not enough. Technical talks are not enough. A sanctions waiver is not enough. A 60-day roadmap is not enough. Improving traffic is not enough if routes are controlled, supplier books have not caught up, LNG disruption remains material, gas fundamentals remain tight, nuclear implementation is disputed, and regional fronts remain unsettled. Operational evidence matters.
COMMERCIAL TAKEAWAY

What UK businesses should do now

Waiting Is Not Neutral

Yesterday was talk hopes. Today is gas bounce and talks doubt. Oil is still far better than the panic levels, but the market has started pushing back. Iran's refusal to hold direct talks with US envoys weakens the de-escalation story. Hormuz is moving, but partially reopened and unreliable is not safely normal. LNG disruption matters more to UK gas than the oil headline does. UK gas has moved back above £1 per therm. The bounce has started.

UK gas must be checked on its own. Do not assume gas has followed oil lower. The £1 per therm line matters, but it must be verified from current gas data, not inferred from oil. If UK gas has moved back above £1 per therm, the buying window is not closed, but the market has started pushing back. Storage refill is the hidden gas risk. Norway needs checking every morning. LNG competition can pull NBP back up even if oil calms.

For customers within 12 months of contract end date, the commercially sensible step is to review immediately, test available supplier pricing, compare short-term and longer-term pricing, and, where pricing and risk profile support it, consider fixing now and fixing longer before supplier books move further.

For customers within 90 days of renewal, the message should be stronger. They must agree a new contract before their current one ends, otherwise they may be exposed to deemed, rollover or out-of-contract rates, which can be materially more expensive than contracted pricing.

If the customer can currently contract into a cheaper future market, waiting does not protect that price. Waiting simply moves the customer closer to delivery, where today's risk premium can become tomorrow's contract price. Cheaper further out does not mean cheaper later. It means there may be an opportunity now, while that period is still priced as future delivery.

This is a review-now window, not a wait-and-see market.
