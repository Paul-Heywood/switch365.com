Middle East Energy Risk Update
Oil Softer, UK Gas Still Exposed, LNG Risk Still Live

Report Date: 06 July 2026 Research Window: 03–06 July 2026 Extended Verification: 30 June – 06 July 2026

Oil had a better weekend. OPEC+ approved another 188,000 bpd output-target increase from August, and Gulf exports showed recovery signs with Brent settling near $72 per barrel. Gas did not get the all-clear. Shell's LNG warning remains the key gas point: Hormuz is not only an oil route, it is also an LNG route, and severe disruption has shut in around one fifth of global monthly LNG supply. US LNG cargoes are being pulled towards higher Asian prices, with Europe's share of US LNG exports falling to just 42% in June. UK gas remains above £1 per therm on verified current data, and the bounce remains visible even while oil looks calmer. For UK business energy buyers, the window is open, but it is fragile. This is a review-now window, not a wait-and-see market.

Why the Futures Market Matters

The futures market shows the price for energy delivered in future periods. A September 2027 customer is not only interested in today's price. They are interested in what the market is currently offering for September 2027 onwards.

If 2027, 2028 or 2029 delivery periods are cheaper than the current market, the customer may be able to use that future pricing now, depending on supplier availability and contract rules.

Cheaper further out does not mean cheaper later. The future market is only useful while the customer can still buy that future delivery period. Waiting lets the market make the decision for the customer. This is not automatic advice to fix. It is a reason to review live prices now.

Commercial Takeaway for Salespeople

The customer should not only look at whether oil is down. They should look at what UK gas, UK power and the future market are offering now.

Oil is softer after OPEC+ added supply and Gulf exports improved, but gas still needs its own evidence. A softer Brent headline does not prove NBP or UK Power has become safe.

Shell's LNG warning is the key selling tool. Hormuz disruption has affected around one fifth of global monthly LNG supply, and LNG availability feeds into TTF, NBP and UK power.

US LNG cargoes being pulled towards higher Asian prices strengthens the competition risk for Europe. The UK and Europe compete with Asia for LNG, so NBP can remain exposed even when oil falls.

Cheaper further out does not mean cheaper later. The futures market is only useful while the customer can still buy that future delivery period. Waiting lets the market make the decision for the customer.

These prices could be lost as fast as they were gained.

Supplier Price-Book Lag

Why the Gap Between Wholesale and Customer Pricing Matters

Current Assessment

Supplier price-book lag is not fully verified from public sources, but it is a normal commercial risk when wholesale markets move quickly. Based on normal market behaviour:

• Wholesale gas (NBP) has moved quickly — front-month from crisis highs down to ~105p/th, but still above £1
• Supplier books may not have moved at the same speed as wholesale markets
• Customer quotes may still reflect previous risk premium
• Some supplier books may reprice down, hold, move cautiously or withdraw offers quickly
• If LNG disruption continues, Asian cargo competition rises, or Hormuz confidence worsens, suppliers can reprice back up sharply

Quote validity may shorten if volatility returns. Customers have a short window to secure pricing before supplier repricing lands.

Impact on UK Business Energy Prices

Why This Matters for UK SMEs

UK business gas prices are primarily driven by NBP (the National Balancing Point wholesale gas benchmark), not by Brent or WTI oil prices. This is the most important message for customers who see oil headlines and assume their gas quote will follow.

LNG is the bridge between Hormuz and UK gas. When Hormuz is disrupted, Middle East LNG supply is affected. That tightens global LNG availability. The UK and Europe compete with Asia for the same LNG cargoes. When Asia pays more (JKM at $17.33 vs TTF at $13.19), US cargoes go east, tightening European supply and supporting TTF and NBP.

UK electricity prices follow gas. Gas-fired power stations set the marginal price in most settlement periods. When gas is elevated, electricity is elevated. Wind and solar can suppress day-ahead spot prices on individual days, but forward curves retain the gas-linked risk premium.

OPEC+ adding oil supply does not solve LNG risk. More crude from Saudi Arabia does not create more LNG for European storage refill. Oil relief is helpful for sentiment, but it is not enough for gas safety.

Storage refill demand can keep gas pricing firm even if oil calms. EU storage at 49.7% and GB at 19.3% means Europe still needs meaningful injection before winter. That injection demand competes with Asian buyers for LNG cargoes.

SMEs on renewal, variable, deemed, rollover or out-of-contract rates are most exposed. Customers with contracts ending further out should not ignore today's NBP forward market — cheaper future periods may be accessible now.

Waiting for certainty can mean losing the cheaper forward curve. If the NBP forward market is cheaper than the near-term market, that does not mean the customer can wait and get the same price later. It means the customer may have an opportunity now, while that period is still priced as future delivery.

Commercial Takeaway

Guidance for UK Businesses and SMEs

For UK Business Energy Buyers

Volatility is still real. Oil is lower after a better weekend, but gas still needs its own evidence. OPEC+ adding supply helps oil but not necessarily UK gas. Gulf exports improving is positive, but it is not full gas-market safety.

UK gas must be checked directly and not inferred from oil. Shell's LNG warning is a direct NBP risk. US LNG cargoes moving towards Asia strengthen Europe's LNG competition risk. TTF is the European gas benchmark and NBP is the UK gas benchmark. If UK gas remains above £1 per therm, the bounce remains visible. If UK gas drops below £1 per therm, the window is open, but still not safe.

Lebanon, Gaza and Red Sea risks remain wider watch points, but should not dominate today's report unless they changed materially. Storage refill, LNG competition, heat and Norway flows are key gas risks.

The market can move before supplier books fully reprice. Buyers within 12 months of contract end should be reviewing immediately. Buyers within 90 days need to avoid drifting towards deemed, rollover or out-of-contract rates.

Where appropriate, the commercial case is to fix now and fix long — 36+ months — before further repricing hits. If NBP, UK power or the wider market further out is cheaper, this can strengthen the case for a longer-term fix.

Cheaper further out does not mean cheaper later. The futures market allows customers to buy energy for a future delivery period. If that future delivery period is cheaper today, the customer may be able to access that pricing now if their contract timing allows it. But if the customer waits, that future period moves closer and can become the current market.

This is a review-now window, not a wait-and-see market.
