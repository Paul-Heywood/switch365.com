★ Why the Futures Market Matters

The futures market shows the price for energy delivered in future periods. A customer renewing in September 2027 is not only interested in today's price. They are interested in what the market is currently offering for September 2027 onwards.

If 2027, 2028 or 2029 delivery periods are cheaper than the current market, the customer may be able to use that future pricing now, depending on supplier availability and contract rules.

The risk with waiting is that those future periods move closer every day. When they become the near-term market, they can pick up current risk, supplier caution, non-commodity changes, weather pressure or geopolitical premium.

⚠ Cheaper further out does not mean cheaper later

The future market is only useful while the customer can still buy that future delivery period.

Waiting lets the market make the decision for the customer

As time passes, future periods become the current market and can absorb the same risk premium.

This is not automatic advice to fix

It is a reason to review live prices now and compare options.

♻ Supplier Price-Book Lag and Procurement Window

Supplier price-book lag refers to the delay between wholesale market movements and supplier pricing updates. When wholesale markets move quickly, supplier books may not immediately reflect the new levels.

  • • UK power has moved +5.6% over 30 days; same lag risk applies
  • • Wholesale markets moved before supplier books during previous escalations
  • • Quote validity may shorten if volatility continues
  • • Fixed offers may be withdrawn if markets gap higher

What This Means

  • • Customers may have a short window to secure pricing
  • • Live broker or supplier quotes may still reflect pre-escalation levels
  • • The window narrows as books reprice
  • • Forward pricing can be lost if the customer waits
  • • Review immediately; do not wait for books to catch up

Supplier Book Lag — Procurement Window Risk

If oil, gas or power softens on peace headlines before the underlying risk is resolved, that can create a short procurement window. But supplier books can reprice again quickly if the deal stalls, Hormuz tension worsens, or military activity restarts. Supplier book lag is most useful to customers when they act before the supplier updates the book. Once the book moves, the customer cannot choose yesterday's price.

This is the danger of waiting for certainty. By the time certainty arrives, the price may already have moved.
