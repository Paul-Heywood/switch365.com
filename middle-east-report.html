? What This Means

The futures market is the market price for energy delivered in a future period. When the near-term market is expensive but the further-out market is cheaper, it usually means today's price contains more immediate risk: supply pressure, shipping uncertainty, weather risk, storage concern or geopolitical premium.

For a customer whose contract starts 12 months from now, the relevant question is not only what energy costs today. It is what the market is offering for their delivery period. If that future period is cheaper, the customer may be able to contract into that lower forward market now.

The key point: waiting does not mean today's cheaper future price becomes tomorrow's available price. As time passes, that future period becomes the near-term market. If the same risks are still present, or if supplier books reprice, that cheaper future price can disappear.

! Why Waiting Can Be Expensive

Customers often assume that if the 12-month or 18-month market is cheaper today, they can wait and pick it up later. That is not how the market works. The future price is available because the customer is buying that future delivery period now. If the customer waits, the delivery period moves closer, current risk gets built into the price, and supplier books can change.

In simple terms, the cheaper forward market can become the expensive current market.

£ Commercial Message for Customers

If your contract end date is far enough out, you may be able to contract into a market that is cheaper than the near-term market. That can support fixing longer where the price, contract date, usage profile and risk position make sense.

Do not let the market make the decision for you by holding off. Review the position now, test live supplier pricing, compare short-term and longer-term options, and decide while the forward market is still available.

Cheaper further out does not mean cheaper later.
The future market is only useful if you act while it is still future pricing.
Waiting means the customer stays exposed while today's future market becomes tomorrow's current market.
This is not automatic advice to fix. It is a reason to review now.

What the ceasefire actually means on the ground.

📋What the Ceasefire Says

The two-week ceasefire agreed 8 April 2026 was extended indefinitely by Trump on 21 April. It covers US-Iran hostilities but does not include Lebanon, where Israel-Hezbollah fighting continues. The framework calls for halting hostilities, reopening Hormuz, and a 15-20 day negotiation period.

Key sticking point: nuclear enrichment. Trump demands no enrichment and removal of all "deeply buried nuclear dust." Iran's 10-point counter-proposal demands war reparations, sanctions lifting, and sovereignty over Hormuz management.

⚠What Is Excluded or Disputed

  • • Lebanon is not included — Trump confirmed: "Because of Hezbollah. They were not included in the deal."
  • • Israeli bombing continues — strikes on Iranian and Iran-linked targets persist
  • • US defensive strikes ongoing — CENTCOM hit missile sites 25 May
  • • Nuclear terms unresolved — Trump seeking amendments; Iran in "caves," response delayed
No major escalation is not the same as real de-escalation. The absence of a full-scale US attack does not mean the crisis is resolved. Hormuz remains restricted, military assets remain deployed, and the core dispute over Iran's nuclear programme is still being negotiated.

🎯Commercial Interpretation

Trump delaying a strike means the military option is deferred, not cancelled. Gulf-state mediation reduces immediate risk but prolongs uncertainty. The market is in limbo: soft enough to create a potential procurement window, but volatile enough that the window can close quickly if talks fail.

For UK businesses, this means: treat current market softness as a potential procurement window, not proof the crisis is over. If a deal is signed and Hormuz reopens fully, prices could soften further. If talks fail and strikes resume, prices can spike rapidly. Either outcome favours having a procurement plan in place.

Supplier Dynamics

Supplier Price-Book Lag

Why wholesale price changes do not always reach customer quotes immediately.

⏳How Supplier Pricing Works

Energy suppliers do not update customer prices in real-time with wholesale markets. They maintain price books — internal pricing matrices that are updated periodically based on wholesale cost movements, risk appetite, hedging positions and competitive positioning.

When wholesale prices move rapidly (up or down), there is typically a lag before those changes flow through to customer quotes. This lag can range from days to weeks depending on the supplier, the magnitude of the wholesale move, and market volatility.

The procurement window: When wholesale prices have fallen but supplier books have not yet fully adjusted, there can be a short window where customer quotes are based on older, higher wholesale levels while the market has already moved lower. This window closes once suppliers reprice.

📉Current Situation — Potential Window

Wholesale gas has softened ~5.7% this week. Supplier pricing may not have fully caught up. This creates a potential procurement window for customers whose contracts are ending soon enough to access current supplier books.

  • • Wholesale down, supplier books catching up: The gap between wholesale and retail pricing may temporarily favour buyers
  • • Window duration uncertain: Suppliers reprice at different speeds. Some daily, some weekly, some on threshold triggers
  • • Volatility can reverse the window: If wholesale moves back up before books adjust, the window slams shut or reverses
  • • Only testable with live quotes: You cannot know where supplier books sit without requesting actual pricing

⚠ Commercial Takeaway

Do not assume that because wholesale prices have moved, supplier prices have moved the same amount in the same direction. The only way to know what pricing is available is to test it.

If wholesale has softened and supplier books have not yet fully caught up, there may be a short procurement window. That window is only discoverable through live quotes. It closes when suppliers reprice.

Action: request pricing now, compare multiple terms, and decide based on actual quotes rather than wholesale direction alone.

  • Review procurement options immediately
  • Request live supplier pricing for multiple contract lengths
  • Compare 1-year, 2-year, and 3-year fixed options

⏳ If your contract ends in 12-24 months

  • Monitor the market but do not disengage
  • Set price alerts at key levels
  • Understand which forward season your renewal falls into
  • Be ready to act if a procurement window opens
  • Consider whether market developments change your risk tolerance
You have more time, but "cheaper further out" only works if you act while it is still the further-out market. Set reminders to review monthly.

⚠ If you are currently out of contract or on deemed rates

  • This is urgent — deemed rates are typically 30-50% above fixed contract pricing
  • Secure a fixed contract immediately, even if short-term
  • Any fixed rate is likely better than deemed/variable rates
  • Do not try to time the market from deemed rates
  • Fix now, review at renewal — two decisions, not one
Every day on deemed rates costs money. The question is not "is this the best price ever?" but "is this better than what I am paying now?"

📋Procurement Checklist

  • 1. Confirm your contract end date and notice period requirements
  • 2. Request live pricing from multiple suppliers for 1, 2, and 3-year terms
  • 3. Compare total contract cost, not just unit rate (standing charges, FIT, etc.)
  • 4. Check if your end date aligns with cheaper forward delivery periods
  • 5. Understand quote validity periods — in volatile markets they can be short
