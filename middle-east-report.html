What the gas table means: Gas prices are not one single number. The table shows different future delivery periods. Near-term contracts (Jul-26, Winter-26) are priced higher than further-out periods (Summer-27, Cal-27) because the market prices current-period risk more heavily. Cheaper gas further out does not mean cheaper gas later. It means there may be an opportunity now, while that period is still priced as future delivery. As those future periods move closer, they can absorb the same risk premium that exists in today's market.

● Why the Futures Market Matters

The futures market shows the price for energy delivered in future periods. A customer renewing from September 2027 is not only interested in today's price — they should look at what the market is currently offering for September 2027 onwards.

If 2027, 2028 or 2029 delivery periods are cheaper than the current market, the customer may be able to use that future pricing now, depending on supplier availability and contract rules. The chart below shows the NBP forward curve shape as of mid-June 2026.

Cheaper further out does not mean cheaper later. The future market is only useful while the customer can still buy that future delivery period. Waiting lets the market make the decision for the customer. As time passes, that future period becomes the near-term market and can absorb the same risk premium.

NBP Forward Curve (approximate midpoints) · Source: ICE, Argus

This is not automatic advice to fix. It is a reason to review live prices now. If your contract end date sits in a future period that is currently cheaper than the near-term market, there may be a commercial case for a longer-term fix. But that case depends on live supplier pricing, your risk profile, and your budget requirements. Test the market, compare options, and decide — do not wait for certainty that may never arrive.

Ceasefire Reality Check

What the ceasefire was understood to cover: The April 7 ceasefire halted direct US-Iran and Iran-Israel military operations. It was extended and renewed multiple times through May. A separate Lebanon track involved Israel-Hezbollah cessation, though Netanyahu repeatedly stated this did not apply to Lebanon operations against Hezbollah.

What is excluded or disputed: The ceasefire never fully stopped Israel-Hezbollah exchanges in Lebanon. Israeli strikes in southern Lebanon continued throughout May and early June. Hezbollah never accepted the Washington-brokered deal of 1-4 June.

What actually happened 5-8 June: Direct US-Iran military engagement (drones intercepted, radar struck, missiles fired at Kuwait/Bahrain). Direct Iran-Israel missile exchange (~30 missiles). Israeli strikes inside Iran including Mahshahr petrochemical complex. Houthi missile fire from Yemen.

No major escalation is not the same as real de-escalation. Limited escalation is still escalation when it involves direct US-Iran action, direct Iran-Israel fire, Gulf military targets, shipping routes or energy infrastructure. The deal framework announced 14-15 June is promising but the memorandum is not yet signed, and 60 days of further negotiations lie ahead.
Commercial point: Even if the deal is signed today (19 June), full normalisation of Hormuz shipping, insurance premiums, LNG flows, and European gas storage refill will take weeks or months. The market may price optimism quickly, but physical supply normalisation moves slowly. Headline relief is not the same as physical supply improvement.

Shipping and Choke Point Status

Strait of Hormuz

The Strait handles ~20 million barrels/day of oil and significant LNG volumes. It has been effectively closed to normal commercial traffic since late February. Trump announced end to US naval blockade on 14 June; deal signing scheduled 19 June. Even if reopened, selective access is not the same as normal global shipping access. Insurance premiums, shipowner caution, and Iranian management protocols will slow normalisation.

Source: EIA · gCaptain

Bab el-Mandeb / Red Sea

Houthis announced "complete and total ban" on Israeli navigation on 8 June, after firing 2 missiles at Israel. This was the first Houthi attack since April. If operations resume against commercial shipping (not just Israeli-linked), a dual choke point crisis would severely impact LNG and oil flows to Europe, including UK-bound cargoes. LNG from Qatar to Europe transits both Hormuz and Red Sea or takes longer routes around Africa.

Source: Long War Journal

Impact on LNG

QatarEnergy declared force majeure in March. Gulf LNG supplies fell by ~9 bcm in May (45% of EU monthly demand). Alternative sources (US, Canada) offset ~90% of losses, per IEA analyst Greg Molnar. Asian LNG benchmark (JKM) rose 94% in March; European TTF rose 59%. LNG competition could intensify this summer if Asian heatwaves coincide with European refill needs.

Source: GMK Center

Impact on UK Energy

UK imports LNG via South Hook, Dragon, and Isle of Grain terminals. UK domestic North Sea production covers ~40% of gas demand; remainder imported via pipeline (Norway, continental Europe) and LNG. NBP is the UK wholesale benchmark and directly influences supplier pricing. Any disruption to LNG supply routes or European gas storage refill raises NBP and therefore UK power prices.

Key commercial point: Chinese priority or approved vessel passage through Hormuz does not mean Europe or the UK has normalised supply conditions. The UK energy system is exposed to NBP, which tracks TTF, which tracks global LNG prices. Even after Hormuz reopens, the knock-on effects on storage, insurance, and shipping schedules will persist for months.

Supplier Price-Book Lag and Procurement Window

Has NBP moved before supplier books? NBP day-ahead has fallen ~14% from ~112 to ~97 p/th over two weeks. Front-month and seasonal contracts have moved similarly. Wholesale markets typically reprice faster than supplier price books.

Has UK power moved before supplier books? Power baseload fell from ~£108 to ~£93/MWh. The same lag dynamic applies — supplier books may not have fully caught up.

How quickly do books update? Typically hours to days for prompt products; days to weeks for forward seasonal and calendar-year products. During high volatility, quote validity may shorten from 48 hours to 24 hours or same-day.

Supplier book lag is not fully verified from public sources, but it is a normal commercial risk when wholesale markets move quickly. Based on standard supplier behaviour, books may take 1-5 trading days to reflect wholesale moves of this magnitude.

Potential procurement window: If wholesale markets have softened on deal hopes but supplier books have not fully caught up, customers may have a short window to secure pricing at levels that still reflect some of the recent wholesale decline. If the deal is signed as scheduled (19 June) and holds, this window could narrow quickly as books reprice upward on optimism. If the deal falters, wholesale could spike and books may lag in the opposite direction.

Quote validity risk: Suppliers may shorten quote validity periods if volatility returns. Fixed offers may be withdrawn if market conditions shift rapidly. The safest approach is to request quotes with the longest validity available and be prepared to decide within 24-48 hours during elevated volatility.

Market Interpretation

What the market is pricing now

The futures market is pricing a partial de-escalation via the US-Iran deal, but not a full return to pre-war supply conditions. NBP at ~97 p/th is well below the ~140-160 p/th range seen during peak March-April panic, but still roughly double pre-war levels (~45-55 p/th). The backwardated curve (near-term > further-out) reflects current-period risk premium.

Are traders fading headlines or taking escalation seriously?

The ~15% drop in NBP and Brent over two weeks suggests traders are pricing in deal optimism. However, the fact that prices remain 50-100% above pre-war levels indicates the market does not believe the crisis is fully resolved. The IEA's warning that "the market will remain severely undersupplied through end of Q3" supports this cautious view.

What would trigger the next leg up or down?

Down (lower prices): Successful deal signing and implementation; Hormuz reopens with normal commercial traffic; no further military exchanges; European storage refill accelerates.

Up (higher prices): Deal collapses or is delayed; military exchanges resume; Houthis escalate Red Sea attacks; European storage refill disappoints; winter demand surprises.

Does the forward market support a longer fix?

Yes, conditionally. Summer 2027 NBP at ~81 p/th is ~24% below Winter 2026 at ~106 p/th. Cal-2027 at ~82 p/th offers a discount to near-term. If your contract end date falls in 2027 or 2028, the forward market may support a 2-3 year fix that accesses these cheaper future periods. But this requires live supplier pricing to confirm — the futures curve is a benchmark, not a quote.

Impact on UK Business Energy Prices

Why UK gas reacts to NBP, LNG and European storage risk: The UK is connected to European gas markets via interconnectors and imports LNG via three major terminals. NBP is the UK wholesale benchmark. When LNG supply is disrupted (via Hormuz or Red Sea), European competition for alternative cargoes intensifies, pushing TTF higher, which pulls NBP up with it.

Why electricity follows gas: Gas-fired generation sets the marginal price for UK electricity for a significant portion of hours. Even with record solar and strong wind, when renewable output drops, gas fills the gap. NBP therefore drives UK power pricing.

Why NBP matters to UK supplier pricing: Energy suppliers buy gas and power on wholesale markets (or hedge via futures). When NBP rises, their input costs rise. This flows through to business customer quotes, typically with a lag of days to weeks.

Why UK power futures matter: A customer renewing in September 2027 should look not just at today's day-ahead price but at what the market is offering for September 2027, 2028 and 2029 delivery. If those future periods are cheaper, a longer fix may be advantageous.

Why waiting is risky: If you are within 12 months of contract end and wait for "certainty," you give up the opportunity to contract into cheaper forward pricing. As time passes, that forward period becomes the near-term market and can absorb the same risk premium. The futures market is only useful while you can still buy that future delivery period.

Why selective Hormuz access is not normalisation: Even if the deal reopens Hormuz, shipping protocols, insurance costs, war-risk premiums, and shipowner caution will persist. Chinese-approved vessel passage does not equal normal European supply. LNG cargo scheduling, port congestion, and European storage refill all take time.

SMEs most exposed: Businesses on variable, deemed, rollover or out-of-contract rates are most exposed to wholesale volatility. Multi-site operators, hospitality, care, manufacturing, and retail with thin margins should prioritise securing fixed pricing.

Commercial Takeaway for UK Businesses

Volatility is real. NBP is a key UK gas benchmark. UK power futures are key for electricity renewal decisions. The market can move before supplier books fully reprice. Buyers within 12 months of contract end should be reviewing immediately.

Where appropriate, the commercial case is to fix now and fix long (36+ months) before further repricing hits — but only where current forward pricing, supplier availability, and your risk profile support it. If NBP or UK power further out is cheaper than near-term, this can strengthen the case for a longer-term fix, especially for SMEs that want budget certainty.

Cheaper further out does not mean cheaper later. The futures market allows customers to buy energy for a future delivery period. If that future delivery period is cheaper today, the customer may be able to access that pricing now if their contract timing allows it. But if the customer waits, that future period moves closer and can become the current market. If volatility, supply risk or supplier caution is still present, the cheaper price may disappear.

If the NBP forward market is cheaper than the near-term market, that does not mean the customer can wait and get the same price later. It means the customer may have an opportunity now, while that period is still priced as future delivery.

If the UK power forward market is cheaper than the near-term market, that does not mean the customer can wait and get the same price later. It means the customer may have an opportunity now, while that period is still priced as future delivery.

Waiting is not neutral. It is a bet that the World Bank baseline, current NBP pricing, current UK power pricing, unresolved military escalation, shipping risk, supplier price-book lag, and the unsettled diplomatic position are all overstating the risk.

For customers within 12 months of contract end date, the commercially sensible step is to review immediately, test available supplier pricing, compare short-term and longer-term pricing, and, where pricing and risk profile support it, consider fixing now and fixing longer before supplier books move further.
