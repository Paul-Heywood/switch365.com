Market Structure

Why the Futures Market Matters

The futures market shows the price for energy delivered in future periods. A September 2027 customer is not only interested in today’s price. They are interested in what the market is currently offering for September 2027 onwards. If 2027, 2028 or 2029 delivery periods are cheaper than the current market, the customer may be able to use that future pricing now, depending on supplier availability and contract rules.

The risk with waiting is that those future periods move closer every day. When they become the near-term market, they can pick up current risk, supplier caution, non-commodity changes, weather pressure or geopolitical premium.

Cheaper further out does not mean cheaper later. The future market is only useful while the customer can still buy that future delivery period. Waiting lets the market make the decision for the customer. This is not automatic advice to fix. It is a reason to review live prices now.

If wholesale markets have moved but supplier books have not fully caught up, customers may have a short window to secure pricing before supplier repricing lands. Quote validity may shorten if volatility returns.

⚠️ The Risk

If Hormuz worsens, ship incidents continue, nuclear inspections fail, Lebanon escalates, storage tightens, LNG competition rises or Norway flows reduce, suppliers can reprice back up sharply. The window can close quickly.

Supplier price-book lag can work both ways. It can create a short opportunity after market relief, but it can also close quickly if the headline fails. Waiting for certainty can mean missing the price movement. These prices could be lost as fast as they were gained.

Customer Impact

Impact on UK Business Energy Prices

How global developments flow through to UK business contracts

Why UK Gas Reacts to These Events

The UK is a net gas importer. NBP (National Balancing Point) is the main UK wholesale gas benchmark. When NBP moves, supplier gas pricing can move. NBP reacts to:

🛢️
LNG & Shipping
Hormuz disruption affects ~20% of global LNG. Qatari curtailment tightens supply.
🛢️
European Storage
47.2% fill, below norm. Low storage = higher refill demand = firmer prices.
🛢️
TTF Linkage
NBP tracks Dutch TTF. EU gas at 40.96 EUR/MWh directly affects UK pricing.
🛢️
Norway Flows
~30% of EU gas imports. Any Gassco outage immediately affects NBP.
🛢️
Supplier Books
Suppliers add margin, risk premium and non-commodity costs to wholesale.

Why electricity follows gas: Gas-fired generation sets the marginal power price ~85% of the time in Britain. When NBP rises, the cost of gas-fired power rises, pulling electricity prices up with it. The UK is more reliant on gas for electricity than France or Germany.

⚠️ SME Exposure

SMEs on renewal, variable, deemed, rollover or out-of-contract rates are most exposed. There is no price cap for businesses. Suppliers tailor contracts to each business based on usage, contract terms, location and market conditions. Energy suppliers do not have a duty to offer a business an energy contract — unlike households where there is an obligation to supply. If the current contract ends without a new one agreed, the business may drift to deemed, rollover or out-of-contract rates, which can be materially more expensive.

For Customers Within 12 Months of Contract End

The commercially sensible step is to review immediately, test available supplier pricing, compare short-term and longer-term pricing, and, where pricing and risk profile support it, consider fixing now and fixing longer (36+ months) before supplier books move further.

If the customer can currently contract into a cheaper future market, waiting does not protect that price. Waiting simply moves the customer closer to delivery, where today’s risk premium can become tomorrow’s contract price. Cheaper further out does not mean cheaper later. It means there may be an opportunity now, while that period is still priced as future delivery.

The message is stronger. You must agree a new contract before your current one ends. Otherwise you may be exposed to deemed, rollover or out-of-contract rates, which can be materially more expensive than contracted pricing. The risk is no longer only market movement. It is also the risk of failing to agree a new contract in time.
