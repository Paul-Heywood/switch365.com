System tightness, NESO and marginal pricing

NESO is the National Energy System Operator. It watches the electricity system in real time and makes sure there is enough power available to meet demand. A margin notice is not a blackout warning, but it does mean the system is tighter and NESO wants more available power.

Under marginal pricing, the market price can be influenced by the last and often more expensive unit of generation needed to meet demand. During heatwaves, cooling demand can rise while evening solar output falls, so the system may need more flexible generation. That can support higher UK power prices and balancing costs.

  • A third summer heatwave prompted NESO to forecast tight electricity margins for a Thursday evening peak in the week of 06–10/07.
  • Heavy nuclear outage slate — Sizewell B units, Heysham 1, Heysham 2 and Hartlepool offline — leaves the system leaning on gas-fired generation when wind dips.
  • With NBP above 150p/th, gas-for-power sets an expensive marginal unit more often.

Forward Market And Backwardation Explained

Why the curve shape — not just the headline price — drives the procurement decision.

The forward market shows what gas or power is currently priced at for future delivery periods. That means a customer starting a new contract in 2027, 2028, 2029 or 2030 may not be buying the same market as a customer renewing in 2026.

Backwardation means the near-term market is more expensive than the future market.

“2027 matters because it shows whether value starts before the longer-dated 2028 / 2029 / 2030 opportunity.”
“Backwardation is not a reason to wait. It is a reason to check whether the customer can act.”
  • Verified evidence of backwardation today: NBP Q1-27 (146.10p) trades below the September–November 2026 strip (151–155p) on ICE, 22/07.

The chart shows whether the market is backwardated. If the near-term periods are materially more expensive than 2027, 2028, 2029 and 2030 delivery periods, the market is offering cheaper future delivery than near-term delivery. That matters because a customer renewing now may be exposed to the expensive front of the curve, while a customer with a later CED may be able to access a cheaper future delivery period now.

2027 matters because it shows whether value starts before the longer-dated 2028 / 2029 story. 2028, 2029 and 2030 matter because they show whether the future market still offers a lower-cost delivery opportunity.

Cheaper further out does not mean cheaper later. It means there may be an opportunity now, while that period is still priced as future delivery.

The mistake customers make is thinking cheaper 2027 / 2028 / 2029 pricing means they can wait. It does not. It means the cheaper part of the curve may be available now, and waiting gives the market more time to take it away.

Futures Market Explanation

“Cheaper further out does not mean cheaper later.”
“The future market is only useful while the customer can still buy that future delivery period.”
“Waiting lets the market make the decision for the customer.”

A futures price is today’s tradable price for energy delivered in a future period. It is not a forecast of what that period will cost when it arrives. When the front of the curve carries a war-risk premium and the back does not, the discount exists only while suppliers are still willing to sell that future period. Once the future period becomes the live market — or once suppliers withdraw long-dated offers — the opportunity is gone.

British Gas Renewal Window

Why renewal windows turn forward-market value into a practical customer action.

What is verified

  • British Gas business customers can get a renewal quote up to 12 months before their contract end date (verified on the British Gas business website in this run); the formal renewal offer arrives around 60 days out.
  • A move to an 18-month window could not be verified from public sources in this run — if an 18-month option exists for a customer, confirm it directly with British Gas or the broker channel before using it in a conversation.
  • The renewal window matters because it turns the forward market into a practical customer action: if the forward curve is cheaper further out, eligible customers may be able to secure their next contract earlier than expected.

Duration Risk Assessment

Does this now look like a longer conflict? Assessed against four scenarios — short-lived overnight escalation; stop-start days-to-weeks; rolling weeks-to-months; larger regional conflict risk.

“The evidence points towards duration risk, not certainty.”
“Markets price duration when political, military, shipping and funding signals all point in the same direction.”
“US funding requests do not prove the war will last months, but they do show that Washington is preparing for costs beyond a single strike cycle.”

1 · Short-lived overnight escalation

Inconsistent with current evidence. Five months in, routes are abnormal, oil has settled above $100 and further funding is being requested.

2 · Stop-start days-to-weeks

Matches the pattern since April — truce windows followed by renewed strikes — but today’s price and route confirmation points beyond a pure stop-start read.

3 · Rolling weeks-to-months Best fit today

Kpler models Middle East output outages of ~8 MBD (forecast ~10 MBD in August) with normalisation pushed to early 2027. One-tanker Hormuz days fit a rolling conflict, not a fading one.

4 · Larger regional conflict risk

A risk to monitor, not the base case: Gulf sirens (Bahrain, 18/07), interceptions over Kuwait and Jordan, Houthi second front, and IRGC rhetoric on UK bases.

Funding signals

$37.5bn spent to date; nearly $90bn additional funding requested (Reuters); $87.6bn supplemental framing (AP); $1.5tn FY2027 defence request. Washington is budgeting beyond a single strike cycle.

Shipping not normalising

Hormuz at its lowest crossing count since 7 May; Bab el-Mandeb traffic down ~29% in a day; no LNG transits of Hormuz since 11/07. Lack of route normalisation is itself a duration signal.

What would shorten the conflict: an implemented ceasefire with verified shipping normalisation; Houthi blockade threat withdrawn; Hormuz crossings recovering; CPC returning to normal.

What would extend it: further tanker attacks; strikes on infrastructure executed; Congressional funding fights that harden both sides; Kazakhstan’s Black Sea route staying impaired.

Do not say the conflict will definitely last until 2028. Present duration risk as the market risk now being priced — not as a forecast.

Red Sea / Suez / SUMED Route Risk

The route chain

  • Bab el-Mandeb is the southern gate of the Red Sea route — now a route-risk market after the Encelia strike and the Houthi blockade declaration.
  • Suez is the northern gate — it can absorb some rerouted volume, but draft and design limits apply.
  • Yanbu is Saudi Arabia’s Red Sea pressure valve — the east-west pipeline feeds crude to Yanbu, which is exactly where the targeted and turning-back tankers loaded.
  • SUMED is part of the crude workaround from Red Sea to Mediterranean — useful, but not a like-for-like substitute for normal transit.

Why the workaround is not free

  • Fully laden VLCCs are not a clean Suez solution — and ULCCs are even less clean.
  • Rerouting Yanbu cargoes to Asia via Suez / Gibraltar / the Cape of Good Hope adds time, fuel, insurance, congestion and price risk.
  • War-risk insurance on the Omani-side Hormuz route is not pricing meaningfully lower (Kpler) — every workaround carries a cost layer.
“Suez can help, but it is not a magic fix.”
“Fully laden VLCCs are not a simple Suez solution.”
“The workaround adds friction, and friction gets priced.”

“Supplier price-book lag is the opportunity and the danger.”
“Wholesale markets move first. Supplier books often follow.”

Broker guidance in the current market is that quotes commonly expire same-day or within 24 hours — evidence of supplier caution and short validity windows. Some rates are therefore still live below the new wholesale reality; that gap is the opportunity. The same gap is the danger for any customer who drifts.
