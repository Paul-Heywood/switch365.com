Executive Summary

The 24-hour period from 5 May to 6 May 2026 has been defined by a striking paradox: genuine military escalation in the Strait of Hormuz alongside relative market calm in benchmark energy contracts. US naval forces engaged Iranian fast boats in Hormuz over the bank holiday weekend, while UAE air defences intercepted Iranian missiles for the first time since the notional ceasefire period began [67]. These developments represent a direct intensification of kinetic activity in the world's most critical energy chokepoint.

Yet the benchmark Summer-2027 (Sum-27) NBP gas contract fell 0.5% to 85.96 pence per therm, marking a second consecutive day of decline, and Brent crude moderated to approximately $113 per barrel, down from a $118 spike on 29 April [67]. This apparent disconnect between geopolitical escalation and market price response is the central analytical challenge of this reporting period.

World Bank Baseline: The Central Case is Adverse

The World Bank's Commodity Markets Outlook April 2026 projects a 24% surge in global energy prices for 2026 under its baseline scenario, which explicitly assumes that "the most acute disruptions caused by the war in the Middle East end in May" and that shipping through the Strait of Hormuz "gradually returns to near pre-war levels by late 2026" [534] [535].

This baseline—described by the World Bank as its most likely central case—establishes that even under relatively optimistic conflict resolution assumptions, UK business energy buyers face materially higher prices than pre-war forecasts.

Ceasefire Status Analysis

Timeline and Coverage

The ceasefire began with indefinite extension agreed for Pakistan-mediated talks. However, significant ambiguity exists regarding scope and coverage. Israel has publicly stated that Lebanon is not part of the ceasefire agreement, while Hezbollah has rejected ceasefire-related diplomacy, creating multiple potential flashpoints [67].

Strait of Hormuz Status

The status of the Strait of Hormuz remains ambiguous under the ceasefire terms. While not explicitly closed, access is restricted with naval enforcement measures, inspections, and ongoing threats to commercial shipping. The market has interpreted this as functional disruption rather than formal blockade.

Breaches and Escalations

Multiple events constitute clear breaches of ceasefire spirit if not letter: US-Iran naval engagement, UAE missile interceptions, Revolutionary Guard attacks on commercial vessels, and continued threats to shipping. These actions suggest the ceasefire is functioning more as a temporary pause in certain areas rather than comprehensive conflict resolution.

Commercial Implications

The ceasefire's ambiguous status creates asymmetric risk for energy markets. While providing temporary stability, the multiple unresolved issues and ongoing breaches suggest the potential for rapid escalation remains high. For UK business customers, this means the repricing window could close suddenly if the ceasefire collapses completely, making delay a high-risk strategy.

Split-Curve Dynamics

The current market configuration shows front-end firming and back-end stability. Front-month gas at 112.10p is up 2.1% week-on-week, winter gas up 2.5%, while Sum-27 at 85.96p is only 7% above pre-conflict levels and Calendar-2027 gas is flat on the week [67].

This creates differentiated exposure by contract timing. Customers with summer 2026 and winter 2026/27 renewals face acute repricing risk, while those with longer-dated start dates may still access relatively stable forward pricing—provided they act before supplier books adjust.

Current market pricing appears lighter than the World Bank baseline implies. The institution's 24% energy price surge projection for 2026—under a scenario assuming May disruption easing—suggests markets may be underpricing the structural impact of even a resolved conflict.

Impact on UK Business Energy Prices

Supply Chain Mechanism

LNG Competition Effect

UK gas follows European gas, which follows LNG availability. Disruption in Gulf LNG supplies forces European buyers to compete more aggressively for alternative cargoes, pushing up prices.

Storage Dynamics

Below-average European gas storage increases price sensitivity. Any supply disruption creates disproportionate price impact as markets factor in winter security of supply concerns.

Shipping Cost Pass-Through

Elevated war risk premiums, rerouting costs, and charter rate increases feed through into delivered gas costs, ultimately reflected in supplier pricing to UK businesses.

Pricing Mechanism

Gas-to-Power Linkage

UK electricity pricing is fundamentally driven by gas-fired generation costs. Gas price movements directly translate to power price changes, with multiplier effects during tight supply periods.

Supplier Book Lag

Major suppliers update price books within 24–72 hours in normal conditions. During acute volatility, this can compress to hours or see offers withdrawn entirely [529].

Repricing Window

The window between wholesale market movements and supplier price-book updates creates narrow execution opportunities that are compressing as suppliers adapt risk management protocols.

Critical Timing Consideration

The World Bank baseline projection fundamentally alters the risk-reward calculus for contract timing decisions. The 24% energy price surge is not a worst-case scenario; it is the institution's central case, explicitly conditioned on May disruption easing.

For UK businesses, this means that waiting for "clarity" or "stabilisation" is a misreading of the risk distribution: the baseline itself is adverse, and the probability-weighted outcome includes substantial upside if the May easing assumption proves optimistic. The commercial question is not whether to hedge against extreme scenarios, but whether to lock in protection against a baseline that is already materially worse than pre-crisis expectations.
