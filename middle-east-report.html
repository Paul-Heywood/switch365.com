1.1 Primary Research Window

The reporting period from 18–19 May 2026 has been dominated by a dramatic sequence of diplomatic and military developments that together have kept global energy markets in a state of acute uncertainty. The single most significant event was President Donald Trump's announcement on the evening of 18 May that he had called off a planned military strike on Iran scheduled for Tuesday 19 May, following direct requests from the leaders of Qatar, Saudi Arabia and the United Arab Emirates.

The Gulf leaders asked for a pause of two to three days to allow what they described as "serious negotiations" to continue, expressing confidence that a deal could be reached that would be "very acceptable" to the United States and the broader Middle East. Trump, in a detailed Truth Social post, stated that he had instructed Defense Secretary Pete Hegseth, General Dan Caine (Chairman of the Joint Chiefs of Staff), and the U.S. military to stand down from the planned attack, but crucially added that the military had been ordered to remain ready to launch "a full, large scale assault of Iran, on a moment's notice" if negotiations fail to produce an acceptable agreement.

The announcement triggered an immediate reaction in oil markets, with Brent crude futures falling more than $2 per barrel within minutes of Trump's post, from $108.83 to around $106, before partially recovering to settle at $107.25 on Monday. By Tuesday 19 May, Brent had settled at approximately $110.21 per barrel, still reflecting significant geopolitical risk premium but showing the market's sensitivity to any signals of potential de-escalation. However, the underlying physical conditions in the Strait of Hormuz remain virtually unchanged. The waterway continues to be effectively closed to normal commercial traffic, with approximately 1,550 vessels stranded and 22,500 mariners trapped, according to confirmation from General Dan Caine on 6 May. Traffic through Hormuz has been running at approximately 5% of pre-war average across April, per vessel transponder data published by Kpler.

Hormuz Reality Check

The only meaningful change on the water has been the commencement of selective passage for Chinese-linked vessels under Iranian management protocols, with Iranian state media reporting that more than 30 ships had been permitted to pass since 13 May, including a Chinese supertanker carrying approximately 2 million barrels of Iraqi crude that had been stranded in the Persian Gulf for over two months.

On the diplomatic front, Iran submitted a revised 14-point peace proposal to Pakistani mediators on 18 May, according to the semi-official Tasnim news agency. The proposal reportedly focuses on negotiations to end the war and includes demands for U.S. "trust-building" measures, including the suspension of oil sanctions during the negotiation period. However, the proposal's status remains firmly in the "under review" category—neither accepted, agreed, nor implemented. Trump's own words on Monday evening captured the precariousness of the situation: "It's a very positive development, but we'll see whether or not it amounts to anything. We've had periods of time where we had, we thought, pretty much getting close to making a deal, and didn't work out."

Deal Closer, But Not Done

This context is critical for UK business energy buyers: a pause in military action is not de-escalation unless physical flows, shipping access, insurance premiums, LNG availability, wholesale pricing and supplier price books also improve—and none of these conditions have materially changed.

The fact that Gulf leaders felt compelled to intervene directly suggests they assessed the risk of imminent military action as credible and immediate. Second, Trump's "clock is ticking" warning on 17–18 May, delivered via Truth Social after a phone call with Israeli Prime Minister Benjamin Netanyahu, has introduced a new time pressure element to the diplomatic equation. The warning—"they better get moving, FAST, or there won't be anything left of them. 1.3 Direct Commercial Conclusion

For UK business energy customers, the message from this reporting period is unambiguous: waiting is not neutral. The postponement of a planned U.S. strike has created a moment of temporary market softness, but it has not reduced the structural risks that have driven wholesale gas prices up 19.84% over the past 30 days and UK power prices up 5.2% over the same period. The World Bank's baseline already projects energy prices surging 24% in 2026 even if the major part of the disruption ends in May, and the EIA's revised assumptions suggest that disruption may last longer than the World Bank baseline assumes. With European gas storage at just 36% full—19 percentage points below the 5-year norm—and the winter refill season approaching, the market's sensitivity to any further supply shock is acute. Supplier price books in the UK typically lag wholesale market movements by days to weeks, meaning that current available fixed pricing may not yet fully reflect the recent upward pressure. For businesses within 12 months of contract end date, this creates a short procurement window during which testing available supplier pricing could secure terms before the next round of repricing lands. The commercially rational position, where current forward pricing and the customer's own risk profile support it, is to review immediately and consider fixing now for 36+ months.

What Changed Since the Last Report

3.1 Military and Political Developments

The military-diplomatic landscape shifted dramatically during the 18–19 May reporting period. The most consequential development was President Trump's disclosure on 18 May that he had planned and then cancelled a "very major attack" on Iran scheduled for Tuesday 19 May. The planned strike, which Trump said he had not previously disclosed, was called off following direct requests from the Emir of Qatar Sheikh Tamim bin Hamad Al Thani, Saudi Crown Prince Mohammed bin Salman Al Saud, and UAE President Mohamed bin Zayed Al Nahyan. These leaders asked Trump to "hold off" for two to three days to allow negotiations to proceed, expressing confidence that a deal would emerge that would include "NO NUCLEAR WEAPONS FOR IRAN". Trump's social media post explicitly stated that he had ordered the U.S. military "to be prepared to go forward with a full, large scale assault of Iran, on a moment's notice, in the event that an acceptable Deal is not reached".

This development followed Trump's "clock is ticking" warning issued on 17 May via Truth Social, which came after a phone call with Israeli Prime Minister Benjamin Netanyahu. The warning—"For Iran, the Clock is Ticking, and they better get moving, FAST, or there won't be anything left of them. TIME IS OF THE ESSENCE!"—represented Trump's most explicit deadline threat since the April 8 ceasefire. According to Axios, Trump was expected to meet with his senior national security team on 19 May in the White House Situation Room to "discuss options for military actions against Iran," citing two American officials. The convergence of these signals—a public deadline warning, a planned strike, and Gulf-state intervention to prevent it—creates a compressed diplomatic timeline where the window for negotiation is measured in days rather than weeks.

Gulf-State Intervention

The Gulf-state intervention is double-edged. On one side, it has prevented immediate military escalation and created a narrow diplomatic window. On the other, it has prolonged uncertainty—the market is now in "limbo" where a deal might emerge in days or military action might resume just as suddenly. For UK business energy buyers, this limbo is not a reason to delay procurement decisions.

On the ground in Lebanon, Israeli military operations have continued unabated and outside the U.S.-Iran ceasefire framework. Netanyahu has consistently maintained that the ceasefire does not apply to Lebanon, and Israeli airstrikes have continued on a daily basis with significant fatalities. During the week of 11–17 May, Israeli strikes killed 23 people on 11 May, 13 on 12 May, and 22 (including 8 children) on 13 May. Hezbollah has responded with increasingly sophisticated drone attacks, including its largest coordinated drone attack since the war began on 12 May, using multiple waves of fiber-optic-guided drones that have demonstrated an ability to evade Israeli jamming technology. The Lebanon front thus remains an independent escalation vector that could trigger wider conflict regardless of the status of U.S.-Iran negotiations.

3.2 Shipping and Commodity Market Changes

The physical situation in the Strait of Hormuz has seen only marginal change. The waterway remains effectively closed to normal commercial traffic, with the only meaningful development being the commencement of selective passage for Chinese-linked vessels under Iranian management protocols. Iranian state media reported that more than 30 ships had been permitted to pass since 13 May, with MarineTraffic data confirming at least four China-linked vessels transiting in a 24-hour period via a designated Iranian safe-shipping corridor. A Chinese supertanker carrying approximately 2 million barrels of Iraqi crude sailed through on 13 May after being stranded for over two months. However, this selective access does not constitute a general reopening. The 1,550+ vessels and 22,500 mariners stranded in and around the strait remain in place, and traffic continues to run at approximately 5% of pre-war average.

The drone attack on the UAE's Barakah nuclear power plant on 17 May introduced a new and deeply concerning element to the conflict. Three drones entered UAE airspace from the western border; two were intercepted but one struck an electrical generator outside the inner perimeter of the Barakah plant, causing a fire. The IAEA confirmed that radiation levels remained normal and no injuries were reported, but Director General Rafael Mariano Grossi expressed "grave concern" and reiterated his call for "maximum military restraint near any NPP". The UAE Foreign Ministry described the incident as an "unprovoked terrorist attack" and stated that "the targeting of peaceful nuclear energy facilities is a flagrant violation of international law". Saudi Arabia separately reported intercepting three drones that entered its territory from Iraqi airspace on the same day. These developments suggest that infrastructure targeting is expanding beyond oil and gas facilities into nuclear and power infrastructure, significantly broadening the potential escalation landscape.

3.3 World Bank and EIA Institutional Updates

The EIA's revised Short-Term Energy Outlook published on 12 May represents the most significant institutional reassessment of the disruption timeline. The agency now assumes the Strait of Hormuz will be effectively closed through the end of May, pushing back its earlier assumption that the closure would last through April. Production shut-ins are now estimated at 10.5 million bpd in April, rising to a peak of 10.8 million bpd in May as Middle Eastern storage tanks reach maximum capacity. This compares to the EIA's earlier forecast that losses would peak at 9.1 million bpd in April. The revised assumptions imply global oil inventories will fall by 2.6 million bpd this year—up dramatically from the earlier estimate of a 300,000 bpd draw. Brent crude is forecast to average $106 per barrel in May and June, before declining to around $89 in Q4 as production gradually recovers. Critically, the EIA noted that if Hormuz remains shut through June, oil prices would be approximately $20 per barrel higher over the near term than current forecasts.

World Bank Warning

The World Bank baseline projects energy prices surging 24% in 2026, explicitly conditional on "the most acute phase of supply disruptions ending within the second quarter of 2026." Current Brent pricing at ~$110/bbl is already trading above this baseline and closer to the World Bank's upside scenario of $115/bbl.

This EIA framing makes the World Bank's baseline assumption—published on 28 April and projecting energy prices to rise 24% in 2026 with the "most acute disruptions" ending in May—increasingly appear optimistic. The World Bank's baseline projected Brent to average $86 per barrel in 2026, with an upside scenario of $115 per barrel if critical facilities suffer more damage and export volumes recover slowly. Current Brent pricing at approximately $110 per barrel is already trading above the World Bank's annual average baseline and closer to its stress scenario, suggesting the market is pricing a more prolonged disruption than the World Bank's central case assumes. The IEA has also revised its outlook, now projecting world oil supply to fall by 3.9 million bpd in 2026 (up from a previous forecast of 1.5 million bpd) assuming Hormuz flows gradually resume from June.

EIA Disruption Warning

The EIA's revised assumptions, published two weeks after the World Bank's outlook, make the baseline look increasingly optimistic. Where the World Bank assumed "the most acute disruptions end in May," the EIA now assumes Hormuz "effectively closed through late May" with production shut-ins rising to 10.8 million bpd.

6.1 What the Ceasefire Says and What It Excludes

The ceasefire agreement reached on 8 April 2026 between the U.S., Iran and Israel was explicitly designed to facilitate negotiations toward a permanent resolution of the conflict. However, the ceasefire has always been partial and conditional, and critical ambiguities have persisted since its inception. The most important limitation is that the ceasefire does not include Lebanon. Israeli Prime Minister Benjamin Netanyahu stated unequivocally on 8 April that "there is not a ceasefire in Lebanon" and that strikes on Lebanon would resume. This position has been maintained consistently, with Netanyahu asserting that the ceasefire agreed between Iran and the U.S.-Israel coalition does not apply to Israeli operations against Hezbollah. The Pakistani mediator, Prime Minister Shehbaz Sharif, had indicated that Lebanon was included, but Netanyahu's contradiction of this claim has been borne out by events on the ground.

The ceasefire's operational terms have also been subject to continuous strain. While the agreement called for a halt to direct U.S.-Iran military hostilities, it did not address the U.S. naval blockade of Iranian ports that remains in effect, nor did it resolve the status of the Strait of Hormuz, which Iran effectively closed on 2 March. The blockade, which U.S. Central Command has enforced by redirecting commercial vessels (85 vessels redirected through 18 May per CENTCOM's own reporting), has been a major sticking point in negotiations. Iran has consistently demanded the blockade's removal as a precondition for progress, while the U.S. has maintained that nuclear concessions must come first. This fundamental sequencing disagreement has meant that even during periods of nominal ceasefire, the physical conditions of economic warfare—blockade, sanctions, and Hormuz closure—have remained in place.

6.2 Ground Reality: What Actually Happened

The ground reality during the ceasefire period has been one of persistent low-intensity conflict punctuated by significant escalations. Since the April 8 ceasefire, the following major incidents have occurred: Iranian and proxy drone and missile attacks against Gulf states including the UAE, Saudi Arabia, Kuwait, Qatar and Bahrain; Israeli daily airstrikes in Lebanon killing hundreds of civilians; the UAE's disclosure of secret retaliatory strikes against Iranian targets including the Lavan Island refinery; Saudi Arabia's secret strikes against Iran and Iranian-backed militias; the dramatic escalation on 4–5 May when Iranian forces attacked multiple vessels in Hormuz, the U.S. destroyed six Iranian small boats, and the UAE intercepted Iranian missiles and drones; and the 17 May drone attack on the Barakah nuclear power plant.

Trump's postponement of the planned 19 May strike represents the second time the U.S. has pulled back from the brink of renewed major hostilities during the ceasefire period. The first was on 6 May, when Trump announced a temporary halt to the "Project Freedom" Hormuz reopening operation, citing "great progress" in negotiations. NBC News reported that this earlier pause was forced by Saudi Arabia's refusal to allow U.S. military aircraft to use Prince Sultan Air Base or fly through Saudi airspace, suggesting that Gulf-state pressure has been a consistent factor in preventing escalation. The pattern that emerges is not of a durable ceasefire but of a tense standoff where military options are continuously prepared and then held in abeyance at Gulf-state request, while diplomatic channels produce proposals that have not yet bridged the fundamental gaps between U.S. and Iranian positions.

7.2 Bab el-Mandeb / Red Sea

The risk of a dual choke point crisis—simultaneous disruption at both Hormuz and Bab el-Mandeb—remains acute. Houthi forces resumed attacks on Red Sea shipping on 28 February 2026, the same day as the U.S.-Israeli strikes on Iran, reversing the limited progress made after the October 2025 Gaza ceasefire. The U.S. Maritime Advisory 2026-006, issued on 26 March, warns that "the Houthis continue to pose a threat to U.S. assets, including commercial vessels" and notes that the group has demonstrated the capability to conduct UAV, USV, ballistic missile, and small boat attacks. Suez Canal transits, which were recovering toward 120 vessel passages per month, have dropped sharply again, with most major carriers rerouting via the Cape of Good Hope. The Times reported on 16 March that the Houthis are awaiting an Iranian signal to resume attacks if U.S. military actions weaken Iran's control of Hormuz, and that approximately 30 tankers near the Saudi port of Yanbu are currently within Houthi strike range. A senior Houthi politburo member has stated that the group is considering a naval blockade targeting vessels belonging to "aggressor countries".

The commercial implications of dual choke point disruption are severe. The Red Sea route to Europe was already operating at 49% below pre-crisis capacity before the Hormuz closure, and the combination of both corridors being blocked has created what DHL describes as the most disruptive logistics environment since the pandemic. For UK energy markets, the Bab el-Mandeb risk matters because Qatari LNG cargoes destined for Europe that might otherwise route via Suez now face a much longer journey around the Cape of Good Hope, adding 10-14 days to transit times and significantly increasing freight costs.

Market Interpretation

9.1 What the Market Is Pricing Now

Current market pricing across Brent, TTF, NBP and UK power suggests traders are pricing a medium-duration disruption scenario that extends beyond the World Bank's May-easing baseline but falls short of a full-year crisis. The EIA's revised forecast of $106/bbl for May-June, with an explicit warning that prices would be $20/bbl higher if Hormuz remains shut through June, provides a near-term anchor that current pricing is broadly consistent with.

In UK gas markets, the NBP at 121.41 p/therm (down 1.54% on the day but up 19.84% over 30 days) reflects a market that has absorbed significant LNG supply risk premium and is now sensitive to any further deterioration in global supply availability. The World Bank's baseline projection of energy prices rising 24% in 2026 is explicitly conditional on "the most acute phase of supply disruptions related to the conflict in the Middle East end[ing] within the second quarter of 2026" and "shipping through the Strait of Hormuz gradually return[ing] to pre-war levels by late 2026". Current Brent pricing at $110/bbl is already 28% above the World Bank's annual average baseline, suggesting either that the market is pricing a more severe disruption than the baseline assumes, or that the baseline will need significant upward revision if current conditions persist into June.

The EIA's revised assumptions, published two weeks after the World Bank's outlook, make the baseline look increasingly optimistic. Where the World Bank assumed "the most acute disruptions end in May," the EIA now assumes Hormuz "effectively closed through late May" with production shut-ins rising to 10.8 million bpd—nearly 2 million bpd higher than the EIA's earlier forecast. The IEA has similarly revised its outlook, now projecting world oil supply to fall by 3.9 million bpd in 2026 (up from 1.5 million bpd previously). These sequential upward revisions by major institutions suggest that the consensus view of disruption severity is still catching up to physical reality.

For UK business energy buyers, the key insight is that the paused strike does not change the physical supply picture. Hormuz remains closed to most commercial traffic. Qatari LNG remains under force majeure. European gas storage remains well below normal. War risk premiums remain at extreme levels. The only thing that has changed is the near-term probability of additional military escalation, and even that change is temporary and conditional. If talks fail, the strike option returns "on a moment's notice".

Impact on UK Business Energy Prices

11.1 The Transmission Mechanism

UK business energy prices are determined through a multi-layered transmission mechanism that begins with global oil and gas markets and ends with supplier price books. The key linkages are as follows. First, UK wholesale gas (NBP) tracks European gas (TTF) because the UK is part of the integrated North-West European gas market, with physical interconnections to Belgium and the Netherlands. Second, European gas tracks global LNG prices because Europe now depends on LNG for approximately 40-45% of its gas supply following the reduction in Russian pipeline flows. Third, global LNG prices are directly affected by Middle East supply disruptions because Qatar supplies approximately 20% of global LNG and roughly 12-14% of European LNG previously transited Hormuz. Fourth, UK electricity prices track gas prices because gas-fired power plants provide 30-40% of UK generation and set the marginal price for much of the day. Fifth, supplier price books reflect wholesale costs plus risk premiums, operating costs, and margin, with typical lag times of days to weeks.

The Hormuz crisis has disrupted this supply chain at multiple points simultaneously. The effective closure of Hormuz has removed Qatar's LNG exports from the market (force majeure declared on 4 March), forcing European buyers to compete more intensely for alternative LNG cargoes from the U.S., Norway, and Algeria. This competition drives up TTF, which drives up NBP, which drives up UK power prices. The war risk premium on shipping—now at 2-3% of hull value, up from 0.1% pre-crisis—adds directly to the delivered cost of any LNG cargoes that do reach European terminals. The LNG freight rate surge—up 600% to $300,000/day in the Atlantic Basin—further increases the cost of delivered gas. These costs flow through to supplier hedging costs and ultimately to business contract pricing.

11.2 World Bank Baseline: Why 24% Matters

The World Bank's projection of a 24% surge in energy prices in 2026 is commercially significant for UK business buyers even before considering any worst-case escalation. This baseline projection assumes "the most acute disruptions end in May"—an assumption that the EIA's revised outlook now challenges. If the World Bank's baseline is already for a 24% price increase in a best-case scenario where major disruption ends in May, then any prolongation of the crisis into June or beyond implies materially higher prices than even this elevated baseline. The World Bank's upside scenario—where critical facilities suffer more damage and export volumes recover slowly—projects Brent averaging $115/bbl in 2026, which would translate into even larger increases for UK gas and power.

For a UK business currently paying 25.82 p/kWh for electricity and 8.67 p/kWh for gas, a 24% increase would raise those costs to approximately 32.0 p/kWh and 10.8 p/kWh respectively. For a medium-sized business using 50,000 kWh of electricity and 100,000 kWh of gas annually, that represents an additional ~£5,300 per year in energy costs. The World Bank's warning that "commodity prices could rise even higher if hostilities escalate or supply disruptions from the war last longer than projected" is not an abstract risk—it is a direct threat to business operating costs.
