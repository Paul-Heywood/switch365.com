B. Market Further Out - Futures Market Explained

📈
Is the Market Cheaper Further Out?

Forward pricing not fully verified from public sources. Use live supplier or broker pricing before making a customer recommendation.

💡

What This Means

The futures market is the market price for energy delivered in a future period. When the near-term market is expensive but the further-out market is cheaper, it usually means today's price contains more immediate risk.

For a customer whose contract starts 12 months from now, the relevant question is what the market is offering for their delivery period. If that future period is cheaper, the customer may be able to contract into that lower forward market now.

⚠️

Why Waiting Can Be Expensive

Customers often assume that if the 12-month or 18-month market is cheaper today, they can wait and pick it up later. That is not how the market works.

If the customer waits, the delivery period moves closer, current risk gets built into the price, and supplier books can change. The cheaper forward market can become the expensive current market.

Commercial Message for Customers

Cheaper further out does not mean cheaper later. The future market is only useful if you act while it is still future pricing.

Waiting means the customer stays exposed while today's future market becomes tomorrow's current market.

This is not automatic advice to fix. It is a reason to review now and test live supplier pricing.

Supplier Price-Book Lag and Procurement Window

Why wholesale moves may not yet show in supplier quotes

How Price-Book Lag Works

Supplier price books are typically updated on a schedule - daily, weekly, or when specific thresholds are breached. When wholesale markets move rapidly due to geopolitical events, there can be a gap between the wholesale price movement and the supplier's published price book.

This gap creates a potential procurement window: a period where a customer can secure pricing before the supplier's book catches up with wholesale reality.

The window is typically hours to days, not weeks. Once the supplier updates, the opportunity closes.

Current Assessment

Supplier book lag is not fully verified from public sources, but it is a normal commercial risk when wholesale markets move quickly.
