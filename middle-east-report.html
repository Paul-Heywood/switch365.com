NESO and Marginal Pricing Explained

NESO is the National Energy System Operator. It watches the electricity system in real time and makes sure there is enough power available to meet demand. A margin notice is not a blackout warning, but it does mean the system is tighter and NESO wants more available power. Under marginal pricing, the market price can be influenced by the last and often more expensive unit of generation needed to meet demand. During heatwaves, cooling demand can rise while evening solar output falls, so the system may need more flexible generation. That can support higher UK power prices and balancing costs.

Forward Market and Backwardation Explained

Why the shape of the forward curve matters for when you buy — not just what you pay today.

What Is The Forward Market?

The forward market shows what gas or power is currently priced at for future delivery periods. Winter 2026 gas is 123.30p/th. Summer 2028 gas is 65.00p/th. A customer starting a new contract in 2028 may not be buying the same market as a customer renewing in 2026.

What Is Backwardation?

Backwardation means the near-term market is more expensive than the future market. If Winter 2026 gas is 123.30p and Summer 2028 is 65.00p, the customer can see that the future delivery period is 47% cheaper than the near-term market. That does not mean wait. It means check whether that cheaper future period can be secured now.

The cheaper future price exists because it is still a future delivery period. Waiting until that period becomes the live market may remove the advantage. Backwardation is not a reason to wait. It is a reason to check whether the customer can act. The forward market turns timing into opportunity. Customers renewing now may be buying the expensive front of the market. Customers with later CEDs may be able to access a cheaper future market now.

Why The Futures Market Matters

The futures market shows the price for energy delivered in future periods — not just today.

A September 2028 customer is not only interested in today's price. They are interested in what the market is currently offering for September 2028 onwards. If 2028, 2029 or 2030 delivery periods are cheaper than the current market, the customer may be able to use that future pricing now, depending on supplier availability and contract rules.

The risk with waiting is that those future periods move closer every day. When they become the near-term market, they can pick up current risk, supplier caution, non-commodity changes, weather pressure or geopolitical premium.

Cheaper further out does not mean cheaper later. The future market is only useful while the customer can still buy that future delivery period. Waiting lets the market make the decision for the customer. This is not automatic advice to fix. It is a reason to review live prices now.

Outside 12 months usually means monitor. Outside 12 months with high usage means check the forward curve.

Normally, customers outside the 12-month window may be placed into monitor mode. But higher-usage customers should not automatically be parked. If their CED sits in 2028 or beyond, the forward curve may give them access to cheaper delivery periods now.

TTF / NBP Explained

Why the customer should not confuse Brent with their gas quote.

TTF is the main European wholesale gas benchmark. NBP is the main UK wholesale gas benchmark. They are not the same market, but they are closely linked because Europe and the UK compete for LNG cargoes and are connected through wider European gas flows.

Oil Benchmark

Brent Is Oil, Not Gas

Brent crude is the international oil benchmark. It is not the customer's gas quote. Oil can move on different factors than gas. Do not assume gas follows oil tick-for-tick.

European Benchmark

TTF Is European Gas

The Dutch Title Transfer Facility is Europe's main gas trading hub. TTF prices influence European gas contracts and can feed through into UK pricing via LNG competition.

UK Benchmark

NBP Is UK Gas

The National Balancing Point is the UK virtual trading point for gas. NBP prices are what UK business gas suppliers reference when building their price books.

The Chain

LNG → TTF → NBP → Your Bill

LNG cargo availability affects TTF. TTF can influence NBP. NBP affects UK business gas pricing. Gas can influence UK power pricing. Forward NBP matters for future-start contracts.

Supplier Price-Book Lag

Wholesale markets can move before supplier books. Supplier books may reprice after gas and power move. Quote validity can shorten. Fixed offers can be withdrawn. Friday's softer market may already be gone. Today's gas move can reduce Friday's opportunity.

For customers inside 90 days, this is not only a market-risk issue. It is also an out-of-contract risk. For customers with later CEDs, this is not only a risk issue. It may also be a forward-market opportunity. Once a supplier withdraws a rate, the customer cannot go back and buy Friday's price.

Supplier price-book lag can work both ways. It can create a short opportunity after market relief, but it can also close quickly if the headline fails. Wholesale markets move first. Supplier books often follow. Waiting for certainty can mean missing the price movement. These prices could be lost as fast as they were gained.
Customer-facing: "After this weekend's escalation, wholesale markets may have moved before supplier books have fully updated. That can create a short window where some current rates are still live, but once suppliers withdraw and reprice, those rates may no longer be available."
Internal: Do not sell panic. Sell timing. Friday's market may already be disappearing. Supplier books may not have fully caught up yet, but after the weekend escalation, we should assume rates can be pulled at short notice.

Market Interpretation

What Oil Is Pricing

Brent at $78.35 is pricing active Hormuz disruption risk, US-Iran military escalation, and the threat to roughly one-fifth of global oil flows. Oil is also pricing the risk of a wider regional conflict.

What Gas Is Pricing

UK gas W-26 offered at 123.30p/th is pricing LNG disruption risk, European storage refill pressure, heat-related demand, Norway flow uncertainty, and the TTF link. Gas is not just following oil — it has its own risk drivers. S-27 at 87.00p shows backwardation is live.

What TTF Is Pricing

TTF at €49.81/MWh is pricing LNG cargo competition between Europe and Asia, European storage levels, Qatar supply disruption, and the threat to Gulf LNG flows through Hormuz.

What NBP Is Pricing

NBP is pricing UK-specific factors: TTF influence, LNG send-out levels, Norwegian flows, UK storage (only 21.23% full), heat-driven power demand, and the broader risk premium from Hormuz.

What UK Power Is Pricing

UK power is pricing gas-fired generation costs, NESO system margin tightness, heat-driven demand, marginal pricing effects, carbon costs, and nuclear outage levels.

What The Forward Curve Is Pricing

Backwardation suggests markets expect some normalisation in 2028–2029. But the front-end premium is real and significant. The curve is pricing near-term risk higher than long-term expectations.

The market has not normalised. The customer decision is not only about today's headline. It is about where the customer's CED sits on the curve, whether supplier books have caught up, and how quickly the current window can close.

Impact On UK Business Energy Prices

UK gas reacts to NBP, TTF, LNG and storage. LNG tanker disruption can affect UK gas even if the UK does not buy that exact cargo — because the UK competes in the same global LNG pool. A Hormuz closure claim affects shipping confidence even before full closure is proven. A commercial vessel attack matters because it influences insurer and shipowner decisions.

Europe and Asia cargo competition matters because Asian buyers are paying historic premiums to secure limited cargoes. TTF matters to NBP because they are linked through LNG competition and European gas flows. Gas matters to UK power because gas-fired generation is the marginal price-setter for much of the time.

Heat and NESO margin notices matter because they show system tightness. Under marginal pricing, the last unit of generation needed to meet demand sets the price — and that unit is often gas-fired and expensive. Brent is not the customer's gas quote. Supplier books can reprice quickly after wholesale moves.

Forward-Market Opportunity

If LNG cargoes are delayed, diverted or repriced, that can feed into TTF, NBP and UK power very quickly. If the customer can currently contract into a cheaper future market, waiting does not protect that price. If supplier books have not fully caught up, the customer may have a short window, but that window can close without notice. Because of marginal pricing, tight UK power conditions can pull more expensive generation into the stack and support higher electricity prices.
