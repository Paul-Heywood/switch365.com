⇄Forward Market and Backwardation Explained

What Is the Forward Market?

The forward market shows what gas or power is currently priced at for future delivery periods. That means a customer starting a new contract in 2028 may not be buying the same market as a customer renewing in 2026. Verified broker pricing shows the near-term market is dramatically more expensive than future delivery: W-26 gas at 119.25p/th versus S-28 at 65.00p/th and S-29 at 58.00p/th. In power, W-26 at £105.00/MWh versus S-28 at £61.00/MWh. That does not mean wait. It means check whether that cheaper future period can be secured now.

Backwardation — Verified Today
Near-term > Future delivery

Gas: W-26 at 119.25p vs S-28 at 65.00p = 54p discount (45% cheaper). S-29 at 58.00p = 61p discount (51% cheaper).

Power: W-26 at £105.00 vs S-28 at £61.00 = £44 discount (42% cheaper).

Backwardation means the near-term market is more expensive than the future market. Backwardation does not mean the customer should wait. It means the cheaper future delivery period may be available to secure now.

Why the Curve Is Backwardated

The front of the curve (W-26) is pricing immediate Hormuz risk, LNG disruption, storage anxiety and summer heat. The back of the curve (S-28, S-29, S-30) is pricing an expectation that these conditions ease over time. But that expectation is not guaranteed. If Hormuz disruption persists, the risk premium can move down the curve, eroding the backwardation advantage.

Why CED Matters

A 2028 CED customer is not buying W-26 at 119.25p. They may be buying a blend of S-28 (65.00p), W-28 (73.00p), S-29 (58.00p), W-29 (68.76p), S-30 (58.55p). If those future periods are cheaper than today's front-end market, that is an opportunity — not a reason to wait.

⇆TTF / NBP Explained

TTF — Title Transfer Facility

TTF is the main European wholesale gas benchmark. It is a virtual trading point in the Netherlands. The volumes traded represent more than 14 times the amount of gas used by the Netherlands for domestic purposes. TTF serves as the regional benchmark for continental European gas pricing. Currently at 48.93 EUR/MWh. [^44^]

NBP — National Balancing Point

NBP is the main UK wholesale gas benchmark. It is a virtual trading point operated by National Grid. UK NBP Natural Gas Futures trade on ICE in pence per therm. NBP reflects UK gas supply-demand, North Sea production, LNG imports, pipeline flows and storage. Currently W-26 at 119.25p/th, S-27 at 85.00p/th.

How They Connect — And Why Brent Is Not the Gas Quote

Brent is oil, not the customer's gas quote. TTF is the European gas benchmark. NBP is the UK gas benchmark. They are not the same market, but they are closely linked because Europe and the UK compete for LNG cargoes and are connected through wider European gas flows.

LNG cargo availability affects TTF. When Hormuz disruption reduces LNG supply, TTF rises because Europe must bid more for remaining cargoes. TTF can influence NBP. When TTF rises, NBP often follows because the UK competes for the same LNG and pipeline gas. NBP affects UK business gas pricing. Suppliers build quotes from NBP forward curves plus their own costs and margin. Gas can influence UK power pricing because gas-fired power stations set the marginal price in many periods.

Sales line: “Brent is not the customer's gas quote. LNG moves TTF, TTF can influence NBP, and NBP can move UK business gas prices.”

⏱Supplier Price-Book Lag

How Lag Works

Wholesale markets can move before supplier books. Suppliers typically update price books on a schedule — daily, twice daily, or when volatility triggers a refresh. This means there is often a gap between the live wholesale price and the price a customer is quoted.

Why It Matters Now

When the market moves up, supplier books can reprice higher with little warning. Quote validity periods can shorten from 48 hours to 24 hours, or from 24 hours to “valid until close of business.” Fixed offers can be withdrawn entirely if the market moves beyond the supplier's risk tolerance.

The Two-Way Risk

Supplier price-book lag can work both ways. It can create a short opportunity after market relief, but it can also close quickly if the headline fails. Today's S-28 at 65p could be gone tomorrow if risk premium moves down the curve. Waiting for certainty can mean missing the price movement.

£Impact on UK Business Energy Prices

How Middle East Risk Reaches the UK Business Customer

LNG disruption → TTF → NBP → UK business gas → UK business power. It is not immediate, and it is not one-for-one, but the chain exists. When Hormuz disruption reduces LNG supply, Europe must bid more for remaining cargoes. That lifts TTF. TTF and NBP are linked through LNG competition and pipeline flows. When NBP rises, UK business gas suppliers reprice. When gas rises, UK power follows because gas-fired stations set the marginal price.

If LNG cargoes are delayed, diverted or repriced, that can feed into TTF, NBP and UK power very quickly. It does not require the UK to buy that exact cargo. It requires the global LNG market to tighten, which raises the clearing price for all buyers.

Why Brent is not the customer's gas quote: Brent is oil. The customer's gas quote comes from NBP (for gas) and NBP-plus-generation-mix (for power). Oil and gas can move together, but they are not the same market. A customer watching Brent to guess their gas renewal price is looking at the wrong benchmark.
