What This Means

The futures market is the market price for energy delivered in a future period. When the near-term market is expensive but the further-out market is cheaper, it usually means today's price contains more immediate risk — supply pressure, shipping uncertainty, geopolitical premium.

For a customer whose contract starts 12 months from now, the relevant question is not only what energy costs today. It is what the market is offering for their delivery period. If that future period is cheaper, the customer may be able to contract into that lower forward market now.

The key point: waiting does not mean today's cheaper future price becomes tomorrow's available price. As time passes, that future period becomes the near-term market — and can pick up the same risk premium.

Why Waiting Can Be Expensive

Customers often assume that if the 12-month or 18-month market is cheaper today, they can wait and pick it up later. That is not how the market works.

The future price is available because the customer is buying that future delivery period now. If the customer waits, the delivery period moves closer, current risk gets built into the price, and supplier books can change.

In simple terms, the cheaper forward market can become the expensive current market.

Cheaper further out does not mean cheaper later.

Commercial Message for Customers: If your contract end date is far enough out, you may be able to contract into a market that is cheaper than the near-term market. That can support fixing longer where the price, contract date, usage profile and risk position make sense. Do not let the market make the decision for you by holding off. Education

Futures Market Explained in Plain English

What is the futures market?

The futures market is where traders and suppliers buy and sell contracts for energy to be delivered at a future date. A "Summer 2027" gas contract, for example, is an agreement to buy gas for delivery during summer 2027 — at a price agreed today.

Why is further-out energy sometimes cheaper?

When there is immediate risk in the market — such as a blocked shipping strait, supply disruption, or geopolitical tension — the near-term price carries a "risk premium." The market assumes that by the time Summer 2027 arrives, some of today's problems may have eased. So it prices that future period lower than the current period.

Why doesn't waiting guarantee that cheaper price?

Because the clock keeps ticking. Summer 2027 gets closer every day. If the Strait of Hormuz is still disrupted in six months, or if supplier caution remains high, that "future" period will start to carry the same risk premium that today's market carries. The customer who waits gives up the chance to lock in the cheaper forward price and bets that the market will be softer later.

Waiting is not neutral. It is a decision to remain exposed while wholesale markets, supplier books and geopolitical risks move.

What should customers do?

If your contract end date aligns with a cheaper forward period (e.g., Summer 2027 or Calendar 2028), review procurement now. Test live supplier pricing. Compare a 12-month fix versus a 24-month or 36-month fix. If the longer fix is cheaper and suits your risk profile, that can be commercially sensible. This is not automatic advice to fix — it is a reason to review now.

Temporary market softness should be treated as a potential procurement window, not proof the crisis is over.

What This Means for SMEs

Both institutions agree: energy prices will be materially higher in 2026 than in 2025, even if the disruption eases. The World Bank says +24% on average. The EIA's higher Brent forecast ($96 vs $86) suggests they see the disruption lasting slightly longer or recovering more slowly.

If the World Bank baseline already points to materially higher energy prices even with disruption easing, then waiting only makes sense if the customer believes the risk is being overstated.

But the EIA is now suggesting the disruption may last longer than the World Bank assumes. That makes the World Bank baseline look more optimistic, not less risky.

For a UK business deciding whether to secure a contract now: both institutions are pointing to higher prices. The disagreement is about how much higher — not whether prices rise.

Supplier Price-Book Lag and Procurement Window

Why wholesale moves before supplier books catch up

How Supplier Pricing Works

UK energy suppliers do not change their retail price books instantly when wholesale markets move. There is a lag — sometimes hours, sometimes days, sometimes weeks — between a wholesale price change and the point at which that change is reflected in the fixed-price quotes that sales teams can offer customers.

When wholesale prices rise quickly, suppliers typically reprice upward fast — sometimes within 24 hours — to protect their margin. When wholesale prices fall, the repricing downward can be slower. This asymmetry is well understood in the industry.

Current Assessment: 22 May 2026

Wholesale gas eased 2–3% on 21 May on Iran diplomatic headlines. TTF down 3.93% on 22 May. This is headline-driven softness, not structural improvement.

Supplier book movement is not fully verified from public sources. However, it is a normal commercial risk when wholesale markets move quickly.

If supplier books have not fully caught up with the wholesale softening, customers may have a short window to secure pricing before supplier repricing lands.

If supplier books have already caught up, the window may have narrowed or closed.

The futures market point: Even if wholesale softens temporarily and supplier books follow, the forward market for Summer 2027 and Calendar 2028 may still offer better value than near-term pricing. Customers whose contract end dates align with those future periods should check whether they can contract into that cheaper forward market now. If they wait, that future period becomes the current period — and may absorb the same risk premium.

Market Interpretation

What the market is pricing now — and what would change it

What the Market Is Pricing Now

The market is pricing a partial, temporary de-escalation — not a resolution. UK gas eased 2–3% and TTF fell 3.93% on 21–22 May because Trump's postponed strike reduced the immediate probability of military escalation. But Brent crude rose 2.03% on the same day, suggesting oil traders see continued supply risk.

The forward curve shape confirms this interpretation: near-term contracts (June 2026, Winter 2026) carry a significant risk premium, while further-out contracts (Summer 2027, Calendar 2028) are priced on the assumption that some of today's disruption has eased by then.

What Would Trigger the Next Leg Up

  • ▶Talks fail and Trump resumes the strike schedule
  • ▶Israel acts unilaterally against Iranian nuclear facilities
  • ▶Hormuz closure extends through June (EIA: +$20/bbl)
  • ▶Ichthys LNG strike goes ahead from 27 May, tightening LNG supply
  • ▶European storage refill falls further behind seasonal norms

What Would Trigger the Next Leg Down

  • ▶Verified, full reopening of Hormuz to all commercial traffic
  • ▶Confirmed US-Iran agreement with implementation timeline
  • ▶War-risk premiums falling materially
  • ▶TTF/NBP sustained decline backed by physical flows, not headlines
  • ▶European gas storage refilling at or above seasonal norms
Analyst view: The market is currently in a "limbo" state — diplomatic progress has reduced the immediate probability of escalation, but physical supply conditions have not materially improved. The forward curve supports this: near-term risk premium remains, further-out pricing is softer. For customers, this limbo is not a reason to wait. It is a reason to review, because limbo can resolve in either direction — and the downside (prices up) is more painful than the upside (prices down) for an unhedged buyer.

  • UK gas storage at 10% — lowest in Europe — means the UK has less buffer to absorb supply shocks.
⚡

Electricity: The Gas Price Link

  • Gas-fired power stations are the marginal generator in most UK settlement periods. When gas prices rise, electricity prices follow — even if wind and solar output is strong.
  • Day-ahead power can fall sharply on high renewable days (as seen 20 May: -22%), but forward power contracts reflect the gas price baseline.
  • Winter 2026 forward power at £97.73/MWh is well above pre-crisis norms, reflecting the gas price risk premium.
🏢

Supplier Pricing Behaviour

  • There is no business energy price cap in the UK. Ofgem's price cap applies only to domestic standard variable tariffs. Bionic
  • Business energy contracts are priced by suppliers based on wholesale costs, network charges, policy costs and margin.
  • In volatile periods, suppliers widen their risk premium and shorten quote validity. Some may withdraw fixed offers entirely.
  • Customers on variable, deemed, rollover or out-of-contract rates are most exposed — they pay spot or near-spot prices.
Key point for SMEs: The repricing window can close quickly. When wholesale markets move, supplier books update — sometimes within hours for electronic pricing, sometimes within days for manual quotes. A customer who delays a decision can find the quote they were holding has expired, and the replacement quote is higher. This is not supplier gamesmanship. It is how wholesale volatility flows through to retail pricing.

Action

Commercial Takeaway — What UK Businesses Should Do

Direct guidance for Switch 365 Ltd customers and prospects

⚠ Waiting Is Not Neutral

Markets remain structurally volatile. Short-term pauses in geopolitical escalation have not normalised wholesale pricing conditions. The forward curve still shows near-term contracts trading at a significant premium to further-out delivery periods — and that premium exists because the market believes today's risk is real.

Waiting is a bet that the World Bank, the EIA, current market pricing, Gulf-state intervention, Trump's warning, the delayed military threat, Hormuz disruption, supplier price-book lag and the unresolved peace proposal are all overstating the risk.

For Customers Within 12 Months of Contract End Date

The commercially sensible step is to review immediately, test available supplier pricing, compare short-term and longer-term pricing, and — where pricing and risk profile support it — consider fixing now and fixing longer (36+ months) before supplier books move further.

For Customers with Contracts Ending Further Out (2027–2028)

If your contract end date aligns with Summer 2027 or Calendar 2028, the forward market may currently offer materially cheaper pricing than near-term contracts. Summer 2027 gas at 86p/th is 25% below Winter 2026 at 115p/th. Calendar 2028 at 72p/th is 38% below.

But this only helps if you act while that forward period is still priced as future delivery. If you wait until Summer 2027 becomes the near-term market, the same risk premium that exists today can get built into that period.

If the customer can currently contract into a cheaper future market, waiting does not protect that price. Waiting simply moves the customer closer to delivery, where today's risk premium can become tomorrow's contract price.

Key Points for Sales Conversations

  • ▶Cheaper further out does not mean cheaper later. The futures market is only useful while you can still buy that future delivery period.
  • ▶Waiting allows the market to make the decision for you. That decision may not be in your favour.
  • ▶Supplier quote validity is shortening. A quote held today may not be available tomorrow.
  • ▶There is no business energy price cap. Ofgem's cap does not protect business customers. Only a fixed contract provides certainty.
  • ▶This is not automatic advice to fix. It is a reason to review now, test pricing, compare options, and make an informed decision while the forward market is still available.
Required final line: Cheaper further out does not mean cheaper later. It means there may be an opportunity now, while that period is still priced as future delivery.
