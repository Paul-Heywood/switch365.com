Trump Pauses Iran Strike.
The Market Is in Limbo.

Gulf states persuaded Trump to hold off a planned attack. A deal looks closer but is not agreed. Hormuz remains restricted. UK business energy buyers should not mistake headline relief for physical de-escalation.

Report Date: 20 May 2026 Window: 19-20 May 2026

Executive Summary

What happened in the last 24 hours and why it matters for UK business energy procurement

Trump paused a planned military attack on Iran scheduled for 20 May after Qatar, Saudi Arabia and the UAE asked him to hold off. He said serious negotiations are underway and a deal may be close. He also warned the US military to remain ready for a full-scale assault at a moment's notice if talks fail. Source: Axios

The pause is conditional. Trump told reporters the delay could be "two or three days" or longer. He has postponed planned attacks at least half a dozen times since the war began. The White House saw Iran's updated peace proposal as insufficient. Source: Axios

Meanwhile, the Strait of Hormuz remains effectively closed to normal commercial traffic. Iran began allowing some Chinese vessels through under Iranian management protocols from 13-14 May, but this is selective passage, not normalisation. Traffic through Hormuz is running at approximately 5% of pre-war levels. Source: China Daily Source: Carra Globe

On the Israel-Lebanon front, the truce was extended for 45 days on 15 May, but Israeli strikes continue. At least 23 people were killed in Israeli attacks on 11 May, and an Israeli strike hit a civil defence centre on 15 May killing six including three paramedics. Hezbollah continues operations against Israeli forces. Lebanon is not covered by the US-Iran ceasefire. Source: Wikipedia/ACLED

Markets reacted with cautious softness. Brent crude is around $110-117 in mid-May, down from the $124 peak in late April but still up over 50% since January. TTF gas is at ~51 EUR/MWh. UK NBP is at ~127 GBp/thm. These levels remain elevated. Source: Trading Economics Source: Trading Economics

Commercial Bottom Line
A paused attack is not de-escalation. It is a temporary delay. Hormuz is still restricted. Supplier price books may not have fully caught up with wholesale movement. For UK businesses within 12 months of contract end, this is still a time to review immediately, not wait for perfect clarity.

Supplier Price-Book Lag

Why wholesale movement and supplier pricing do not move in sync

Wholesale Markets
Hours
Reacts in real-time to headlines, geopolitics, supply data
→
Supplier Price Books
Days to Weeks
Updated periodically based on averaged forward curves

How the lag works: UK energy suppliers do not reprice in real-time. Their price books are typically updated based on averaged forward market curves over days or weeks. When wholesale markets spike (or dip) rapidly, there is a gap between where the market is and where supplier pricing sits.

What this means right now: Wholesale markets have shown some softness on diplomatic headlines (Brent down from $124 peak, TTF easing slightly). However, supplier price books may not have fully reflected this softness yet - and if the diplomatic pause proves temporary, books may reprice upward before customers can secure the dip.

The window: If wholesale has softened but supplier books have not fully caught up, customers may have a short window to secure pricing at levels that could disappear once books reprice. Conversely, if books have already caught up, that window may have narrowed or closed.

Supplier Book Status: Watch Point
Supplier price-book lag is not fully verified from public sources for the current reporting window. This is a normal commercial risk when wholesale markets move quickly. Sales teams should test live supplier pricing to verify what is actually available, not assume books have or have not caught up.

Impact on UK Business Energy Prices

Why global events translate directly into UK business energy bills

UK gas follows global LNG. The UK relies on LNG for a significant portion of gas supply (21% of gross annual supply over 2020-2024). When Qatar LNG went offline (force majeure 4 March), global LNG competition intensified. The UK must now compete with Asia for Atlantic cargoes. Higher JKM ($19.62/MMBtu) means higher European and UK gas prices.

UK electricity follows gas. Gas-fired power generation remains a major component of UK electricity supply. When gas prices rise, electricity prices follow. UK wholesale power at ~£104.58/MWh is up 27% since January. The July-September 2026 Ofgem price cap is forecast to rise to ~£1,841 (from £1,641).

Shipping disruption hits delivered cost. Even if wholesale commodity prices stabilise, the cost of physically delivering LNG cargoes has risen due to longer routes (Cape of Good Hope), higher war-risk insurance, and vessel congestion. These costs flow into supplier pricing.

The repricing window closes quickly. When markets move, supplier pricing can change within hours or days. A customer who delays reviewing their contract may find the price they saw last week is no longer available. Quote validity periods may shorten during volatile periods.

The World Bank 24% figure matters even without worst-case escalation. The World Bank's baseline already forecasts a 24% energy price rise in 2026 - and that baseline assumes the major disruption ends in May. If disruption continues, the upside is greater. Even in the best-case scenario (by World Bank assumptions), energy prices are materially higher than pre-crisis.

Chinese priority access through Hormuz does not help the UK. China's ability to secure vessel passage is good for China's energy security but tightens global competition for alternative barrels and LNG cargoes. Europe and the UK must bid against Asia for limited Atlantic supply.

Why Delay Is Dangerous
If the World Bank baseline (disruption ends in May, 24% energy price rise) is already built into forward curves, and the EIA now assumes disruption lasts longer, then waiting is a bet that both institutions are wrong. It is also a bet that no further escalation occurs, that Hormuz reopens fully, that Qatar LNG resumes, and that European storage refills normally. Each of those is uncertain. For businesses within 12 months of contract end, the commercially sensible step is to review now, not wait for certainty that may not arrive.

Important Distinction
Temporary diplomatic language is not enough. A paused attack, a "deal is close" statement, or Gulf-state mediation reduces immediate military risk but does not constitute de-escalation unless it is followed by verified physical supply normalisation. The market has seen multiple pauses before. Each time, the underlying conflict resumed. Treat headline relief as a potential procurement window, not proof the crisis is over.

Commercial Takeaway

What UK businesses should consider now

Waiting is not neutral. It is a decision to remain exposed.

Volatility is real. UK wholesale gas has risen 25% in a month. UK power is up 27% since January. Brent is 50%+ above January levels. These are not small moves. They translate directly into business energy costs.

The market can move before supplier books fully reprice. When wholesale spikes, supplier pricing updates with a lag. That lag can create a short window to secure pricing - but it can also mean missing a dip if books reprice upward before you act.

Buyers within 12 months of contract end should be reviewing immediately. Do not wait for your renewal date to approach. Supplier pricing, availability, and quote validity can all change before your renewal window opens. Testing pricing now gives you information and options.

Consider fixing now and fixing longer (36+ months) where pricing supports it. If the further-out market is cheaper than near-term pricing, a longer fix may reduce exposure and improve budget certainty. This is particularly relevant for SMEs that value predictability over flexibility.

The World Bank baseline makes delay more dangerous, not less. Even if the major disruption ends in May (World Bank assumption), energy prices are still forecast to rise 24% in 2026. If disruption continues (EIA assumption), the upside is greater. Either way, the floor is elevated.

Hormuz selective opening to Chinese vessels is not normalisation. It does not restore free commercial transit. It does not reduce war-risk premiums. It does not restart Qatar LNG. It does not help European or UK supply.

Gulf-state intervention reduces immediate military risk but prolongs limbo. The market is in a holding pattern. Holding patterns can last weeks or months. During that time, prices can drift up on storage concerns, insurance costs, or competing demand.

A deal looking closer but not agreed is not a reason to wait. If a deal is reached, prices may soften. But if talks fail, prices can spike rapidly. The asymmetry of risk (limited downside from fixing early, significant upside from delay) favours action.
