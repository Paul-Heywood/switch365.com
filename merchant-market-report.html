What Has Changed in the Merchant Services Market

Card Scheme and Processing Fees

PSR Findings

The Payment Systems Regulator's 2024 market review found that Visa and Mastercard increased scheme and processing fees by more than 30% in real terms over the preceding five-year period[823].

Total revenues from scheme and processing fees more than doubled since 2017, with little or no demonstrable link to changes in service quality.

Cross-Border Impact

Following Brexit, card schemes increased interchange fees for UK-EEA card-not-present transactions dramatically[822]:

  • • Debit: 0.2% → 1.15% (475% increase)
  • • Credit: 0.3% → 1.5% (400% increase)

Geopolitical and Supply-Chain Pressure

Critical Distinction: Indirect Cost Pressure vs. Direct Rate Setting

Energy Market Pricing

  • • Direct, transparent price discovery
  • • Immediate geopolitical transmission
  • • Commodity trading platforms
  • • Wholesale to retail price linkage

Merchant Services Pricing

  • • Acquirer-processor negotiations
  • • Administrative scheme fee schedules
  • • Hardware procurement contracts
  • • Bundled service offerings

The Iran-Israel-USA conflict is an additional pressure factor, not the primary driver. It does not operate on merchant services pricing like wholesale gas markets affect energy contracts. The correct framing recognizes that geopolitical disruption may add pressure to the cost base behind payment hardware, freight logistics, provider subsidy models and future pricing discipline[801].

Final Customer Takeaway

Merchant services is not moving like the energy market, but the commercial principle is similar. When provider costs are rising behind the scenes, payment technology is changing rapidly, and providers are still competing aggressively for volume, businesses have more leverage by reviewing early and securing favourable terms.

Waiting may not produce a better deal. It may simply reduce choice, allow legacy costs to continue accumulating, and leave the business making a rushed decision under renewal pressure with diminished negotiating position.

The decision is not "panic sign now." It is "benchmark now, understand the real cost, and secure value while the market is still competitive."
