Executive Summary
The commercial picture in under 60 seconds

Some Chinese-linked vessels have transited, but this is selective passage, not normal commercial shipping. Global oil supply losses exceed 14 million barrels per day. Cumulative losses have now passed 1 billion barrels. [^58^][^80^]

The World Bank baseline forecasts energy prices to rise 24% in 2026, with Brent averaging $86/bbl. This baseline already assumes the most acute disruptions end in May. In a stress scenario, Brent could average $115/bbl. The EIA's April forecast assumed the conflict would not persist past April, yet disruption continues into late May, suggesting the World Bank baseline may be optimistic. [^9^][^10^]

UK wholesale gas holds near 126p/th. Day-ahead power trades around £85-105/MWh. Longer-dated contracts (Summer 2027, Calendar 2028) are trading at a discount to near-term pricing, suggesting the market prices eventual normalisation — but the path there is highly uncertain. UK gas storage is at just 10-36% of capacity, among the lowest in Europe. [^28^][^2^][^49^][^55^]

Bottom line for UK business buyers

Customers within 12 months of renewal should review now, test live pricing and avoid waiting for perfect clarity. Headline relief from diplomatic optimism is not the same as physical supply improvement. If longer-term pricing is cheaper than near-term, this may support fixing longer (36+ months) where the customer's risk profile and supplier availability allow.

Ceasefire Reality Check
What the ceasefire says vs. what is actually happening
!

Core principle

No major escalation is not the same as real de-escalation. A pause in bombing is not proof that the crisis is over. Headline relief from diplomatic language does not equal physical supply improvement.

What the ceasefire says

  • › US-Iran hostilities paused from 8 April
  • › Meant to facilitate negotiations
  • › Israel-Hezbollah truce also in effect
  • › Hormuz passage to be "coordinated"

What is actually happening

  • › Israeli bombing in Lebanon continues
  • › Hezbollah firing projectiles at Israel
  • › Hormuz remains restricted/blockaded
  • › US naval blockade of Iran continues
  • › 2,900+ deaths in Lebanon since war began

Commercial Assessment

Trump delaying the strike after Gulf-state requests reduces immediate military escalation risk, but it also prolongs uncertainty. The market is now in a state of diplomatic limbo: a deal looks possible, but it is not guaranteed, and the military option remains live.

Gulf-state intervention is tactically significant — Saudi Arabia, Qatar and the UAE have substantial leverage with both Washington and Tehran. But their optimism about a deal does not mean the deal will materialise on terms acceptable to all parties.

For UK business energy buyers, the key point is this: the ceasefire has not normalised physical energy flows. Hormuz is still restricted. LNG is still disrupted. Storage is still low. Supplier pricing is still elevated. Diplomatic optimism is welcome, but it is not a reason to delay procurement planning.

Commercial Interpretation for SMEs

Both the World Bank and the EIA are forecasting materially higher energy prices in 2026 than pre-war levels, even under their baseline assumptions. The World Bank's baseline — which assumes the most acute disruptions end in May — still sees a 24% energy price surge. The EIA assumed the conflict would not persist past April, yet here we are in late May with Hormuz still restricted and Trump warning of military action.

This means: the World Bank baseline may already be optimistic. If disruption continues into June and beyond, the stress scenario ($115/bbl Brent) becomes more relevant. Current market pricing (Brent ~$105-112) is already closer to the stress scenario than the baseline.

If the World Bank baseline already points to materially higher energy prices even with disruption easing, then waiting only makes sense if the customer believes the risk is being overstated. For most UK businesses, the prudent assumption is that energy prices in 2026-2027 will be higher, not lower, than today's already-elevated levels.

Supplier Price-Book Lag and Procurement Window
Why wholesale moves faster than supplier pricing — and what that means for buyers

How the lag works

UK energy suppliers set their price books based on wholesale market movements, but there is always a lag. When wholesale prices move sharply — up or down — supplier pricing may take days or weeks to fully adjust. This creates potential procurement windows: periods when wholesale markets have moved but supplier books have not yet caught up.

Wholesale market
Moves in real-time based on global events, weather, supply disruptions. Can gap up or down within hours.
Supplier price book
Updated periodically — daily for some suppliers, weekly for others. May use averaging periods or forward curves.
Customer quote
Based on the supplier's current price book at the time of quote. Valid for a limited period — 7 to 45 days typically.

Current assessment

!

Procurement window may exist — but it can close quickly

Wholesale markets have been volatile with headlines around Trump, Hormuz and the ceasefire. If a short-term headline (e.g., Trump delaying the strike) causes wholesale prices to dip slightly, but supplier books are still priced off earlier, higher wholesale levels, there may be a brief window to secure relatively favourable fixed pricing. This window closes as soon as suppliers update their books.

Conversely, if wholesale prices rise sharply on escalation news and supplier books have not yet caught up, customers may still be able to secure pricing before the next round of supplier repricing lands. Either way, the message is the same: do not wait.

Supplier book lag is not fully verified from public sources — this assessment is based on normal commercial market behaviour observed during previous periods of wholesale volatility.

Impact on UK Business Energy Prices
Why global events flow through to UK business energy bills

The transmission mechanism: from Hormuz to your bill

🌍 → ⛽
1. Hormuz disruption hits LNG
Middle East LNG exports are severely curtailed. Qatar, a major supplier to Europe, cannot ship normally. Europe must compete with Asia for alternative LNG cargoes from the US and elsewhere.
⛽ → 🇬🇧
2. UK gas prices rise
The UK imports ~50% of its gas, mostly via LNG and Norwegian pipeline. When global LNG is tight, NBP prices rise to attract cargoes. UK storage at 10% means we have little buffer.
⛽ → ⚡
3. Electricity follows gas
Gas-fired power plants set the marginal price for ~40% of UK electricity. When gas prices rise, electricity prices rise with them — even when wind and solar output is strong.
⚡ → 🏢
4. Supplier pricing adjusts
Suppliers update their price books to reflect higher wholesale costs. Fixed contract rates rise. Deemed/rollover rates rise even faster. The gap between fixed and out-of-contract widens.

Commercial Takeaway
Guidance for UK businesses and SMEs — what to do now

Waiting is not neutral.
It is a bet that the risk is being overstated.

For customers within 12 months of contract end date:

  • 1. Review immediately. Do not wait for the crisis to resolve. It may not.
  • 2. Test live pricing. Get quotes from multiple suppliers. Compare fixed vs variable options.
  • 3. Consider fixing now. Where pricing and risk profile support it, fix before further repricing.
  • 4. Consider fixing longer. 36+ months may offer relative value if further-out pricing is cheaper.
  • 5. Budget for volatility. Even if you don't fix now, plan for higher prices through 2026-2027.

Why fixing longer (36+ months) may make sense:

  • › The forward curve is backwardated — Summer 2027 and Calendar 2028 are cheaper than near-term
  • › The World Bank baseline already points to higher prices even if disruption eases
  • › Budget certainty is valuable for SMEs managing cash flow
  • › Exit fees may apply — weigh against the cost of future volatility
  • › This depends on your contract end date, usage profile, and supplier availability

Not automatic advice — site-specific recommendation requires individual consumption, contract and renewal data.

What NOT to do

  • ✗ Do not wait for "perfect clarity" — it may never come
  • ✗ Do not assume headline relief equals physical improvement
  • ✗ Do not assume the World Bank baseline is too pessimistic
  • ✗ Do not stay on deemed/rollover rates — they are 40-75% more expensive than fixed
  • ✗ Do not ignore the 12-month renewal window — supplier books can move faster than you think
